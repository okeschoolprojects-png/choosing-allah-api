*The nafs, hawa, and why the limits Allah drew are mercy.*
A lot of us didn’t first meet Islam as mercy. We met it as a list. No alcohol. No pork. No dating. Pray five times. Fast Ramadan. Lower your gaze. A checklist religion, handed over as rules before reasons, commands before the One who gave them.
I understand that picture because I grew up around it. I heard plenty about halal and haram, rulings, arguments, and who was falling into which mistake. What I struggled to explain was the ground underneath all of it. Who is Allah? Why does He have the right to command me? Why should I trust what He asks when my own desire pulls somewhere else? I knew some of the branches long before I understood the root.
That is the question here. Is Islam just a set of rules?
To answer it, you have to go somewhere most arguments about rules never go, into the self those rules are speaking to.
## Who decides what good is?
One sentence usually travels with the complaint about religious rules: I don’t need all this law, I’ll just be a good person.
Good according to whom?
Every generation has called something good that the next generation called cruel. Laws change, public morals move, and practices once defended as normal become a source of shame. The reverse happens too. Something one generation hid because it considered it indecent becomes entertainment for the next. If goodness has no source above society, then goodness becomes whatever the people around you currently agree to praise.
That agreement can move very quickly. Film in the United States was governed by the strict Hays Code from the 1930s. A few decades later, material that would once have ended a career was being sold openly. The people did not become a different species in between. The accepted line moved, then people moved with it.
This does not mean every human judgment is worthless. Allah gave us reason, conscience, and a fitrah that recognizes much of what is beautiful and ugly. Yet all three can be pressured, neglected, trained into silence, or taught to admire what once made the heart recoil. A conscience living inside a culture is still being taught by that culture.
Allah says: *“Have you seen the one who has taken their own desires as their god? Allah left them to stray knowingly, sealed their hearing and hearts, and placed a veil over their sight. Who then can guide them after Allah? Will you not then be mindful?”* (Surah Al-Jathiyah, aya 23)
That is the danger. The problem is not only that people desire. It is that desire starts acting like a judge.
## We already live by rules
Our ordinary days are full of rules we barely notice. We arrive before an exam begins, stop when the traffic light tells us to, line up at the airport, follow a work schedule, and accept that a football match becomes meaningless if every player decides which rules feel right in the moment.
A school needs rules to teach. A country needs laws so the strong cannot do whatever they please with the weak. Even a family needs limits, though every child who has ever been told to put the phone away has, for at least five minutes, felt differently.
We may dislike a particular rule. We may think the person enforcing it is unfair. Very few of us, though, believe that freedom means the complete absence of limits. A life with no limits does not become peaceful. It becomes a contest between whichever appetite, person, or institution is strongest.
So the real question is not whether your life will be shaped by rules. It already is. The real question is who deserves to draw the deepest limits of it.
A government knows part of what is happening in a society. Parents know more about their child, though parents are still human and can get things wrong. Allah knows the public act, the private motive, the harm that appears years later, and the need inside you that you have not yet learned to name.
That is why an Islamic rule cannot be separated from the One who gave it. The same command can feel completely different depending on who is speaking. If a stranger grabs your arm near rough water and tells you not to swim there, you may pull away. If your father grew up beside that shore and knows where the current has taken people before, the hand on your arm means something else. The words are the same. Knowledge of the speaker changes how you hear them.
A lot of us were introduced to the command before we were properly introduced to the Commander. No wonder the command sometimes sounded cold.
## What submission means
Submission is not a word the modern world likes. We are taught early that a choice is good because we made it, and a demand is suspicious because somebody else made it. That assumption sits underneath a lot of conversations about Islam. Why should Allah tell me what to wear, what to consume, who I can sleep with, or what I must do with my time?
The question deserves more than “because it is haram.”
It begins with something this book has already tried to make plain: Allah made us, owns us, knows us, and will receive us when we return. His command does not arrive from a stranger with an interest in controlling us. It comes from the Lord whose favors we spent the previous pages trying and failing to count.
This still does not make every act of obedience easy. Some commands may feel natural to you. Others may press directly against something you want. A person can know that Allah’s command is true while still struggling to carry it. That struggle does not make them a hypocrite, and finding a rule difficult does not mean they secretly hate their Lord. Sometimes obedience becomes precious precisely because another part of us is pulling the other way.
The companions understood this. Anas ibn Malik, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that he was pouring drinks for guests when word spread through Madinah that alcohol had been forbidden. The cups were emptied, and wine ran through the streets. These were people who had enjoyed the drink a moment earlier. Its taste had not changed. They had simply learned that Allah had spoken. (Sahih al-Bukhari)
Allah describes the believing response with startling simplicity: *“The only response of the true believers, when they are called to Allah and His Messenger so he may judge between them, is to say, 'We hear and we obey.' It is they who will truly succeed.”* (Surah An-Nur, aya 51)
That line does not describe people who never felt pull in the opposite direction. It describes people who decided that the pull would not be the judge.
## The self Allah is addressing
Allah did not send His commands to angels. He sent them to human beings with appetites, anger, habits, loneliness, attraction, pride, memory, and the capacity to rise after falling. Your weakness was already known when the limit was revealed.
The Qur’an speaks about the nafs, the self, in different states. These are not different souls. They are conditions the same soul can move through.
There is the nafs that urges a person toward evil. Yusuf, peace be upon him, says: *“I do not seek to free myself from blame, for indeed the soul is ever inclined to evil, except those shown mercy by my Lord. Surely my Lord is All-Forgiving, Most Merciful.”* (Surah Yusuf, aya 53)
There is the reproaching self, the part of you that argues back after the fall. It is the discomfort that will not let the sin settle peacefully, the voice that knows something has gone wrong even while another part of you wants to defend it. Allah swears by this soul in Surah Al-Qiyamah.
Then there is the soul at rest, the one addressed at the end of the road: *“O tranquil soul, return to your Lord, well pleased and well pleasing. Enter among My servants, and enter My Paradise.”* (Surah Al-Fajr, ayas 27–30)
The deen trains the first state, listens to the warning of the second, and asks Allah to bring us toward the third. Prayer interrupts the day before the day can swallow us. Fasting teaches the body that wanting something does not mean it must be given immediately. Zakat takes wealth out of the grip of the self. Lowering the gaze closes a door before desire has filled the whole room.
This is the part I missed when Islam looked like a checklist. The rules were speaking to movements already happening inside me. They were not scattered restrictions. They were training.
## Desire is not identity
The modern answer to desire is often very simple: if you feel it, express it. If you want it strongly, it must reveal something important about who you are. Resisting the desire then starts to look like betraying the self.
Islam gives the human being more room than that. An inner pull may come from fitrah, hunger, loneliness, habit, a wound, social pressure, Shaytan, or an old pattern that has been fed too often. We do not have to turn every feeling into a name and every appetite into an instruction. We can ask where it leads, what it does to the heart, and whether Allah is pleased with the road it opens.
That is not hatred of the self. It is taking the self seriously enough to know it can be trained.
Ibn al-Qayyim, a Hanbali scholar of the eighth Islamic century, wrote extensively about hawa, the pull toward what we want before asking whether it is good. Hawa is part of being human. Trouble begins when it becomes the judge. If every desire writes its own law, the loudest one eventually rules the rest of the person.
You see a smaller version of that every day. The alarm rings and one part of you wants another hour. Anger rises and prepares a sentence the tongue will regret. The phone lights up during a conversation with someone you love, and your hand reaches for it before you have even decided to move. None of those impulses gives a complete account of who you are. They are pulls. You still answer for what you do with them.
Mujahadah is the struggle inside that answer. It is the work of refusing to let the lowest pull become the highest authority. Food, rest, beauty, marriage, companionship, and wealth can all be gifts. Islam teaches us to receive them without kneeling to them.
## Why the limits are mercy
A limit can protect something before we fully understand what is at risk.
The prohibition of alcohol protects the mind, the family, the road, and the dignity of the person who would otherwise wake up carrying what was done while they were no longer fully themselves. The limits around sex protect lineage, trust, children, and hearts that bond even when a culture insists the encounter was casual. The rules around money protect the poor from the appetite of the rich and protect the rich from imagining that ownership made them self-sufficient.
We may not see every wisdom immediately, and we should be careful about inventing reasons Allah and His Messenger never gave. Still, the broad pattern is hard to miss. The commands preserve what our appetites are capable of spending cheaply.
There is mercy in what Allah permits too. Islam does not ask us to live without pleasure. We eat, marry, trade, travel, laugh, sleep, dress well, give gifts, and enjoy what Allah made lawful. The lawful part of life is vast. Yet a strange thing happens when one forbidden door becomes attractive: the whole lawful world disappears from view, and the one closed door starts to feel like the entire religion.
Allah knows this tendency in us. He knows that the forbidden thing can shine simply because it is forbidden, and that discipline can feel like deprivation while it is saving us from something we cannot yet see. A person may only understand the mercy years later. Sometimes they understand it by watching somebody else pay the price.
## You are allowed to struggle
There is a difference between rejecting a command and struggling to obey it. The first says Allah has no right to ask. The second may say: my Lord, I know You have the right, but I am weak and I need Your help.
That second sentence is a dua.
Too many people imagine that a good Muslim reaches a stage where obedience becomes effortless. Then they fall, feel desire, miss a prayer, return to an old habit, or resent the difficulty of a command, and conclude that something in their faith must have been false. The existence of a struggle does not prove that worship has failed. The struggle may be where the worship is happening.
Allah did not place us inside this work and leave us alone with willpower. He gave us prayer, dua, fasting, good company, repentance, and the ability to begin again. He also promised that striving toward Him is answered with guidance.
Allah says: *“As for those who struggle in Our cause, We will surely guide them along Our Way. And Allah is certainly with the good-doers.”* (Surah Al-Ankabut, aya 69)
The person fighting the same sin for the tenth time may be ashamed to ask again. Ask again. The person who finds a command difficult may worry that Allah is disgusted by the difficulty. He is the One who created the heart carrying it. Alhamdulillah, He did not make perfection the price of approaching Him.
And when the task feels larger than you, the Qur’an gives you words plain enough to say without pretending: *“Allah does not require of any soul more than what it can afford.”* (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 286)
## The rule and the One who gave it
Islam has rules. It would be dishonest to pretend otherwise. Some are easy to understand, some require study, and some ask us to place Allah’s judgment above the mood of our time and the pull inside our own chest.
But Islam is not just a set of rules. It is knowing Allah, loving Him, trusting Him, and letting that knowledge reach the ordinary parts of life. The prayer time. The money in your hand. The argument where you could lie. The screen nobody else can see. The appetite asking to be obeyed.
The rules are what trust in Allah looks like on an ordinary Tuesday.
And if you are still struggling with one of them, do not stand outside until you feel worthy. Bring the struggle with you. Learn the ruling properly. Ask Allah for help. Take one step, then another, and return whenever you fall. A limit from Allah is not proof that He wants you gone. It is part of how He leads you home.
Allah says: *“How could He not know His Own creation, when He is the Most Subtle, All-Aware?”* (Surah Al-Mulk, aya 14)
Once you stop seeing limits as mercy, you start calling poison freedom. You start watching a world built on appetite and calling it normal. That is how the ache spreads through a whole culture, not just through one private life.
