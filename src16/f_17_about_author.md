Rayan Mokhtari was born and raised in Berkane, in the far northeast of Morocco. He grew up in a neighbourhood built around orange trees and the local masjid, in a family where Islam was background air rather than a set of decisions. His uncle’s farm just outside town is one of the places he has in mind whenever the book talks about planting, waiting, and trusting that the rest belongs to Allah.

He left Berkane at seventeen to study in Belgium, and it was there, in a country where Islam has to be explained rather than assumed, that the questions in this book became unavoidable. When a classmate asks you why you believe what you believe, the inherited answer turns out not to be enough. You have to go back and build something that holds.

A large part of what drove the writing was a friendship. At IMSAL, a mosque in Belgium that welcomed international students from every background, he met Andreas, a Belgian who had chosen Islam as an adult, deliberately and after years of honest thinking. Watching someone arrive at the faith Rayan had been given, and seeing it earned rather than inherited, sent him back to examine everything he had never had to justify for himself.

He is not a scholar. He has not memorized the Qur’an or studied fiqh in a formal setting. What he has done is refuse to let a question rest until the answer is solid enough to build on, and then write it down the way he would say it to a younger version of himself.

This is his first book. He lives between Morocco and Belgium.
