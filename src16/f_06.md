*The sentence that frees us from every smaller master.*
What do we mean when we say *La ilaha illallah*?
I spent my first eighteen years in Morocco before leaving for university in Belgium, and I watched how easily the sentence became part of the air around us. After a death, men gather in rows and repeat it together until the words turn into a sound pattern, almost detached from the demand they make. The Shahada runs through nasheeds, hangs framed on living room walls, and slips out on its own the moment someone is startled, while life carries on underneath it exactly as before. A person can know it is the first pillar of Islam and still treat it as the least urgent one. We argue endlessly about the rulings that make noise and skip the foundation they are supposed to rest on without even noticing. Prayer gets discussed down to the finest detail, Zakat gets calculated to the last percent, and Ramadan gets organized around meal plans. All of that matters, yet the sentence that decides who actually owns the heart goes unexamined.
The Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, sent Muadh ibn Jabal, one of his companions, to Yemen and the order he gave him lays out the whole hierarchy of the religion. Ibn Abbas, the Prophet’s cousin, may Allah be pleased with him, reported: “When the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, sent Mu’adh to Yemen, he said to him, 'You are going to a nation from the People of the Scripture, so let the first thing to which you will invite them be the Tauhid of Allah. If they learn that, tell them that Allah has enjoined on them five prayers to be offered in one day and one night. And if they pray, tell them that Allah has enjoined on them Zakat of their properties, and it is to be taken from the rich among them and given to the poor.'” (Sahih al-Bukhari).
The people of Yemen already had scripture, knew the names of past prophets, and had established places of worship. Even so, the Prophet sent Muadh straight to Tawhid before a single word about prayer or charity, and only once Tawhid was settled did prayer enter, and only once prayer was established did Zakat follow. He was settling the deepest question in the human heart first, so that everything built on top of it would have something solid to stand on. The early Muslims came to know their Lord before they were handed a single rule, and once He was known, the rules were light to carry.
We can see what that foundation produced the moment a command finally came down. The prohibition of alcohol reached a Madinah where wine was completely ordinary, poured at gatherings and stored in homes. Anas ibn Malik, the Prophet’s young attendant, may Allah be pleased with him, reported: “I was serving Abu Ubaydah, Abu Talhah, and Ubayy ibn Ka’b with a drink prepared from ripe and unripe dates. Then somebody came to them and said, 'Alcoholic drinks have been prohibited.' On hearing that, Abu Talhah said, 'Get up, O Anas, and pour it out!' So I poured it out.” He emptied the vessels right then, and the wine ran through the streets of Madinah (Sahih al-Bukhari). SubhanAllah, picture the drink already in your hand and letting it fall the instant you hear your Lord has spoken.
The same thing happened when the command for believing women to draw their coverings over themselves came down. Aisha, the Prophet’s wife, may Allah be pleased with her, reported: “May Allah have mercy on the early immigrant women. When the verse 'That they should draw their veils over their bosoms' was revealed, they tore their thick outer garments and made veils from them.” (Sahih al-Bukhari). The command arrived and the obedience was immediate, because the question of who they belonged to had been settled years before. When we already know Who is speaking, obedience stops being a negotiation.
## The heart’s true master
When we hear the word “worship,” we usually picture prostration, prayer, and fasting. Worship is far wider than the movements of the body. It is whatever commands our ultimate surrender, whatever receives the deepest love and the deepest fear.
Adi ibn Hatim, a former Christian Arab chief who later became one of the Prophet’s companions, may Allah be pleased with him, once came to the Prophet wearing a gold cross around his neck. The Prophet told him to remove the idol. Adi was confused: he and his people had never bowed to their rabbis and monks. The Prophet asked him a different question. “When they made the forbidden lawful for you and the lawful forbidden, did you follow them?” Adi said yes. “That,” the Prophet said, “was their worship of them.” (Sunan at-Tirmidhi).
Obedience in the thing that only Allah has the right to decide is itself a form of worship, even when no one bows and no one calls it religion. A person can recite the Shahada every day and still live as if provision belongs to a boss, dignity belongs to a crowd, and peace belongs to a screen. That is why this chapter can’t stay in abstract theology; the Qur’an is not describing museum idols, it is describing the living human heart.
The scholars explain *La ilaha illallah* as *La ma’buda bi haqqin illallah*: nothing has the right to be worshipped except Allah. The sentence begins with negation. *La ilaha*: no deity, no master over the heart. Then the affirmation: *illallah*, except Allah. The heart is cleared before it is filled.
## The unbroken chain
Allah tells us that the message of every prophet was identical at its root:
“We never sent a messenger before you ˹O Prophet˺ without revealing to him: 'There is no god ˹worthy of worship˺ except Me, so worship Me ˹alone˺.'” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 25)
Every prophet stepped into a society that had crowned a false god, and every prophet waged a war against that specific form of servitude. Go back through their lives and you see the same illness taking different shapes in different ages, adapting itself to the weaknesses of each era.
Nuh, peace be upon him, faced grief turned to stone. His people didn’t stumble into idolatry through malice; they stumbled through love and the slow erosion of knowledge. The Qur’an preserves the names of their idols: Wadd, Suwa, Yaghuth, Ya’uq, and Nasr. These were originally names of righteous, pious men. When they died, their community was devastated. Someone suggested setting up statues in the places these men used to sit, just to remember them, just to feel inspired. No one bowed to them at first. Then that generation died, and the next one forgot the origin, and the stones became gods. A genuine love for a righteous man, left without correction, became shirk over the span of two generations.
“Indeed, We sent Noah to his people, and he remained among them for a thousand years, less fifty. Then the Flood overtook them, while they persisted in wrongdoing.” (Surah Al-Ankabut, aya 14)
Nine hundred and fifty years on a single message. I can barely hold the same conviction for nine hundred and fifty days. That figure alone tells you the gravity of Tawhid in the scale of a prophet.
The same drift happens inside our own traditions. In the shrines of the *awliya*, saints people get attached to, and in the Zawiya Boutchichiyya in our own Morocco, the psychology of Nuh’s people repeats in real time. People begin with genuine respect for a righteous scholar, build a structure over his grave to honor his memory, then visit to feel close to his piety. A generation later the reverence warps into dependency. People tie threads to the gates, rub the wood for blessings, ask the dead man for a child, for healing, for provision. They carry their prayers to a grave rather than to the One the man in that grave spent his whole life worshipping. Shirk slips in through a corrupted sense of love.
After the flood, the people of Aad built a civilization on strength. Tall, wealthy, architecturally dominant, they boasted openly: who is superior to us in might? Allah sent their brother Hud, peace be upon him, with the same message every other prophet carried, and they brushed him aside.
“As for ’Ad, they acted arrogantly throughout the land with no right, boasting, 'Who is superior to us in might?' Did they not see that Allah Who created them was far superior to them in might?” (Surah Fussilat, aya 15)
Allah destroyed them with an invisible force: a furious, roaring wind that battered them for seven nights and eight days, leaving their towering bodies scattered on the ground like hollow, uprooted palm trunks. A civilization that asked who could match its strength was erased by moving air.
Then came Thamud. Their false god was architectural permanence. They carved palaces directly into the faces of solid mountains, convinced that if they mastered the stone they could secure their lives against disruption. Allah sent their brother Salih, peace be upon him, and the leaders of Thamud handed him a test. They pointed to a solitary, massive rock and demanded he bring a living, pregnant she-camel out of it. Allah brought the camel out of the stone in front of their eyes. The command was simple: let her drink from your water and graze on your land. Their arrogance and possessiveness won. They hamstrung and killed her, choosing their carved mountains over the sign from the Maker of mountains, and a single blast from the sky left them dead inside the very fortresses they thought would protect them.
“They used to carve homes in the mountains, feeling secure.” (Surah Al-Hijr, aya 82)
With Ibrahim, peace be upon him, the idols were the family trade. His father Azar carved them, sold them, and worshipped them. The grief of that relationship runs through the Qur’an in a way that other prophet stories don’t. Ibrahim looked at his own father and saw a man with his hands full of things that couldn’t hear him, couldn’t help him, couldn’t protect him.
“˹Remember˺ when Abraham said to his father, Azar, 'Do you take idols as gods? It is clear to me that you and your people are entirely astray.'” (Surah Al-An’am, aya 74)
He smashed their idols with an axe while the people were away, leaving only the largest one standing with the axe hung around its neck. When they returned in fury and demanded to know who had done it, he pointed to the statue. Ask it, he said; if it can speak, ask it. They knew it couldn’t speak. They knew it couldn’t defend itself. They threw him into a fire anyway, because the argument was never really intellectual: it was about not letting go of what the fathers had done. Allah saved him from the fire and sent him on to face a harder enemy, a king who pointed at his own chest and said he could give life and death. Ibrahim asked him to make the sun rise from the west. The king fell silent.
Musa, peace be upon him, walked into the loudest false god in the whole Qur’an. Pharaoh didn’t hide behind a priesthood or a memorial:
“saying, 'I am your lord, the most high!'” (Surah An-Nazi’at, aya 24)
He used the agriculture, the wealth, and the river system of Egypt to blind his people into submission: am I not sovereign over Egypt, and all these streams flowing at my feet? This is Taghut in its rawest political form: one man demanding that the fear, the provision, the law, and the obedience of an entire people run through him alone. Even inside Pharaoh’s own house, his lie did not hold. His wife Asiya turned away from his throne and asked Allah for a house near Him in Paradise. Musa walked into that court with a wooden staff and the truth. Allah drowned the god-king in the very water he boasted he ruled, and left his body as a sign.
With Isa, peace be upon him, the distortion was less dramatic and reached further. He came to people burdened by scholars who had weaponized religion for their own gain. After he departed, some of those who claimed to love him raised him past his true rank as a human servant and a noble messenger, until they worshipped him alongside God. What makes this especially striking is that the Qur’an shows us how Isa defined himself, from the very cradle, before anyone could project a theology onto him: his first word as an infant speaking by miracle was servant, not son of God, not God incarnate. And on the Day of Judgment, Allah will question him directly in front of all of humanity:
“And ˹on Judgment Day˺ Allah will say, 'O Jesus, son of Mary! Did you ever ask the people to worship you and your mother as gods besides Allah?'” (Surah Al-Ma’idah, aya 116)
Isa will disown those who worshipped him. False gods, including the righteous ones raised to that rank against their will, can’t save anyone on that Day.
Finally, the Prophet Muhammad, blessings and peace be upon him, was sent back to the exact house Ibrahim had raised for Tawhid. He spent thirteen years in Makkah fighting the idols surrounding the Kaabah, and the people of Makkah were commercially invested in them: the pilgrimage traffic was their livelihood, the 360 idols around the Kaabah were their business model, and monotheism threatened every part of it.
“Has he reduced ˹all˺ the gods to One God? Indeed, this is something totally bizarre!” (Surah Sad, aya 5)
When he re-entered Makkah victorious, he didn’t just declare the city conquered and order the idols removed. He walked up to them himself and started striking them down with his own stick, reciting the verse of truth overcoming falsehood with every blow, dismantling shirk in the exact spot his forefather Ibrahim had built for Tawhid.
One thread runs through every one of these lives: the same creed, handed down intact, aimed each time at whatever form of false sovereignty the current age had built.
## The heart’s false masters, today
Taghut is the Qur’anic term for any false god, broadly defined by the scholars as anything worshipped, obeyed, or followed past the limit Allah set for it. The Qur’an places turning away from Taghut in the very first half of faith:
“So whoever renounces false gods and believes in Allah has certainly grasped the firmest, unfailing hand-hold.” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 256)
Renouncing the false gods comes first. You can’t take hold of the rope until you’ve let go of everything you were gripping instead. The idols don’t look like Wadd and Suwa anymore. They wear different faces in this century, but the structure underneath is the same.
## The grief you won’t let heal
Pain is a strange thing to call a god, but anything that dictates our ultimate identity is a master. A trauma or grievance can move from something that happened to us to the core of who we are, and the moment it does, everything else gets forced to orbit it. The wound becomes the excuse, the identity, and eventually the brand. Sociologists have documented this shift from what they call a “dignity culture” toward a “victimhood culture”: a world where moral worth accrues by broadcasting fragility rather than demonstrating resilience.
The therapy-speak vocabulary that spread through platforms like TikTok made this easier. Terms like toxic, gaslighting, narcissist, and trauma got unmoored from their clinical definitions and turned into identity markers. The person who has suffered most convincingly earns the most right to be heard, and once that becomes the game, healing threatens the position. If the pain is gone, the identity is gone.
There’s also a physiological hook: nursing a grievance triggers the brain’s reward circuits in ways that are genuinely hard to walk away from. The feeling of being wronged gives a trance-like sense of control and self-righteousness that can become its own craving. A person ends up defending their right to be miserable more fiercely than they defend their faith.
Islam recognizes pain. It demands justice. What it never permits is making the pain the center of who we are:
“Indeed, Allah does not wrong people in the least, but it is people who wrong themselves.” (Surah Yunus, aya 44)
Our identity is *'abd*, servant of Allah. That’s who we are before we are anyone’s victim.
## The substance
For millions of people, a chemical rules their life. Not metaphorically: the brain’s reward circuits get hijacked by drugs, alcohol, or highly engineered substances until the brain literally begins to prioritize the chemical over food, over the people it loves, over survival. The Purdue Pharma opioid crisis demonstrated that entire industries will manufacture this dependency to convert human pain into a revenue stream. Economists and public health researchers now classify the massive rise in overdose and alcohol-related fatalities across the developed world as “deaths of despair”: a cause they trace directly to the collapse of community, faith, and purpose in societies that handed people everything except a reason.
The body literally bows to the drug. Allah commanded us not to hand ourselves over to ruin:
“and do not let your own hands throw you into destruction” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 195)
It’s not a coincidence that secular recovery programs like Alcoholics Anonymous explicitly require a person to surrender their will to a “Higher Power” in step three. Even clinicians recognized that addiction is a spiritual usurpation: the only way to overthrow a tyrannical false god is to ask the True Lord for the strength to tear it down.
## What the mirror demands
This one is precise. The beauty standard as a genuine master of the heart is different from a healthy desire to look presentable. I mean the complete surrender of dignity, wealth, and physical health to an ever-moving external standard that was never going to hold still.
Facial recognition and social media filters now mathematically reward a single AI-optimized bone structure and penalize ordinary human faces. Doctors report a condition they’re calling “Zoom dysmorphia”: people enduring painful cosmetic surgery to look like their filtered video-call selves. The American Society of Plastic Surgeons reports hundreds of thousands of teenagers seeking cosmetic procedures before they’ve finished growing.
And it’s not a condition that only targets women. A parallel epidemic is devastating young men too, through muscle dysmorphia, sometimes called bigorexia. Men surrender their long-term health to steroid cycles and unregulated testosterone therapies chasing a hyper-masculine ideal that isn’t biologically sustainable. The gym becomes a temple of obsession where the body is worshipped and constantly found lacking.
“and do not display yourselves as women did in the days of ˹pre-Islamic˺ ignorance.” (Surah Al-Ahzab, aya 33)
Though this verse was addressed to women in its specific context, the spiritual disease it names, the desperate need to be seen, validated, and admired by the crowd, lands in anyone who makes their physical form their ultimate currency. The Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, cut the root of this anxiety: “Verily, Allah does not look at your appearance or wealth, but rather He looks at your hearts and actions.” (Sahih Muslim). When a heart genuinely internalizes that the only gaze that matters is Allah’s, the mirror loses its authority.
## The hunger
The Arabic word the Qur’an uses for this is *hawa* (any whim or impulse), though its sharpest form has always been lust. Modern culture has turned it into a creed: treat every carnal urge as a directive, obey the impulse before asking whether it serves you. Lust becomes identity; identity becomes law; law becomes sacred. Once that happens, restraint feels like oppression, and repentance feels like self-betrayal. Allah asks a precise question about the person who has made this their master:
“Have you seen ˹O Prophet˺ the one who has taken their own desires as their god? Will you then be a keeper over them?” (Surah Al-Furqan, aya 43)
The answer to this specific hunger was built into creation itself:
“And of His signs is that He created for you mates from among yourselves so that you may find tranquility in them, and He has placed between you compassion and mercy.” (Surah Ar-Rum, aya 21)
Sakinah. Tranquility. Whether the pressure comes from a podcast telling a man he doesn’t need a wife, or a feed telling a woman she doesn’t need a husband, both end up treating a spouse as an obstacle to whatever the self wants next. Marriage chosen with the right intention was never competing with a fulfilling life. It was designed to be the frame that holds one.
## The self on its own throne
When the boundaries of submission are broken, the self becomes the idol.
“Most certainly, one exceeds all bounds once they think they are self-sufficient.” (Surah Al-'Alaq, ayas 6 and 7)
We know the slogans because they’re the wallpaper of the age: follow your heart, live your truth, you do you. A whole industry packages this in a softer form, from manifesting coaches who promise a person can summon a new reality by wanting it hard enough, to astrology apps that let someone check what the stars permit before making a decision. Each one shifts the throne from Allah to the self and calls the displacement empowerment. And beyond these more commercially packaged versions, there are corners of the internet where people explicitly ask to be treated as idols, demanding others bow to them, call them “goddess” or “king,” and surrender their time and money for a fleeting digital interaction. The logic has overstepped metaphor entirely.
Taghut is not limited to any of the forms named so far. A person can worship their own intellect, refusing to submit to divine revelation unless their limited logic can approve it first. Some worship romantic love, demanding a human partner fulfill needs that only the Maker can satisfy. Others hand their morals to a political ideology rather than to the truth. The idols are everywhere once you know the structure: anything elevated to the place in the heart that belongs only to Allah.
## The feed
“Competition for more ˹gains˺ diverts you ˹from Allah˺, until you end up in ˹your˺ graves.” (Surah At-Takathur, ayas 1 and 2)
More. The word drives the internet. More followers, more views, more matches, more proof that we matter. A person can lose an entire evening checking whether strangers approved of a version of themselves that was edited for approval in the first place.
The men who built the machines that run on this have said out loud what they designed them to do. As we saw in the introduction, Sean Parker, Facebook’s founding president, admitted the aim was to exploit “a vulnerability in human psychology” with “a little dopamine hit” inside a “social-validation feedback loop,” and Chamath Palihapitiya told Stanford students those loops are “destroying how society works.” Tristan Harris, a former design ethicist at Google, calls the whole industry “a race to the bottom of the brain stem” for human attention. These aren’t sermons from a minbar; they’re confessions from the engineers.
AI pulls this hunger into a stranger place still. A person can now sit alone and pour their shame, their secrets, their fear of the future into a machine that answers instantly, patiently, and without the discomfort of a real human face. It feels like being known, but there’s no knowing there, only prediction and a voice trained to keep the conversation alive. The need itself is noble; Allah made us want to be heard, held, and understood. The danger begins when that need is carried to something that can’t love us back, can’t pray for us, can’t fear Allah for us, and can’t answer for a single word on the Day we stand before Him.
## The grind
Work has become a religious identity for the modern elite. The writer Derek Thompson described this in *The Atlantic* as “workism”: a creed that promises transcendence, community, and meaning through career achievement, in the space those things used to be found in organized religion. On LinkedIn the career gets performed like a daily liturgy, ambition slides into ego, and human value gets perfectly equated with productivity.
This idol asks for sacrifices that people would mock if a traditional religion asked for them. A person will willingly abandon their family, miss their prayers repeatedly, and destroy their health to climb a corporate ladder that could remove them next quarter.
“Whoever desires ˹only˺ this worldly life and its luxuries, We will pay them in full for their deeds in this life. It is they who will have nothing in the Hereafter except the Fire.” (Surah Hud, ayas 15 and 16)
A career is a means to earn a halal living. When a person believes their sustenance depends entirely on their own grinding rather than on the decree of Ar-Razzaq, the Provider, they’ve made the job their lord.
## Wealth
“Woe to every backbiter, slanderer, who amasses wealth ˹greedily˺ and counts it ˹repeatedly˺, thinking that their wealth will make them immortal!” (Surah Al-Humazah, ayas 1–3)
The person refreshing the bank app. Counting investments. Measuring a life purely by numbers. Wealth becomes a promise of immortality: if I own enough, no one can humiliate me, and death will somehow stay polite at the door. The Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, named the slavery plainly: Abu Hurayrah, one of the Prophet’s most prolific hadith narrators, may Allah be pleased with him, reported: “Wretched is the slave of the dinar, the slave of the dirham, and the slave of fine cloth. If he is given, he is pleased; and if he is not given, he is resentful.” (Sahih al-Bukhari). Slave of. A heart that is pleased only when money comes and turns resentful when it doesn’t has handed over something only Allah is meant to hold.
The Qur’an gives this worship a name and a face: Qarun, a man from the people of Musa, whose treasure was so vast that the keys to his storehouses alone were a burden on strong men. When the people of knowledge counselled him to carry the gift lightly and spend from it, he brushed them aside:
“He replied, 'I have been granted all this because of some knowledge I have.'” (Surah Al-Qasas, aya 78)
He went out before his people in his full display, and those whose eyes were fixed on this life wished they had the like of it. The ground closed the argument:
“Then We caused the earth to swallow him up, along with his home.” (Surah Al-Qasas, aya 81)
Every key that had bowed the backs of strong men went into the earth with him. Anas ibn Malik, the Prophet’s young attendant, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “If Adam’s son had a valley full of gold, he would like to have two valleys, for nothing fills his mouth except dust. And Allah forgives him who repents to Him.” (Sahih al-Bukhari). The hunger was never really about the gold. We reach the figure we swore would settle us, and the goalpost has already moved.
John D. Rockefeller, asked how much money is enough, is said to have answered: “Just a little bit more.” Jim Carrey told a graduating class: “I think everybody should get rich and famous and do everything they ever dreamed of, so they can see that it’s not the answer.” We don’t have to look only at Western examples; in our own communities a family buries itself in debt for a wedding built to be filmed, a man is measured by the badge on the car he parks outside the mosque, and back home in Morocco a person’s standing in a gathering rises and falls with what everyone knows they own.
## The children
There's a pattern I've watched in Moroccan communities my whole life, and I suspect you've seen it too if you grew up anywhere with a similar culture. A man reaches his thirties or forties and his life looks like a series of unresolved problems: bad company, substances, no footing to speak of, a vague hostility to the whole project of growing up. The family reaches a decision. Get him married. Get him kids. The logic isn't written anywhere but it moves through households like folk wisdom: once he has a family, he'll settle down. He won't have time for the street at night. Responsibility will do what religion and advice didn't.
I'll be fair, sometimes it looks like it works, in the short term. A man with a baby at home has less time for the things that were pulling him. But when I look at those same men a decade later, I don't always see someone who found Allah. I see someone who swapped one set of preoccupations for another. The children absorbed the energy that used to go to the street, but the center never moved. The heart was never rebuilt. It was just kept busy. A busy heart and a believing heart aren't the same thing.
The community treats children as the cure because children occupy, and occupation can look like reform. But an adornment placed on a ruin doesn't fix the ruin. You build a house first, then you add what makes it beautiful. That's exactly what Allah called them:
"Wealth and children are the adornments of this worldly life, but the everlasting good deeds are far better with your Lord in reward and in hope." (Surah Al-Kahf, aya 46)
People say a man becomes wiser once he's married. I've heard this more times than I can count. It's not quite right. What happens is that he becomes busy, busy enough that the old chaos doesn't fit anymore, which looks like wisdom from the outside. But sometimes he gets so busy that salah doesn't fit either. The children take the slot that the remembrance of Allah was supposed to hold, and that's a different problem wearing a respectable face.
The warning in the Qur'an isn't addressed to strangers to Islam. It goes to the believing community, precisely because this pull doesn't disappear once you've said the Shahada:
"O believers! Do not let your wealth or your children divert you from the remembrance of Allah. For whoever does so, it is they who are the true losers." (Surah Al-Munafiqun, aya 9)
Iman has to come first. The believing heart arrives before the family arrives, because a person who has genuinely settled the question of who Allah is in their life will carry marriage and children differently. They'll carry them as a trust from Allah, not as a replacement for Him. A man who was handed a wife and children before his heart had any anchor didn't get a cure; he got a more complicated version of the same problem. And he's probably going to raise children who inherit the same gap.
## The crowd in the stadium
There is another false god that rarely gets named, because it doesn’t look like desire or wealth. It looks like loyalty, belonging, identity. The crowd we belong to, and what it can demand.
Watch what happens in Morocco when the national team wins. In 2022, the Atlas Lions became the first team from Africa or the Arab world in the ninety-two-year history of the World Cup to reach a semi-final, beating Spain and Portugal along the way, and Casablanca’s United Nations Square filled with more people than most of the country’s mosques will ever hold at once. In 2026 Morocco climbed to sixth in the FIFA world rankings and reached the quarter-finals again, and the streets filled again. The devotion doesn’t stay inside the stadium: when Morocco knocked the Netherlands out of the 2026 World Cup, celebrations in Dutch cities with large Moroccan communities turned into confrontations with riot police in The Hague. Inside Casablanca, the derby between Raja and Wydad is rated one of the fiercest in the world, and the violence sometimes turns inward: in 2016, clashes between rival Raja ultras groups during a single match left two people dead and forty-nine injured in one afternoon.
I was raised in Berkane, right along the main boulevard, and I could describe one of those nights blind. The street fills within minutes. Cars circle with horns held down, motorcycles rev until your ears ring, strangers chanting in the middle of the road. A few years ago one of those wins landed right at Maghrib, the sunset prayer. I stood at the window unable to hear the adhan over the celebration outside, in a country where that same call goes out five times a day without anyone lowering their voice for it. Men and women who would never otherwise be seen together in public were out side by side; a woman dressed up for the occasion had a young man trailing her, more interested in her than in the score.
The question that should follow a person home after a night like that has nothing to do with football. It’s the question this chapter opened with: what do we actually mean when we say La ilaha illallah? Is it a truth that governs what pulls us into the street at Maghrib, or is it a phrase we reach for at a funeral and set down everywhere else?
The point isn’t that loving a team is itself shirk. The point is what the intensity reveals. A human being who can weep, chant, spend, and risk arrest for eleven men in a shirt they’ve never met is not a creature with a shallow capacity for devotion. The capacity is enormous. It’s simply badly aimed. The same fitrah that empties a street at Maghrib is the fitrah that, properly directed, empties itself in prostration five times a day.
## What we call culture
Of all the false gods, this one hides best, because it wears the face of family and belonging. It almost never announces itself as worship. It speaks through softer language: this is who we are, this is what we have always done.
But the moment inherited practice is placed above what Allah revealed, culture has climbed onto the throne. The Qur’an puts this reflex on record as the standard reply of every people who turned their prophet away:
“When it is said to them, 'Follow what Allah has revealed,' they reply, 'No! We ˹only˺ follow what we found our forefathers practicing.' ˹Would they still do so,˺ even if their forefathers had ˹absolutely˺ no understanding or guidance?” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 170)
The verse asks one question: what if they were wrong? Tradition, on its own, settles nothing. A practice doesn’t become true because it is a century old, and it doesn’t become Sunnah because our grandmothers did it. The only question that decides the matter is whether Allah and His Messenger, blessings and peace be upon him, actually taught it. This wasn’t the excuse of one stubborn nation; it was the wall every prophet crashed into, from the people of Nuh clinging to their memorials to the Makkans guarding the pantheon around the Kaabah. They all reached for the same defence.
I don’t have to look past Morocco to find it. Take Ashura. In the Sunnah it is a day of fasting; the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, fasted the tenth of Muharram and encouraged it. But in Morocco the day has been layered with things that have nothing to do with what he actually did: the bonfires children leap over at night, the water thrown and splashed the next morning, the drums, the toys, the doors knocked for handouts. Much of this traces back to older Amazigh, Andalusian, and Jewish customs that accumulated on the date over centuries. Ask where the fire and the water come from in the religion, and the honest answer is that they don’t; they come from “this is what we have always done.”
The same instinct shapes how we handle death, wrapping the grieving household in gatherings and fixed days and hired recitations the first generations never knew, and questioning any of it feels like an insult to the family rather than a return to the Sunnah.
Culture itself is not the enemy, and the Qur’an keeps that balance. Islam didn’t erase the customs of the Arabs; it kept their generosity, their language, their poetry, and simply cut away what contradicted the truth. There is nothing wrong, and much that is beautiful, in Moroccan hospitality, dress, food, and the warmth of how we gather. The disease begins only when culture is handed the final word over the deen: when *3ib*, what people will say, outranks *haram*, what Allah has said, and when we defend an inherited practice we can’t trace to the Book or the Sunnah purely because letting it go feels like betraying the people we love.
The test is simple: when revelation and inheritance disagree, which one gets cut?
## Three things Tawhid means
The scholars of Ahl-us-Sunnah (the mainstream Sunni scholarly tradition) explain the cure through three things that can’t be separated.
The first is affirming Allah’s absolute Lordship: *Tawhid ar-Rububiyyah*. Allah alone creates, owns, sustains, gives life, gives death, provides, withholds, raises, and lowers. The argument for why there can only be one Lord is made in a single aya:
“Had there been other gods besides Allah in the heavens or the earth, both ˹realms˺ would have surely been corrupted. So Glorified is Allah, Lord of the Throne, far above what they claim.” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 22)
A being who can be checked, blocked, or overruled by another is not a god. The laws of physics hold steady across the whole universe, reading like the work of one will. Then Allah underlines the same point with the smallest possible example:
“O humanity! A lesson is set forth, so listen to it ˹carefully˺: those ˹idols˺ you invoke besides Allah can never create ˹so much as˺ a fly, even if they ˹all˺ were to come together for that. And if a fly were to snatch anything away from them, they cannot ˹even˺ retrieve it from the fly. How powerless are those who invoke and those invoked!” (Surah Al-Hajj, aya 73)
Every false lord named in this chapter, the tyrant who claimed the sky, the feed that decides what a person craves, the crowd that can empty a street at Maghrib, could gather every resource they’ve ever commanded and still fail to build the one insect a person swats away without thinking.
The second is affirming the Names and Attributes of Allah exactly as He affirmed them for Himself: *Tawhid al-Asma wa as-Sifat*. We affirm them without twisting their meanings, denying them, comparing them to creation, or asking how.
“There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing.” (Surah Ash-Shura, aya 11)
He is *Al-Hayy*, the Ever-Living, whose life had no beginning and has no end. He is *Al-Alim*, whose knowledge takes in everything at once, down to the weight of an atom in the darkest trench of the sea. When He wills a thing He only says *“Be,”* and it is. He truly loves, and He is truly pleased with the people of faith. He even rejoices at the return of a repentant servant. Anas ibn Malik, the Prophet’s young attendant, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “Allah is more joyful at the repentance of His servant than one of you who loses his riding beast in a barren desert and then, against all hope, finds it again.” (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim). There are also attributes we would never have known had He not told us: His Face, His two Hands, His Eyes. We affirm them exactly as they were affirmed, handing the “how” back to Him without imagining a shape.
This isn’t dry creed. It changes what we do when we are completely alone. If we know Allah is *Al-Basir*, the All-Seeing, the dark room changes. The locked phone changes. The private search changes. A person can fear a security camera in a shop more than they fear Allah watching them in their own bedroom. That is a worship problem before it is a knowledge problem.
The third is singling Allah out in worship: *Tawhid al-Uluhiyyah*. This is the natural destination of the first two branches. If He alone creates and provides, and if He alone holds the attributes of absolute perfection, then He alone deserves to receive the heart’s deepest love, fear, hope, reliance, and obedience.
“Say, 'Surely my prayer, my sacrifice, my life, and my death are all for Allah, Lord of all worlds. He has no partner.'” (Surah Al-An’am, ayas 162 and 163)
Tawhid is not a sentence kept inside the mosque. It reaches our career, appetite, bedroom, spending, anger, ambitions, friendships, marriage, and plans for the future.
## The life gathered for Allah
Yusuf, peace be upon him, asked a piercing question from a prison cell:
“O my fellow-prisoners! Which is far better: many different lords or Allah, the One, the Supreme?” (Surah Yusuf, aya 39)
Many different lords. That’s modern life named in four words. The body says obey me, the feed says entertain me, the beauty standard says fix yourself, the career says sacrifice your family, the crowd says fear me.
Allah says: come back to one Lord.
A person serving one Lord can finally become whole. They still work, eat, marry, study, earn, laugh, and enjoy what Allah made lawful. Tawhid doesn’t flatten life; it gathers it. Prayer, money, appetite, beauty, ambition, pain, love, and death all face one direction.
“Do not set up any other god with Allah, or you will end up condemned, abandoned.” (Surah Al-Isra, aya 22)
False gods use us and leave us. Desire leaves us emptier. Wealth leaves us at the grave. Beauty leaves us looking in the mirror one day, grieving what faded. The crowd leaves us the moment the performance fails. Only Allah remains.
The one who truly understands *La ilaha illallah* begins to ask different questions. What am I afraid to lose? Whose anger do I dread? What do I obey when I’m tired? What would I refuse to give up even if I knew it was destroying me? Those questions reveal the actual masters. And then the Shahada comes back, not as a chant, but as a sword: cutting through the false masters one by one until only Allah remains worthy of the heart’s deepest surrender.
