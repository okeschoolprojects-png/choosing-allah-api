*A conversation about the first question you’ll ever be asked when you leave this world: who is your Lord?*
Knowing Him changes everything. But there’s a colder question underneath that, one modern life makes very easy to ask: how do we know any of this is real?
Growing up in Berkane, nobody debated whether God existed. The adhan came over the rooftops five times a day. Every conversation ended in inshallah or alhamdulillah, not as performance but as the basic rhythm of life, the way English speakers say “fine” when someone asks how they’re doing. God wasn’t a conclusion I’d reached. He was the air I’d always breathed.
Then I moved to Belgium for university, and the air changed. My roommate was smart, thoughtful, genuinely decent. He’d grown up in a home where God was never mentioned, not out of hostility, just irrelevance. He just held this assumption underneath everything he said: we’re here by chance, we’re gone when we’re gone, and the task is to be good and build something meaningful in the time between. No drama, no claim either way. Just a different starting point.
I remember sitting with that assumption for the first time, properly, and noticing something go slightly cold in me. Not a crisis; a question coming into focus: do I know this is true, or am I just repeating what my walls said? Because if I don’t know, I want to.
That’s the question this chapter is for. Go looking yourself, instead of just inheriting my answer or waving it away.
When we do go looking, we hear a very loud modern answer to where we came from. Yuval Noah Harari, whose books circulate like scripture through Silicon Valley’s boardrooms, writes in *Homo Deus* that 'organisms are algorithms' and human beings are 'hackable animals.' He argues that free will is a myth because emotions are 'biochemical algorithms that are vital for the survival and reproduction of all mammals.' We’re machines, in his telling: processing inputs, producing outputs, in a universe with no author and no destination.
The nineteenth century saw it coming. Nietzsche and Dostoevsky both warned that killing the idea of God would leave civilization unable to answer the simplest question: why should anyone keep going? Dostoevsky put the logic in one line in *The Brothers Karamazov*: “If there is no God, then everything is permitted.” A sentence that starts as philosophy and ends, eventually, as policy.
We’re living that prediction out. By almost every material measure, this is the most comfortable era in human history. We’ve also never been lonelier. The wealthiest, most medicated societies on earth keep producing people who feel hollow. We’ve started filling that hollow with artificial intimacy; young people spending entire days talking to AI chatbots, building emotional lives around lines of code designed to simulate care.
In early 2024, a fourteen-year-old boy named Sewell Setzer III died by suicide after becoming deeply attached to an AI character on the platform [Character.AI](http://Character.AI). His mother’s wrongful-death lawsuit alleges that he withdrew from the world, stopped sleeping, and poured his deepest feelings into a machine that responded with calibrated affection. The case is a tragedy, not a tidy explanation for every lonely person who turns to a screen. But a chatbot can offer the feeling of being cared for without asking anything back, no need to show up for somebody else, no chance of being inconvenienced by their need.
A crisis of meaning can sit beside depression, loneliness, or a mind that genuinely needs treatment, the same way a fever can sit beside a real infection. None of those things are a simple failure of belief. Still, when people lose the sense that life has any given purpose, they search desperately for something else to hold it on. Some pour their identity entirely into political tribes or online subcultures and defend them with the devotion of the religious. Others have opted out of having families, announcing on camera that bringing a child into this world is a moral crime. When a belief system falls apart, what’s left is a drowning person grabbing at debris.
Look at the people who reached the top of what this world is selling. If money, fame, beauty, and applause were the things we were built for, the people who gathered the most of them should be the most at peace. Jim Carrey, who climbed as high as an entertainer can climb, said it plainly: “I think everybody should get rich and famous and do everything they ever dreamed of so they can see that it’s not the answer.” Tom Brady, sitting on three Super Bowl rings at twenty-seven, admitted this on national television. In a 2005 interview on *60 Minutes*, he said:
“Why do I have three Super Bowl rings, and still think there’s something greater out there for me? I mean, maybe a lot of people would say, 'Hey man, this is what is.' I reached my goal, my dream, my life. Me, I think: God, it’s gotta be more than this. I mean this can’t be what it’s all cracked up to be. I mean I’ve done it. I’m 27. And what else is there for me?”
The interviewer asked what the answer was. The greatest quarterback alive could only say: “I wish I knew.”
He held the prize and felt nothing in his hands.
For many, that emptiness became fatal. Avicii filled stadiums on every continent, then took his own life in 2018. Within months of each other, Chester Bennington and Chris Cornell did the same. The ache in them wasn’t faulty wiring.
## The mask of comfort
Have you ever wondered: if we were built to know Him, why do so many of us feel nothing?
We live under a massive, artificial layer of comfort. We’ve padded our lives so thickly with convenience and noise that we’ve convinced ourselves we’re completely independent. We don’t feel the need for a Lord because the system around us is designed to make us feel like lords.
But watch what happens when real danger hits and that layer evaporates. A sudden, terrifying medical diagnosis, or the death of someone you love without warning. Any moment where the structure of your life collapses under you. In those moments, nobody prays to their bank account. Nobody reaches for their follower count. Every one of us collapses into calling on something higher.
That instinct is the *fitrah*: your built-in compass pointing to the Creator. It was there all along, waiting to surface the second the illusion of control breaks.
Allah describes it as a design built into every person before they ever choose a religion:
“So be steadfast in faith in all uprightness ˹O Prophet˺, the natural Way of Allah which He has instilled in ˹all˺ people. Let there be no change in this creation of Allah. That is the Straight Way, but most people do not know.” (Surah Ar-Rum, aya 30)
Abu Hurayrah, one of the Companions who devoted himself to memorizing and preserving the Prophet’s words and narrated more hadith than almost anyone else, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, put the same truth in a sentence: “No child is born except on al-fitrah, then his parents make him a Jew, a Christian, or a Magian.” (Sahih al-Bukhari).
You and I already stood in that testimony, long before language or a home or a culture wrote over it, when Allah brought forth every soul and asked if He was our Lord. We already answered yes once. Whatever layers settle over that recognition, it is still there underneath.
We saw it plainly when the earthquake struck the High Atlas Mountains south of Marrakesh in September 2023. Entire villages came down in seconds. A Moroccan boy climbing out of the rubble in the dark described pure terror, and in the same breath gave thanks to God that his family was alive, before he’d even checked whether his phone still worked. No script, no calm afternoon for theology. A mother interviewed the same night, still covered in the dust of her own collapsed home, kept saying alhamdulillah between sentences, not as a phrase rehearsed for the camera but because gratitude was the only language left once the walls came down.
The same country, just a year before, emptied its streets for the 2022 World Cup. Moroccans closed their shops, abandoned their routines, and poured into the streets when the Atlas Lions became the first African or Arab team in World Cup history to reach a semi-final. Casablanca filled with more people than had gathered in the kingdom in living memory. The fitrah and the World Cup frenzy came from the same organ.
Half a world away, the 2004 tsunami stripped Indonesia’s coastlines of every comfort in minutes, and the mosques left standing filled with people falling into prostration. Same collapse, same reflex, on the other side of the world from Casablanca.
The Qur’an describes this pattern precisely:
“If they happen to be aboard a ship ˹caught in a storm˺, they cry out to Allah ˹alone˺ in sincere devotion. But as soon as He delivers them ˹safely˺ to shore, they associate ˹others with Him once again˺.” (Surah Al-Ankabut, aya 65)
The fitrah tells us why this question keeps returning. It points the heart in a direction.
## The witness of the dying
There’s one danger that never passes: the moment of death.
A dying person can’t reach for a phone, savings, followers, or the reputations we spent years building. Everything we lean on through life stands uselessly at the edge of the bed. Doctors and nurses who sit with people in their final hours describe something they rarely say out loud: the proud soften, and people who spent decades insisting there was nothing out there begin to whisper to Someone.
The Qur’an gives us the clearest example there is: Pharaoh, a man who spent his whole life claiming to be a god himself. When the water finally closed over him:
“We brought the Children of Israel across the sea. Then Pharaoh and his soldiers pursued them unjustly and oppressively. But as Pharaoh was drowning, he cried out, 'I believe that there is no god except that in whom the Children of Israel believe, and I am ˹now˺ one of those who submit.'” (Surah Yunus, aya 90)
The answer came at once:
“˹He was told,˺ 'Now ˹you believe˺? But you always disobeyed and were one of the corruptors.'” (Surah Yunus, aya 91)
There’s something worth sitting with in how harsh that reply sounds. The belief was available to him every ordinary Tuesday of his life. He waited until it cost him nothing, until faith was just a reflex squeezed out by terror. Faith offered under the water, when every other door has slammed shut, isn’t the free response the test asks for.
Allah describes that final moment for all of us, the point where the soul begins to leave and the watching family can do absolutely nothing:
“Why then ˹are you helpless˺ when the soul ˹of a dying person˺ reaches ˹their˺ throat, while you are looking on? And We are nearer to such a person than you, but you cannot see.” (Surah Al-Waqi’ah, ayas 83–85)
I’m not saying this to frighten you. The truth we argue against on a comfortable afternoon is often the one our souls reach for at the end, and it’s worth meeting it now, on your own terms.
## The argument in the mind
Pharaoh ran out of ordinary Tuesdays. You still have yours. The Qur’an doesn’t wait for the walls to come down. It asks you to use your reason right now, on a day like this one. In Surah At-Tur, two verses close the door on every other alternative:
“Or were they created by nothing, or are they ˹their own˺ creators?” (Surah At-Tur, aya 35)
We obviously exist. Did we come from nothing, with no cause at all? Nothing in human experience works that way; science itself is built on the principle that things have causes. Did we create ourselves? A thing can’t act before it exists. Since we didn’t make ourselves, Allah asks the next question:
“Or did they create the heavens and the earth? In fact, they have no certainty.” (Surah At-Tur, aya 36)
We didn’t stitch the universe together either. And modern cosmology points hard at a definite beginning, which requires a cause.
Notice how the aya ends: 'in fact, they have no certainty.' The reluctance to believe isn’t usually about evidence. It’s about implication. Believing we were made on purpose means our lives are on loan. It means we owe something to the Maker. And that’s exactly what we don’t want to hear. If we admit there’s a Creator, we have to admit our time and our choices aren’t just ours to spend however we please.
The supposed freedom on the other side of that refusal is an illusion. If we’re submitting to our shifting desires, or to the culture around us, or the crushing expectations of people who don’t even care about us, those are cruel masters. They’re never satisfied. Submitting to the One who made you is the only submission that gives something back in exchange.
These verses have a history. Shortly after the battle of Badr, a Makkan nobleman named Jubayr ibn Mut’im traveled to Madinah on unrelated business. He wasn’t a believer. When he arrived, the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, was leading Maghrib prayer and reciting Surah At-Tur. Jubayr stood outside and listened. As the recitation reached these verses, he later said his heart nearly left his chest. It would still be years before he embraced Islam, but that night planted something in him that never fully left.
## Looking at the horizon
Before we look at the signs, I want you to see where this whole method comes from.
Allah tells us He showed Ibrahim the dominion of the heavens and the earth so he would be sure in faith:
“We also showed Abraham the wonders of the heavens and the earth, so he would be sure in faith.” (Surah Al-An’am, aya 75)
Slow down on that last word: al-muqinin. Yaqeen is bigger than belief. Belief can sit in the head alongside a hundred doubts; yaqeen is certainty that has settled into the chest, the kind a person would stake everything on. And notice how Allah built it in Ibrahim. He didn’t pour it into his heart while he slept. He showed him things. The road you and I are about to walk is the oldest road to certainty there is, and the Teacher hasn’t changed.
Allah never asks you to chase Him or crack a hidden code. He gives one instruction, and He repeats it: look.
“We will show them Our signs in the universe and within themselves until it becomes clear to them that this Quran is the truth.” (Surah Fussilat, aya 53)
Two places: the horizon outside you and the world folded up inside you. That’s where we’ll walk.
**What the early scholars said**
The early scholars understood this too. When people came asking for proof of the Creator, they often pointed at ordinary things before they pointed at abstract arguments.
A story often told about Imam Abu Hanifah, may Allah have mercy on him, has him facing a group who came to argue that the world had no Maker. He is said to have asked: what would you say about a ship loaded with goods that crosses a violent sea, docks itself, unloads its cargo, and returns home, all with no captain and no crew? They told him that was impossible. He answered: if a single ship can’t sail without someone steering it, how does this entire world run itself with no Maker keeping it?
Even an ordinary bedouin gave an answer that has outlasted most theological treatises. When someone asked him how he knew his Lord existed, he said: “Camel dung points to a camel, and footprints point to a traveller. Does a sky possessing constellations, an earth possessing valleys, and oceans possessing waves not point to the All-Hearing, All-Seeing?” (Tafsir Ibn Kathir).
This was never just the instinct of a few brilliant scholars. It’s what Allah tells every one of us to do:
“Do they not ever reflect on camels, how they were masterfully created; and the sky, how it was raised high; and the mountains, how they were firmly set up; and the earth, how it was levelled out?” (Surah Al-Ghashiyah, ayas 17–20)
The animal you ride. The sky overhead. The mountains on the skyline. The ground underfoot. Allah isn’t handing you a difficult equation. He’s asking you to notice what you stopped noticing.
And here’s something worth sitting with: we already reason this way every day. Think of a curtain with a pen coming out from under it, writing a beautiful poem. You’d never think the curtain wrote it. You’d know there’s someone behind it. A cow, a bee, a seed: they’re just the curtain. The One acting behind them is the Creator, who knows exactly what we need.
Once you start reasoning that way, it gets hard to stop seeing how much has been arranged for you. The universe could have been a hostile blank, and almost all of it is. The strange exception is right here on earth, where everything a body needs sits precisely within reach, the way a room is prepared before a guest arrives. Allah points at it:
“Have We not smoothed out the earth ˹like a bed˺? And the mountains as stakes? And We created you in pairs and made your sleep a means for rest.” (Surah An-Naba, ayas 6–9)
And Allah keeps turning your face from one sign to the next:
“Indeed, in the creation of the heavens and the earth and the alternation of the night and the day are signs for those of understanding.” (Surah Aal Imran, aya 190)
Then, after walking us through all of it, He sets down the one challenge that has never been answered:
“This is Allah’s creation. Now show Me what those ˹gods˺ other than Him have created. In fact, the wrongdoers are clearly astray.” (Surah Luqman, aya 11)
You don’t need a telescope to run this test yourself; step outside on any ordinary evening and the same precision is sitting there waiting:
“There are countless signs on earth for those with sure faith, as there are within yourselves. Can you not see?” (Surah Adh-Dhariyat, ayas 20 and 21)
The same word He used when He showed Ibrahim the heavens and the earth: lil-muqinin. The signs are how yaqeen gets built.
## A universe fine-tuned for you
You just spent an evening finding signs at your feet. Now scale the same test up, because the earth is strange enough by itself, but it’s sitting inside something stranger still. The more carefully people study the universe, the stranger one fact keeps appearing: the rules holding everything together are set to numbers so precise that nudging almost any of them by a fraction would mean none of us were here. No stars. No planets. No water. And you wouldn’t be here to ask why.
**The numbers that shouldn’t be there**
The expansion of the universe is the clearest place to begin, because the margin for error is almost nonexistent. Speed it up slightly and matter flies apart too fast for gravity to ever gather it into stars or galaxies: you’re left with freezing, dark nothing. Slow it down by the same amount and gravity wins instead, and all that matter caves back in on itself before life gets a start. We’re balanced on a razor between those two outcomes, and the margin is a number with so many zeros it can barely be expressed.
“Indeed, We have created everything, perfectly preordained.” (Surah Al-Qamar, aya 49)
Come down to atoms. The invisible glue binding an atomic core, what physicists call the strong nuclear force, sits within a band that keeps a physical universe possible. Physicists still argue over exactly how narrow that band is, but move it far enough in either direction and you lose the elements a body is made of: no carbon, no chance of anything as complex as you. Even the most conservative estimates leave you standing in a universe that had far more ways to fail than to work. I read that line more than once, waiting for it to soften into exaggeration. It never did.
Zoom to our own neighborhood and the precision holds. Jupiter sits on the far edge of the solar system, pulling dangerous comets and asteroids off course long before they reach us. The sun stands at a distance where water stays liquid, and Earth’s tilt gives us seasons, rainfall, and the growing cycles our food depends on. Even water freezes in the one direction life needs: it expands and floats, laying a lid over ponds and oceans in winter so what lives beneath can survive.
Stephen Hawking, who spent his career on the physics of the cosmos and never believed in God, looked at these numbers and admitted in *A Brief History of Time*: “The remarkable fact is that the values of these numbers seem to have been very finely adjusted to make possible the development of life.”
Walking into a house on a freezing night and finding the heat on, the lamps lit, food on the table, and your name on the door: you wouldn’t conclude the house arranged itself by chance.
“The sun and the moon travel with precision.” (Surah Ar-Rahman, aya 5)
One word. It carries the whole argument. And then Allah invites any honest mind to go looking for a single flaw:
“He is the One Who created seven heavens, one above the other. You will never see any imperfection in the creation of the Most Compassionate. So look again: do you see any flaws?” (Surah Al-Mulk, aya 3)
People have taken Him up on that challenge through bigger and bigger telescopes for centuries, and so far nobody has come back with a flaw.
## The book written inside you
The search doesn’t only point outward, though. Turn your hands over for a second; the same argument is written into them. I remember sitting in a Belgian biology class being handed this fact like it was neutral information, no different from the periodic table, and nobody in the room seemed bothered by how strange it actually was.
Inside nearly every cell you carry sits a full set of instructions called DNA: a recipe book for building the whole of you, your eyes, the sound of your voice. It’s written in a chemical alphabet of four letters, lined up in a sequence billions of letters long. Print the code from one single cell and it would fill hundreds of thick volumes. Every cell you own carries its own complete copy. I sat with that number longer than I expected to. Hundreds of volumes, folded into something smaller than a pinhead, copied without error into every cell I’ve ever grown. Nobody handed me that thought in a lecture. It just refused to leave once I let it in.
What’s folded inside you is genuine information, arranged in a deliberate sequence, the way letters are arranged to carry meaning; more tightly packed than any code human engineers have ever written. Musa, peace be upon him, described it perfectly when Pharaoh asked who his Lord was:
“He replied, 'Our Lord is the One Who has given everything its distinctive form, then guided it.'” (Surah Ta-Ha, aya 50)
Allah didn’t pile up raw material. He wrote instructions into our cells, guiding how we’re formed and how we run.
**The city inside a cell**
Each cell is a working city. Microscopic motors called ATP synthase are spinning inside you right now to produce the energy keeping you alive. Protein machines walk cellular highways carrying cargo from place to place, and a papercut you forget within the hour triggers a chain reaction of proteins that either seals the wound or, if one link is missing, leaves you bleeding or clotting in all the wrong places. This isn’t a machine that can assemble itself lazily over time; the system has to work together, or the organism dies.
Bill Gates put it plainly: “DNA is like a computer program but far, far more advanced than any software ever created.” Richard Dawkins, who built a career arguing against religion, opened one of his books with: “Biology is the study of complicated things that give the appearance of having been designed for a purpose.”
Here’s the honest question underneath both of those quotes: has anyone ever seen writing appear without a writer behind it? You could spill ink across a page a thousand times and you’d never produce a sentence. Letters that carry meaning trace back to a mind that arranged them.
The people who read that code most carefully are often the ones it unsettled most. Antony Flew spent over fifty years making the case against God, then changed his mind publicly late in life, pointing to “the almost unbelievable complexity of the arrangements which are needed to produce life” and concluding that “intelligence must have been involved.” He went where the evidence led. Francis Collins, the geneticist who led the first full mapping of the human genome, landed in the same place and titled a book about it *The Language of God*. And it wasn’t only the biologists who got there: Rosalind Picard spent her career at MIT on the science of computing and counted herself an atheist for years, until the evidence in front of her stopped letting her stay one. None of them started out believing. They read the manuscript closely enough, for long enough, that random stopped being a word they could use for it.
## Ibrahim and the things that set
That kind of precision pushes the mind toward one more question: if the world is this clearly built, why do human beings still hand themselves over to things that set, crack, fail, and fall apart? Ibrahim, peace be upon him, grew up under a father named Azar who carved idols and sent his own son out to sell them. The boy who would become one of the greatest prophets spent part of his childhood handing customers gods he already knew were just wood and stone.
Allah tells us He was showing Ibrahim the dominion of the heavens and the earth. Then night covered him, and he saw a star:
“So when the night covered him with darkness, he saw a star. He said, 'This is my lord.' But when it set, he said, 'I like not those that set.'” (Surah Al-An’am, aya 76)
He let the claim of his people have its full hearing. The star disappeared, and he answered it simply: he doesn’t love things that set. The moon rose and he tried again. The moon set too. The sun came up, and he said this one is greater. When the sun went down, the sky had nothing left to offer. He turned to his father and his people with the same test he’d just run on the heavens:
“And remember when Abraham said to his father, Azar, 'Do you take idols as gods? It is clear to me that you and your people are entirely misguided.'” (Surah Al-An’am, aya 74)
His people gathered for a festival and left their idols unattended. Ibrahim stayed behind:
“By Allah! I will surely plot against your idols after you have turned your backs and gone away.” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 57)
“So he smashed them into pieces, except the biggest of them, so they might turn to it for answers.” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 58)
He left the axe resting against the one idol he’d deliberately spared. The town came home to a floor of broken pieces and one intact statue standing over all of it. When they demanded to know who did this:
“He replied sarcastically, 'No, this one, the biggest of them, did it! So ask them, if they can talk!'” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 63)
Something shifted in the crowd right there. The people ready to convict Ibrahim turned on each other:
“So they came back to their senses, saying to one another, 'You yourselves are truly the wrongdoers!'” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 64)
That flash of honesty didn’t survive contact with their pride. Within a verse, they’d gone back to insisting the idols couldn’t speak. Ibrahim pressed it:
“Shame on you and whatever you worship instead of Allah! Do you not have any sense?” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 67)
They reached for fire instead of an answer:
“They concluded, 'Burn him up to avenge your gods, if you must act.'” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 68)
They built the fire and threw a prophet into it to defend statues that couldn’t defend themselves. Allah didn’t send an army. He spoke to the fire:
“We ordered, 'O fire! Be cool and safe for Abraham!'” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 69)
“They had sought to harm him, but We made them the worst losers.” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 70)
Ibrahim was meeting his people inside their own religion and letting it collapse on its own terms: first in the sky, then in their own temple. subhanallah.
We don’t bow to carved idols now. But the test hasn’t gone anywhere. You’ve already seen in the introduction how the engineers of social media deliberately built these platforms to consume your time and conscious attention. Ask what Ibrahim asked the biggest idol left standing: can it answer for itself? Or does someone behind the curtain move it?
## The mind’s barricades
When we apply Ibrahim’s test, the false lords fall away. But the human mind gets uneasy with the demands of submitting to the true Lord; so we build intellectual barricades to protect our independence.
**“Why doesn’t God just show himself?”**
One of the most common is the hiddenness question. The premise assumes He’s hiding. The Qur’an’s answer is a direct promise of nearness, without compulsion:
“When My servants ask you ˹O Prophet˺ about Me: I am truly near. I respond to their prayers when they call upon Me.” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 186)
Belief in the unseen is named earlier in the same surah as the first mark of the God-conscious. An undisputable, forced proof would remove the one variable the test depends on: honest orientation, without compulsion. The evidence is calibrated. Clear enough for someone looking honestly to find it.
**“Who made God?”**
Another barricade: if everything that begins to exist needs a maker, who made Allah?
The Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, knew this whisper would come. Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him, reported: “The Messenger of Allah said, 'Satan comes to one of you and says, Who created this? Who created that? until he says, Who created your Lord?'” (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim).
He also gave the Companions something practical for it: “Some of the Companions came to the Prophet and said, 'We find in ourselves thoughts that are too awful to even speak of.' He asked, 'Do you really find that?' They said, 'Yes.' He replied, 'That is clear faith.'” (Sahih Muslim). An entrenched doubt needs its logic dismantled; a passing whisper your heart already hates just needs to be dismissed, as Imam al-Nawawi put it.
The logical answer is straightforward: the argument only claims that everything that begins to exist needs a cause. The One who is the First, with no beginning at all, needs no cause. Think of a train: each carriage needs to be pulled by the one in front. An infinite chain of carriages that each need pulling never adds up to movement. Somewhere there has to be an engine. When we ask what came before Allah, we’re smuggling time into the question. The One who made the universe made time along with it; He’s not inside the clock waiting for a cause the way we wait for tomorrow.
Allah names Himself this way directly:
“He is the First and the Last, the Most High and Most Near, and He has ˹perfect˺ knowledge of all things.” (Surah Al-Hadid, aya 3)
Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him, also reported: “The Prophet said, 'Whoever finds this in himself, let him say Amantu billah, and let him seek refuge in Allah, and let him stop.'” (Sahih Muslim). Saying 'Amantu billah' asserts what we already know and breaks the spiral. There’s a companion piece to that advice, and it came through the Prophet’s young cousin Abdullah ibn Abbas, may Allah be pleased with him, who grew into one of the most learned scholars among the Companions. He reported: “The Prophet said, 'Think about the creation, and do not think about the Creator.'” (Al-Tabarani and Al-Bayhaqi).
**“Science has explained it all”**
There’s a third barricade, the one loudest in our era: science has explained the universe so thoroughly it leaves no room for God. Think about it. Science tells us how the engine runs. It never reaches why there’s an engine at all. A complete account of how something works says nothing about whether a mind stands behind it. Evolution can describe the way living things change within the world they inhabit. It doesn’t settle why a world with stable laws exists in the first place, or why those laws happen to permit life. It doesn’t explain why matter can carry the kind of information we were just looking at. By the way, I’m not saying science and faith are at war; that conflict narrative is itself a modern invention. Newton wrote more about God and Scripture than he ever wrote about physics. Faraday and Maxwell studied nature as the handwriting of its Creator. Ibn al-Haytham, one of the founding figures of the scientific method itself, wrote that searching for truth in the natural world was for him the surest path to drawing closer to God.
## The hunger of the soul
We were built to worship. The hunger for it is wired in, and it’s bottomless: nothing the size of this world was ever going to fill it.
When we disconnect from the Creator, that hunger doesn’t disappear. It just looks for something else to consume. We take a heart built for the infinite and try to stuff it with finite things. We pour our whole souls into a career or the desperate need to be validated by the crowd. We bend our morals to fit in. Interestingly, we call all of this freedom; but watched closely, it behaves like the opposite.
A human heart is simply too heavy for this world to hold without dropping it. May Allah be pleased with Uqbah bin Amir, a Companion known for his skill in archery and his closeness to the Prophet in his final years. He reported: “The Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said, 'Whoever attaches himself to a thing is left entrusted to it.'” (Jami' at-Sunan at-Tirmidhi). No created thing can carry that weight.
You were made for something your world can’t give you.
“O humanity! It is you who stand in need of Allah, but Allah ˹alone˺ is the Self-Sufficient, Praiseworthy.” (Surah Fatir, aya 15)
That need isn’t a flaw in you. It’s the door He built into you on purpose, so you’d have somewhere to go with it. Remember the air I said I’d always breathed, back when this chapter opened? You’ve spent every page since finding out what was actually holding you up inside it, and He’s been speaking to you the whole time, in every aya you just read. The reasoning is done now. What’s left is whether you’re ready to answer Him.
