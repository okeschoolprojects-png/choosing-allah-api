So here we are, at the last page. I’m not going to spend it trying to win you over one more time. I want to spend it telling you what I think is coming, because you’re going to live through something no Muslims before you ever had to, and I’d rather you walked into it awake.
Here’s what I think is coming. The world’s going to get faster and louder every year you’re alive, and it’s going to get better and better at knowing exactly what pulls you in. Screens will raise children before their parents get the chance to. People will start handing their heaviest questions, the ones about right and wrong and who to give their heart to, to machines that answer in a second and feel nothing when they get it wrong. Your time and attention are going to be the most valuable thing you own, because almost everyone who reaches you through a screen will be trying to buy them.
None of that scares me for you, and I don’t want it to scare you either. Every age that looked like it was swallowing the believers turned out to be the age that made a few of them shine instead. A heart that stays still because it belongs to Allah is going to be the rarest thing in a world built to pull you a hundred ways at once, and the rarer it gets, the more it’s worth. So when everything around you speeds up, remember that you’re allowed to slow down, and that the stillness you keep for Allah is going to be the strongest thing about you.
Our ummah is hurting.
You can see it from your phone. Gaza, where parents dig through their own homes with their hands. Sudan, coming apart while the world barely turns its head. Muslims locked in cells for their faith, and Muslims who lower their voice when they say where they’re from. And then there’s the hurt nobody films. A girl slips her hijab into her bag before school. A boy who can name every player on the pitch can’t remember the last time he opened the Qur’an. Somewhere a father lies awake in the dark, because his daughter just told him she isn’t sure she believes any of this anymore.
Maybe that’s a stranger to you. Maybe it’s you.
For a long time I thought rescue would arrive as a person. Someone important, a leader or a voice, a name that would gather all of us back together while the rest of us waited and watched. It took me years to see how comfortable that idea was, because it let me grieve for the ummah without ever asking what Allah wanted from me.
Here’s what I missed. Victory belongs to Allah alone. He says so plainly:
“Victory comes only from Allah. Surely Allah is Almighty, All-Wise.” (Surah Al-Anfal, aya 10)
It was never going to come from our anger, or from the right man finally showing up. But Allah didn’t leave us standing on the sidelines. In the same breath, He tells us where we come in:
“O believers! If you stand up for Allah, He will help you and make your steps firm.” (Surah Muhammad, aya 7)
Standing up for Allah isn’t a slogan we carry around. The scholars explained it simply: you help His deen. You learn it, you live it, you obey it when it costs you, and you carry it into your home and your work and on to your children. The help is always His to give. He just tied it to something we have to do first, and then He carries the rest.
He told us the same thing another way:
“Indeed, Allah would never change a people’s state ˹of favour˺ until they change their own state ˹of faith˺.” (Surah Ar-Ra’d, aya 11)
That verse has always frightened me a little, and I think it’s meant to. It won’t let me talk about ‘the ummah’ as if it’s somebody else in another country. I’m the ummah. So are you. Your prayer is part of its condition, and so is the way you speak to your mother and the work you do carefully when no one’s watching.
So I’m going to ask you for something, and I want you to feel how much I mean it. Don’t live small. A Muslim was never made to drift through life, taking whatever the world hands him and apologising for his deen in the corner. Allah gave you something. Maybe it’s a sharp mind, or steady hands, or the patience to teach a child everyone else gave up on, or maybe you’re just the one who keeps a whole family standing when things break. Whatever it is, use it, and use it well, because doing your work beautifully is itself part of this religion. Build something true. Keep your word, and leave everyone you deal with a little safer because you feared Allah.
You remember the traders of Hadramawt from earlier in this book. They crossed it on honest scales and kept promises and Qur’an recited out loud in the morning, and whole lands loved the deen because they first loved the people carrying it. That’s still how it works. Your life might travel further than you ever will.
And don’t tell yourself you’re too small for any of this. Renewal in this deen has rarely come from a palace. Ahmad ibn Hanbal was beaten in the street for refusing to say a single sentence he knew was false, and what reached your hands was his patience, long after the men who beat him were forgotten. Ibn Taymiyyah gave us some of what we still lean on from inside a prison cell. Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said:
“Allah will raise up for this ummah, at the head of every hundred years, someone who will renew its religion for it.” (Sunan Abi Dawud)
Maybe you imagine a famous name. But no one has ever renewed this ummah alone. Every one of them needed ordinary people beside them who were willing to learn and to stay when it got hard.
You can’t end the war in Gaza from your bedroom, and I know how helpless that can feel. So do what’s in your hands. Make dua for its people, by name when you know their names, and give whatever you can. Learn enough to speak about them with care. And teach your children that the people of Palestine aren’t a sad clip between two adverts, they’re our family, their mothers are our mothers, their children are ours, and their wounds have a claim on us. Then let the grief do something in you. Let it push you to pray, and to build a family that knows why it’s Muslim. There are people who’d be glad to keep you distracted and a little ashamed of your faith. Don’t make it easy for them.
If you’re seventeen, I know how tired you are of grown-ups talking about your future like you’ve already lost it. You’re still working out who you are. Your friends are into things that make you uneasy. Maybe you carry doubts you’d never say out loud, and you’re worn thin from trying to look normal and religious in the same breath. I’m not going to pretend it’s easy. But you’re a Muslim right at the start of everything, and nobody gets to write you off this early. You’ve got time and strength and a heart that can still be shaped. So take it seriously. Pray even when it feels clumsy, ask your questions out loud, and read the Qur’an for yourself in a language you understand. Find people whose Islam makes you want to be better, and stay close to them.
And if you’re thirty, with a baby asleep down the hall, you already know something the seventeen-year-old doesn’t. Children copy us long before they understand us. They can tell whether we rush through salah, and whether we only mention Allah when we’re scared. They can tell whether the Qur’an lives on a high shelf or gets opened at the kitchen table. You’ll get things wrong, every parent does. Give them the Islam that made you stay. Tell them how merciful He is, let them see you say sorry, and let them catch you making dua for people you’ve never met. Make your home the one place on earth where they can ask the hardest question they’ve got and still feel loved when they’re done asking it.
There’s a short surah I keep coming back to, and it’s the reason I had the nerve to write any of this. It puts faith and good deeds in your own hands, then names one last thing we owe each other: that we spend our lives calling one another toward the truth and holding one another to patience.
“By the ˹passage of˺ time! Surely humanity is in ˹grave˺ loss, except those who have faith, do good, and urge each other to the truth, and urge each other to perseverance.” (Surah Al-Asr, ayas 1–3)
So that’s what I’ve been doing across this whole book, urging you toward what’s true and asking you to hold on. Now it’s yours. Take whatever in these pages was true, urge someone else toward it, and carry it further than I ever could.
There’s a dua I keep making for all of us, and I want you to have it before I go.
I’m asking Him to make us people who serve His deen with the lives we already have, instead of waiting for lives we don’t. To give relief and victory to the people of Palestine, to protect the people of Gaza and bring safety to Sudan, to free the captured and heal the wounded and return the missing to the ones aching for them. To guard the faith of our children, and to make our homes places where the Qur’an is heard, where prayer is loved, where mothers are honoured and fathers are kind, and where nobody has to hide a doubt until it becomes a door they can’t find their way back through. And to put barakah in our small beginnings, the first prayer after a long time away, the first honest word, the little we give when we don’t have much, the habit we finally leave for His sake, and to forgive me for anything in this book I got wrong and keep it from harming a single heart that trusted these pages.
I’m just a boy from Berkane who used to pray Fajr half asleep beside his cousin. I wrote all of this hoping one person I’d never meet might read it and come a little closer to Allah. If that person is you, then every hour this book took was worth it.
So live what these pages got right, and put it into someone else’s hands before your time runs out. It was never meant to stop with you.
And if Allah is kind to us, we’ll meet one day where there’s nothing left to be afraid of, and you can tell me what you did after you closed this book. I’ll be so glad to hear it.
Until then, hold on to Him, and don’t ever let this world talk you into living small.
Wassalamu alaikum wa rahmatullahi wa barakatuh.
