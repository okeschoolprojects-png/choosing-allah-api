*A letter to whoever is holding this book.*
Our scholars opened every book they wrote the same way, and I’m not about to break a fourteen-century chain. So:
In the name of Allah, the Most Compassionate, the Most Merciful. All praise and thanks belong to Him, the One who created us, taught us what we didn’t know, and loved us too much to leave us wandering in the dark. I bear witness that there is no god worthy of worship except Him, alone, without partner, and I bear witness that Muhammad is His slave and messenger, sent as a mercy to people who haven’t even met him yet. I hope from Allah that we all meet him in the Hereafter at his Hawd, ameen. May Allah send His blessings and peace upon him, upon his family, and upon everyone who holds on to what he taught until the Last Day.
Before I write another word, I ask Allah to purify my intention and make this work sincerely for His sake, so that whatever good sits in these pages finds you and takes root long after I’m gone. About the mistakes I’ll make in the pages ahead, let me borrow the words of Ibn Mas’ud, may Allah be pleased with him, who would give his best answer to a hard question and then say: “If it’s right, it’s from Allah, and if it’s wrong, it’s from me and from Shaytan, and Allah and His Messenger are free of it.” That’s the deal between us for this whole book. Everything true in it is from Him. Everything wrong in it is mine, and I ask Him to forgive me and keep it from harming you. I ask Him to soften whatever is hard in you before you reach the first chapter, the way He opened Musa’s chest when he stood in front of Pharaoh with fear sitting on his heart. And if He gives me nothing else, I ask Him to gather me and you one day in a garden where every face is radiant, looking at its Lord.
Alhamdulillah, I’m grateful you’re here. A paper book has to compete with every notification on your phone, every recommended video waiting underneath the one you’re watching, and a hundred other things asking for your eyes. You chose to give these pages some of your time. May Allah bless every minute of it.
For most of my life I wasn’t much of a reader: a novel here, something a teacher assigned there. When I finally started picking up books for myself it was nothing impressive, some James Clear and Simon Sinek I half-believed, a few pop-psychology paperbacks grabbed at airports while I tried to figure out my own head. I’m the last person anyone expected to write one of these. So if someone like me ended up with something urgent enough to put on paper, maybe that tells you what these pages mean to me.
I’m also not a scholar, in case you haven’t yet pieced that together. I haven’t studied fiqh properly or memorized the Qur’an. I grew up in Berkane, in the far northeast of Morocco, between orange trees and a neighbourhood masjid and a family that carried the deen the way most families do. I left at seventeen to study in Belgium, and since then I’ve spent a lot of time watching the gap between what I was handed and what I could explain grow wider and more uncomfortable.
A friend finally forced me to look at that gap. His name is Andreas, a Belgian I met at IMSAL, a mosque in Belgium that opened its doors to the city’s international students. You’d find a brother from Somalia beside a brother from Indonesia, and someone like Andreas, who had grown up nowhere near a masjid, all praying shoulder to shoulder. Sometime in late 2025 he took his shahada. I was around for some of that journey, alhamdulillah.
Then something strange started happening. Andreas came to me with the kind of questions a new Muslim asks because the religion is still fresh and everything still needs explaining. I couldn’t answer some of them. Stranger still, there were things he had learned about Islam that I, born into it, had never known. He had come to the deen as an adult through deliberate reading, and he had gone looking for things I’d never thought to search for. The answers were out there; I just hadn’t worked them out for myself. I knew Islam the way you know your childhood home: so present and familiar that you stop seeing the walls at all.
One question arrived and wouldn’t leave me alone. If I can’t explain this to a grown man who chose Islam with his eyes open, how am I going to explain it to my kids? I believed, yes. I was Muslim, yes. When I looked closely, though, it was half conviction and half habit, a religion I had been handed and never had to earn. I had questions at three in the morning and doubts I didn’t know how to say out loud. I had to work through them with no one handing me a map. That’s the road this book came out of. Read it as an older brother sitting down next to you and saying: “I’ve been down this road. Let me show you what I found.”
I don’t know who you are as you’re reading this. You might be a teenager who got handed this by someone older and isn’t yet sure you want it. You might be a few years past that and tired of being lectured. Perhaps you pray every salah on time. Perhaps you haven’t stood on a prayer mat in months and feel awkward even admitting it to yourself. You may carry questions, or you may know exactly what you believe and only want to feel close to Allah again. Whoever you are, whatever your age, man or woman, certain or struggling: I wrote this with you in mind.
Walk up to a young Muslim today and ask why they believe in Islam. A lot of us will pause before admitting that we don’t really know how to answer. The Yaqeen Institute surveyed Muslims in America and found that 55% had wrestled with at least one serious doubt about the religion. Some of us inherited a name without ever being properly introduced to the One behind it.
The books that can help with this already exist. The trouble is that a young person carrying doubt tonight probably isn’t going to pull volumes of Tafsir Ibn Kathir from a shelf when the language feels as if it’s arriving from another century. I could never do those works justice anyway. Think of this book as a bridge toward them, the same truths carried into the language of someone living in 2026 with a hundred tabs open in his head.
Surah Al-Asr gave me the reason to try. Imam ash-Shafi’i said that if people reflected on this surah, it would be enough for them:
“By the ˹passage of˺ time! Surely humanity is in ˹grave˺ loss, except those who have faith, do good, and urge each other to the truth, and urge each other to perseverance.” (Surah Al-Asr, ayas 1–3)
Being Muslim is the greatest gift Allah has given me. This book comes from gratitude. Knowing Him gives grief somewhere to go and joy someone to thank. It means success has a purpose, and even our worst day is lived under the care of the One who made us. If Islam reached you mostly as warnings and restrictions, I hope these pages show you the gift that was waiting underneath.
To see why that gift sometimes gets lost, we have to look at how we live right now. Let me take you into a normal evening in a Muslim home. Someone’s making tea in the kitchen. Your father is asking who left the lights on again, your mother is telling someone to eat before the food gets cold. Maybe the Qur’an is playing somewhere in the background and maybe it isn’t, but the religion is still there in the room. You feel it in the small things, in the way certain jokes die before they reach your parents, or a cousin lowers their voice when they mention something they shouldn’t have been watching. Nobody has to give a lecture; the walls already know.
Then the evening splits.
You go to your room, close the door, and the whole moral atmosphere changes in the time it takes your thumb to unlock a screen. In your hand is another world, faster and louder, shameless in a way that looks like confidence. It doesn’t ask what your parents taught you or care what your masjid said last Friday. It feeds you clips, faces, arguments and bodies until you can’t tell where the entertainment ends and the education begins.
I’ve watched this inside my own family. A clip that lands on my screen in Belgium can show up on my younger cousins' phones in Berkane before the evening is over. What reaches us isn’t filtered by family or country anymore; an engagement algorithm decides, and the only thing it measures is how many more minutes it can hold our eyes. Sean Parker, Facebook’s first president, admitted in 2017 that the platform was designed to exploit “a vulnerability in human psychology,” and that every like delivers “a little dopamine hit.” The people who built the machine knew what they were doing.
Some of us end up carrying two worlds at once. We may pray on Friday and spend Saturday scrolling through content that treats prayer as a punchline. A question we can’t say at home gets typed into an anonymous account late at night. Without anybody making a grand decision, the values of the feed begin leaking into the way we think. “Do whatever you want as long as you’re not hurting anyone” starts to sound like enough to settle every moral question a human being has ever asked.
That doesn’t mean our families failed us. Many parents carried the deen faithfully through lives much harder than ours, and some understand this world better than we give them credit for. Still, the screen moves faster than a household can. A mother can teach her child for years, and one algorithm can place a stranger in the child’s ear every night without ever knocking at the door. The gap is sometimes a technological one, and sometimes a gap of language: everybody senses that something is wrong, yet nobody knows how to begin the conversation.
The questions change with it. Does Islam oppress women? Isn’t hijab about control? Why does inheritance work the way it does? They’re being asked in Arabic now, in group chats from Cairo to Casablanca, and I take them seriously. We’ll give them the scholarship they deserve later in this book. For now, sit with where they often begin: questions about the rules of a Ruler someone was never introduced to.
The same three words can land differently from two different mouths. Think about it: a stranger grabs your arm at the beach and barks, “Don’t swim here.” You shake him off. Then your father says it, the man who has swum that coast his whole life and knows where the current drags people under, and you stop because you know the hand on your arm is love. When nobody knows the King, every law looks like a cage.
I wrote this book to drag these questions into the open, because silence has never protected anyone. Ibrahim, peace be upon him, confronted the idols of his people where everybody could hear him. Ours hide even better. They live in our pockets, learn what holds our gaze and follow us into rooms where nobody else can see. I’ll keep naming them plainly, then bring the conversation back to the One they are trying to make us forget.
I know where the strain of carrying both worlds can lead. Maybe you’ve seen hypocrisy that turns your stomach, people filling the front row at Friday prayer and cheating their workers on Monday, a culture obsessed with the appearance of religion while its heart goes missing. If Islam has mostly reached you through that hypocrisy, I understand why part of you might want the exit. You may think that if you could put all of this down, stop measuring everything against halal and haram, and live on your own terms, you’d finally breathe. You didn’t arrive there lazily. That deserves an answer, not a lecture.
So we begin where every heart has to begin: with Allah Himself.
The chapters ahead will ask who Allah is, why He’s worth believing in, why the Qur’an is His word, and why Muhammad, blessings and peace be upon him, is His messenger. Later we’ll face purpose and pain, the road home after drifting, and what waits after death. We’ll take it one step at a time.
One thing you won’t get from me is a book of fiqh. The detailed rulings of prayer and fasting matter enormously, and there are scholars far more qualified than I am whose books you should seek out. You and I are dealing with the ground underneath all of that.
If I could leave you only one thing, I’d leave you Him. Health fades, the Rolex rusts, the corner office goes to someone else. The One who made you doesn’t leave or fade, and if you have Him you’re never alone.
Keep reading with your heart open. Bring Him your hardest questions. He isn’t afraid of a single one of them, and neither am I.
Whoever you are, and whatever you’ve done or left undone, please remember me in your duas the way I’ve remembered you in mine while writing every page of this. I’ve asked Allah again and again to let these words reach your heart, even if I never live to see it happen.
Your brother in faith,
Rayan Mokhtari
