*The question I heard long before I could answer it.*
A girl in one of my classes in Belgium said it to me almost kindly, the way you point something out to someone who hasn’t noticed it yet. Your religion oppresses women. She wasn’t trying to start a fight. She said it the way you’d mention it was raining outside.
I didn’t have an answer that day. I had a defensive heat in my chest and no sentence to put under it, which is worse than staying silent. So let me do here what I couldn’t do then.
You’ve met the charge in all its shapes. Islam cages women. Hijab is a man’s idea strapped onto a woman’s head. A daughter is worth half a son. The whole thing was built by men, for men, and stamped with God’s name so nobody could argue back. I’m not going to wave any of that off. Some of it comes from people who can’t stand the deen. Some of it comes from Muslim homes where a woman really was treated as less, with a verse bent out of shape to keep her quiet. Both of those deserve a straight answer, and I’ll try to give one.
The argument almost always skips the first question, though, so let me start there. Before you ask whether Islam freed the woman, ask what the people making the accusation did with her once they had her.
## The freedom with a price tag
The same culture that pities the covered woman built an entire economy out of the uncovered one.
The first market is the mirror. The global beauty and personal-care industry is worth more than half a trillion dollars a year, and its business model is older than any of its products: teach a woman to feel that her own face is a problem, then sell her the correction in monthly installments. When the surface stops being enough, the knife is waiting. The International Society of Aesthetic Plastic Surgery counted close to 38 million cosmetic procedures worldwide in 2024, up more than 40% in four years, the overwhelming majority of them on women. A generation of girls now grows up editing its own face before breakfast and then booking a surgeon to make the edit permanent.
From the mirror the logic moves outward onto the body itself, and it doesn’t hide what it’s doing. A content analysis of American television news found that 62% of segments featured presenters with a high degree of sex appeal, overwhelmingly women, more than half of them in what the researchers called 'suggestive clothing' on air. The restaurant chain Hooters wrote the same thing into its own paperwork, describing the waitress’s look as 'a main selling feature', and a whole imitation industry grew up beside it. Coffee stands across the American Northwest put baristas in bikinis and pulled in close to \$7 billion a year from men alone; when the city of Everett, Washington tried to impose a basic dress code, the owners sued and walked away with a \$500,000 payout after the court struck the rule down.
Before anyone decides this is a Western sickness the East stayed clean of, a review of how women are depicted across Arab media found more than 78% of those depictions were objectifying or demeaning, clustered in exactly the glossy lifestyle programming that sells a presenter’s face and body as the format itself. The market crosses every border the moment a society lets it open.
Then there’s the frontier where the body is sold with nothing in between. OnlyFans reported over \$1.3 billion in creator earnings in a single recent year, and the researchers who study it describe an industry that manufactures the feeling of intimacy and bills for it by the month. A lonely man pays for the sensation of being wanted. A woman is told that renting herself out by the photo is the purest way of owning herself. The same machine is working both of them.
Whatever the brochure promised, the product on the shelf is a body. Islam saw that market fourteen centuries before it went digital and shut the door on it. A woman’s body was never inventory, never a selling feature, never the thing on display: *“and not to display their charms except what is apparent.”* (Surah An-Nur, aya 31)
## Covered, not caged
So let’s talk about the cloth everyone argues over. To a lot of people the hijab is the whole case closed: some man decided a woman’s hair was dangerous and made her hide it. I understand why it looks that way from the outside.
Here’s what that reading walks straight past. In a world that had already decided a woman is worth more the more of her you can see, covering flips the entire premise. It says the parts of me a market wants to price are not for sale, and my face is not an audition. Ask the convert women who put it on as adults, with nobody in their family asking them to and often at real social cost. They don’t describe a cage. They describe taking their own body back from a crowd that had assumed it was theirs to look at.
None of that excuses the homes where the cloth is forced, where a girl is threatened into it and her heart is never once consulted. That isn’t the deen speaking. That’s the same family custom borrowing Allah’s name that does its damage in other rooms too. Allah tied the whole thing to conviction, not to a father’s temper, and a covering worn only to keep the peace at home hasn’t yet reached the place He was actually asking for. The command is His to make. The meaning is hers to put into it.
## The modest fashion trap
Once a culture has decided that a woman’s body is the product, it finds a way to sell even the rejection of that. Enter modest fashion.
In 2015, the global modest fashion market was valued at \$44 billion. By 2025, analysts projected it would reach \$88 billion. Gucci, Nike, Tommy Hilfiger, DKNY, H&M, and Marks & Spencer all launched modest fashion lines. The same industry that spent decades telling Muslim women that their covering made them invisible suddenly discovered they were worth \$88 billion in annual revenue. The hijab wasn’t liberated. It was monetised.
No one illustrated this more honestly than Halima Aden, the Somali-American model who became the first hijabi to appear on the covers of Vogue, Sports Illustrated, and Allure. The story was framed as a win: a covered woman breaking through the most immodest industry on earth. Then in November 2020, Halima walked away from the whole thing.
In a series of posts she explained what the years in the industry had actually cost her. She described shoots where she was handed a hijab that was little more than a head wrap. 'I was so blind in my deen that I would wrap some fabric on and call it a hijab,' she wrote. 'I wish I could go back and be like, that’s not a hijab.' She described the pressure as relentless, quiet, and always dressed up as a compliment: 'It kept shrinking and shrinking. It got to the point where I feel like I was the white man’s fetishised version of me.' She walked away not because Islam failed her, but because she finally listened to it.
This is what the modest fashion machine actually does. It takes the hijab, strips it of its meaning, makes it a statement piece, a branding decision, a market category, and returns it to the wearer with all the taqwa drained out and a price tag sewed in. The hijab was never about cotton and coverage measurements. It was about the relationship between a woman and the One who asked her to wear it. Fashion cannot touch that relationship, but it can replace it with something that looks exactly like it from the outside.
The real question Halima was asking isn’t 'was that piece of fabric long enough?' It is the question Islam has always asked: for whom are you dressing? Allah told the believing women to lower their gaze and guard their chastity, and 'not to display their beauty except what is normally apparent.' (Surah An-Nur, aya 31) When you wake up in the morning and choose what to put on your body, the honest answer to that question tells you everything about where you actually are.
## The anti-hijab squad
If the fashion industry went at the hijab from the outside, a group of Muslim-born women have spent the last twenty years going at it from the inside. Asra Nomani and Hala Arafa published a piece in the Washington Post arguing that the hijab is not a Quranic injunction. Mona Eltahawy, a journalist who called for a 'feminist revolution' in Muslim societies, said that Muslim girls are 'brainwashed' into veiling. Amina Wadud leads mixed-gender prayers as a political statement. Qanta Ahmed, a Muslim doctor, appeared on Fox News to praise the Saudi government for lifting restrictions on women’s dress. Raheel Raza, a Pakistani-Canadian activist, testified before various Western government committees arguing that the hijab is a political symbol of extremism.
This group receive significant media amplification. They are invited onto television panels, quoted in think tanks, and funded by organisations whose interest in the welfare of Muslim women is, at best, selective. Asra Nomani, for example, voted for Donald Trump in 2016. Mona Eltahawy has written that Muslim societies need to be 'liberated' by force if necessary. The pattern is consistent: the women in this group are not arguing for Muslim women’s choices. They are arguing against one specific choice, the choice to cover.
And their argument is weak on the text. Three of the four major schools of Islamic jurisprudence, Hanafi, Maliki, Shafi’i, and Hanbali, hold the covering of everything except the hands and face to be obligatory. The linguistic evidence in the Quranic verse of Al-Nur and the verse of Al-Ahzab has been examined by scholars for fourteen centuries. When the Quranic root 'khimar' was revealed, the women of Madinah immediately took their headscarves and covered their chests with them. Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her, reports the women’s response to the verse directly. The scholarly consensus on the question is not fragile. What the anti-hijab voices are doing is not scholarship. It is politics wearing scholarship’s coat.
The irony is worth sitting with. The same Western media culture that insists Muslim women should be free to take the hijab off never once demands that women be free to keep it on when it becomes inconvenient for an employer, a politician, or a policy. France bans the face veil in public. Belgium and the Netherlands have followed. Quebec passed secularism laws preventing public sector workers from wearing religious symbols. The freedom being offered to Muslim women is always and only the freedom to uncover. The freedom to cover, when it meets the interests of the state or the market, disappears. This is not liberation. It is a different kind of pressure wearing liberation’s language.
## True modesty is an act of worship
Understand this clearly: modesty in Islam is not a dress code. It is an act of worship. The woman who covers because she fears Allah and loves Him is not performing a costume change. She is placing herself before her Lord with every piece of cloth.
Sana Khan was an Indian actress and model, known across Bollywood for years. She was beautiful in the way the industry rewards, she had sponsorships, and she had a following. In October 2020 she announced she was leaving the film industry entirely to dedicate her life to Islam. She married an Islamic scholar. 'I have been so fortunate,' she wrote, 'to find a man who loves me for my deen and not my looks. I will now focus on the love of Allah.'
Zaira Wasim won a National Film Award in India at fifteen. At nineteen she starred alongside Aamir Khan. At twenty-one she walked away from all of it. 'I am not fighting with my identity or my religion,' she wrote in her exit post. 'I have struggled a lot to maintain my \[relationship with faith\] and if today I take a step towards Allah, then I am not wrong. I don’t want to be wrong this time.'
Both of them were told they were wasting their lives. Both of them said, in their own words, that they were finally living them.
This is the thing the outside world cannot see and the market cannot price. A woman who dresses for Allah does not need the approval of a fashion week or a casting director or a government official writing a secularism law. She has already received the only verdict that lasts. The Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, described the best of women as: 'One who pleases him when he looks at her, obeys him when he instructs her, and does not go against his wishes regarding herself and her wealth.' (An-Nasa’i) The world calls that submission. Islam calls it the kind of love that knows what it is submitted to.
Covering is not the whole of modesty. It is the visible edge of something that runs much deeper: guarding the eyes, guarding the tongue, guarding the heart, refusing to reduce yourself or anyone else to a surface. The same deen that covered the woman also told her husband he had no right to harm her body, shame her in public, prevent her from seeing her family, or take what she owned. The cloth and the law came together as a single package. The people who wave the cloth around as evidence of oppression almost never quote the law.
## The rights she had first
The timeline alone should embarrass the accusation. In Britain, a married woman couldn’t legally own property or even keep her own wages until the Married Women’s Property Acts of 1870 and 1882; before that, the law folded her whole legal identity into her husband’s the day she married, and whatever she earned became his. That isn’t the distant past. Those are the great-great-grandmothers of the same society that now lectures Muslims about how they treat women.
Now run the clock back more than a thousand years further. The Muslim woman kept her own name after marriage. Her mahr, the marriage gift, was hers alone and never her father’s. Her earnings stayed her own, with no obligation to spend a coin of them on the household, while the man’s income was pledged to it by law. She inherited in her own right, ran her own trade, accepted or refused a proposal with her own mouth. And in the Arabia that Islam walked into, a woman could be inherited like a piece of the estate when a man died. The Qur’an ended that in a single line: *“O believers! It is not permissible for you to inherit women against their will.”* (Surah An-Nisa, aya 19)
## She was never overlooked
Still, none of that reaches the deepest version of the doubt. A person can concede every right on the list and still carry a deeper fear underneath it: does Allah Himself rank the woman below the man?
There’s a moment in the Prophet’s own household that answers it head-on, blessings and peace be upon him. Umm Salamah, one of his wives and among the sharpest minds in the room around him, noticed something and asked it out loud: why did the Qur’an keep addressing the men? She wasn’t scolded for the question. The answer came down as revelation, writing women into the verse clause by clause until no one could pretend they’d been left out: *“Surely ˹for˺ Muslim men and women, believing men and women, devout men and women, truthful men and women, patient men and women, humble men and women, charitable men and women, fasting men and women, men and women who guard their chastity, and men and women who remember Allah often—for ˹all of˺ them Allah has prepared forgiveness and a great reward.”* (Surah Al-Ahzab, aya 35)
A woman asked where she stood with her Lord, and her Lord answered her by name.
## Different is not a smaller share of worth
I’m not going to pretend Islam hands men and women an identical list. It doesn’t, and you deserve straight talk more than you deserve a comfortable dodge. A son’s inheritance share is often larger than a daughter’s. Testimony is arranged differently in certain financial matters. Leading the congregational prayer falls to the men. Say all of it plainly, without flinching.
Then look at what sits underneath the one people quote most. The larger inheritance share rides on a matching burden: the man is financially responsible for the women in his care, and the woman is responsible for no one’s upkeep yet keeps everything she owns and earns. He inherits more and owes more; she inherits less and owes nothing. Ask yourself which of those two you’d rather sign. A difference in role was never a ranking of souls, and Allah put believing men and women on the same side of the same work, as partners rather than a superior and his subordinate: *“The believers, both men and women, are guardians of one another. They encourage good and forbid evil.”* (Surah At-Tawbah, aya 71)
## The women this deen actually made
If Islam were the cage its critics describe, it produced some strange prisoners.
Khadijah, may Allah be pleased with her, ran her own trade caravans and hired Muhammad before she ever married him, blessings and peace be upon him. She was the one who proposed, and she was the first human being on earth to believe in his message. Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her, grew into one of the most quoted scholars in the history of the religion, correcting senior companions on law and hadith while grown men sat and took it down. Nusaybah bint Ka’b, may Allah be pleased with her, stood over the Prophet at the battle of Uhud with a sword and a shield and took wounds that were meant for him. Centuries of Islamic learning then ran through women teachers so numerous that one modern scholar filled forty volumes doing little more than listing the female hadith masters men traveled across countries to study under.
And the deen set the mother somewhere it set no one else. A man once came to the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, asking who had the most right to his good company. “Your mother,” he said. The man asked again. “Your mother.” A third time. “Your mother.” Only on the fourth did he say, “Then your father.” (Sahih al-Bukhari) He measured a man’s worth by how that man treated the women closest to him: “The best of you are those who are best to their wives, and I am the best of you to my wives.” (Sunan at-Tirmidhi)
So bring the question back to where it started. Isn’t Islam unfair to women? The version worth asking is harder than that one: who gets to decide what a woman is worth? The market has already answered, and it has already put a price on her, by the face and by the following. Islam answers differently, and its answer doesn’t move with the season. Her worth was fixed by the One who made her, before a single stranger ever got a vote.
So to the sister reading this: you were never the thing this deen came to restrain. You were the one it came to defend. And to the brother reading it: she was placed in your life as an honor to protect, not a rank to lord over.
May Allah let every woman who carries this deen feel how far He raised her, guard our sisters and mothers and daughters from every eye that would put a price on them, and gather us in a home where the only standing that ever counted was taqwa. Ameen.
