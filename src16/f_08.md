*What you are here for, and where real contentment is actually found.*
There’s a particular kind of quiet that settles when you’ve cut through everything competing for the heart’s loyalty. You stop dividing. And almost immediately, a question arrives that the noise had been drowning out for years: what are you actually here for? Not the degree, not the career, not any of the smaller versions of that question you’ve been asked a hundred times. The real one, the one sitting underneath all of them, waiting.
Consider how often we are asked what we want to do with our lives. What will we study? What career are we chasing? Which university, which job, which next step? These are good questions, and they deserve real thought. Yet almost no one puts to us the one question sitting underneath all of them: what are we actually for? We are taught to plan a living long before we are ever asked to find a reason to live.
We could answer every one of those smaller questions perfectly and still find the big one untouched. A career people admire, a full diary, a good income. None of them tell us what the whole thing is for. We can climb the entire ladder only to discover it was leaning on the wrong wall.
This isn’t a rare, private struggle. A Gallup survey found that at least one in three Gen Z adults struggle to feel a sense of meaning or purpose in their lives, and a Harvard study of young adults found that 58% had experienced little or no purpose or meaning in the past month alone, with half saying that simply not knowing what to do with their life was hurting their mental health. It’s not only the young, either: the World Health Organization’s Commission on Social Connection found that one in six people worldwide feel lonely, a disconnection linked to an estimated 871,000 deaths a year, roughly a hundred every hour.
People reach the things they were sure would complete them (the award, the title, the wealth) and still feel an emptiness they cannot name. That ache is a sign that we were made for something greater than this passing world. The Qur’an names both sides of the ache: a heart that finds rest only in the remembrance of its Maker, and a life that constricts the moment that remembrance is dropped.
“Those who have believed and whose hearts are assured by the remembrance of Allah. Unquestionably, by the remembrance of Allah hearts are assured.” (Ar-Ra’d, aya 28)
“And whoever turns away from My remembrance, indeed, he will have a depressed life, and We will gather him on the Day of Resurrection blind.” (Ta-Ha, aya 124)
That ache isn’t a problem to medicate away. It points toward two questions that sit underneath every smaller one: why am I here, and where am I going? This chapter answers both, in that order, because the smaller questions about career and achievement only make sense once the big ones are settled.
## Why was I created?
A Muslim can spend years never doubting that Allah exists and still lie awake over a completely different question: why did He create me at all? Why was I never asked? Why did I not get a say in whether I would exist, in what body, into what family, carrying what I would carry?
This question is older than any of us, asked by believers and atheists alike. A philosopher named David Benatar built an entire career at the University of Cape Town arguing a version of it from completely outside any faith: he holds that coming into existence is always a harm, because someone who never existed loses nothing, while someone who exists eventually suffers regardless of how well their life goes. Noticing that you never chose to exist is simply what a thinking mind does when it looks honestly at itself. Decades earlier, the German philosopher Martin Heidegger had already given the feeling a name, geworfenheit, thrownness: the basic condition of finding yourself already existing in a body, a family, a language, and a moment in history you never selected from a menu. Two very different philosophers, neither of them writing about Allah at all, both arrived at the same plain fact believers have always started from: nobody consents to their own beginning.
Consent is something only an existing person can give. Before you were created, there was no you sitting somewhere, weighing the offer, deciding whether to accept a body, a family, a lifetime. Asking why Allah did not consult you first is really asking why He did not put a question to someone who did not yet exist to hear it, because the you capable of answering only came into being at the moment of creation itself. Allah names the same truth about our own beginning directly.
“Does man not remember that We created him before, when he was nothing?” (Maryam, aya 67)
The self was never a passenger sitting somewhere before the body, waiting to be loaded in without a vote. There was nothing. Then there was you.
Underneath the consent question often sits a harder fear: what if I was signed up for a lifetime of hardship without ever being warned? Picture a child born into a war zone, or into a body that will spend its whole life in pain, never having asked for any of it. That fear is fair, and it deserves its own honest answer. It gets one later in this book, in the chapter built entirely around why a good and powerful God allows the hardship that can make existence feel unbearable. What this chapter can answer right now is the part that is already settled: given that we exist, asked or not, what is this existence actually for?
Allah gives the answer directly:
“And I did not create the jinn and mankind except to worship Me. I do not want from them any provision, nor do I want them to feed Me.” (Adh-Dhariyat, aya 56 and aya 57)
Worship here isn’t limited to prayer and fasting. Shaykh al-Islam Ibn Taymiyyah defined it, in his treatise al-'Ubudiyyah, as a comprehensive term for everything Allah loves and is pleased with, from words to hidden and apparent deeds: kindness to your parents, honesty in your work, patience in hardship, even a smile. That is the definition the rest of this chapter works with: the whole of life, returned to the One who made it.
This is a different kind of answer than the one modern ethics usually reaches for. Utilitarian philosophy, going back to Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, argues that the right thing to do is whatever produces the most good or happiness for the most people, and by extension that a meaningful life is simply one that maximizes good outcomes. It’s a serious answer, and it can make someone genuinely useful. But it never actually answers why am I here, only what should I calculate once I am. It gives you a method for scoring actions and stays silent on why there is a scorer at all, or who the final scores are ever reported to. Worship answers the question utilitarianism never asked: not just what produces good, but who all of it is ultimately for.
## When the same act is worship, or isn’t
Take two students who spend every Saturday morning tutoring kids at the same community centre. From the outside, they are doing the identical deed. One is building a line for her college application. The other has nothing on her mind but Allah while she teaches. Strip away the setting and the act carries no weight of its own; the entire value sits in what each of them is actually reaching for while doing it.
The same gap runs through almost everything else you will do this week. The same exam can be studied for out of fear of your parents' disappointment, or studied for as an amanah, a trust placed in you to use your mind well. The same group project can be carried by you because you cannot stand looking lazy in front of classmates, or because you would be embarrassed before Allah to hand in careless work. The same hard conversation with a parent can be softened because you want them to keep thinking well of you, or because the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, named kindness to parents as one of the deeds Allah loves most. None of these acts announce their own worth. You decide it, every time, with what you are actually reaching for while you do them.
I should probably admit this catches me more than I’d like to. Writing something, getting a sentence right, and then noticing, somewhere in the middle of feeling pleased about it, that I don’t know if it was for Allah or for the feeling of having written it well. The question doesn’t come with a clean answer. You just have to keep asking it.
Allah built an entire test around exactly that gap.
“Blessed is He in whose hand is dominion, and He is over all things competent. He who created death and life to test you as to which of you is best in deed.” (Al-Mulk, ayas 1–2)
Ibn Kathir points out, in his tafsir, that Allah did not say He will test who does the most deeds, but who is best in deed. The early scholar al-Fudayl ibn Iyad explained that the best deed is the one that is both entirely sincere and entirely correct: sincere meaning done for Allah alone, correct meaning done according to the Sunnah. One condition fixes the heart to its Lord. The other fixes the form to His Messenger, peace and blessings be upon him.
Allah names the same condition as the entire point of worship itself. The command puts sincerity first (*mukhlisina lahu ad-din*, sincere to Him alone) before it ever names prayer or zakat. Sincerity isn’t decoration added on top of worship. It’s the root the rest of it has to grow out of.
“And they were only commanded to worship Allah, sincere to Him in religion, inclining to truth, and to establish prayer and give zakat. That is the correct religion.” (Al-Bayyinah, aya 5)
Just how much weight that root carries is easiest to see in the deeds that were missing it entirely. It is narrated by Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him (one of the most devoted companions of the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, and the greatest transmitter of hadith) that the Messenger of Allah, peace and blessings be upon him, said: "The first of people whose case will be judged on the Day of Resurrection will be a man who was martyred. He will be brought forth, Allah will make known to him His favors and the man will recognize them. Allah will say: What did you do with them? He will say: I fought for Your sake until I was martyred. Allah will say: You have lied; you fought only so that it might be said, 'He is courageous.' And so it was said. Then it will be ordered that he be dragged on his face and cast into the Fire.
Then a man who acquired knowledge, taught it, and recited the Qur’an will be brought forth. Allah will make known to him His favors and the man will recognize them. Allah will say: What did you do with them? He will say: I acquired knowledge, taught it, and recited the Qur’an for Your sake. Allah will say: You have lied; you acquired knowledge so that it might be said, 'He is a scholar,' and you recited the Qur’an so that it might be said, 'He is a reciter.' And so it was said. Then it will be ordered that he be dragged on his face and cast into the Fire.
Then a man whom Allah made wealthy and gave every kind of property will be brought forth. Allah will make known to him His favors and the man will recognize them. Allah will say: What did you do with them? He will say: I did not leave any avenue You love wealth to be spent on except that I spent on it for Your sake. Allah will say: You have lied; you did so that it might be said, 'He is generous.' And so it was said. Then it will be ordered that he be dragged on his face and cast into the Fire." (Narrated by Abu Hurayrah, related in Sahih Muslim)
The deeds were real and enormous, and still worthless, because the heart behind them was aimed at an audience instead of at Allah. That is the full weight of best over most: a single sincere prayer outweighs a lifetime of impressive ones performed for anyone else.
Psychology ran into the same gap by accident, decades before anyone in the field had a name for it. In 1971 the psychologist Edward Deci had university students solve a puzzle called the Soma cube across three separate sessions. Between the first and second session, he started paying one group for every puzzle they solved and left a second group unpaid. Before the third session, he left each student alone in the room during a short break and watched, through a one-way mirror, what they did with their free time. The unpaid students kept playing with the puzzle anyway. The paid students, once the money stopped, mostly put it down and picked up a magazine instead. Deci had stumbled onto what later researchers named the overjustification effect: pay someone for something they already wanted to do, and the payment does not add to their motivation, it replaces it. Mark Lepper ran a version of the same experiment on children at Stanford in 1973, using felt-tip markers instead of puzzles. Children who were promised a certificate for drawing spent noticeably less time drawing afterward, on their own time, for no reward at all, than children who had never been promised anything. The reward had not made the drawing more attractive. It had become the entire reason for it, and once it disappeared, so did the interest.
Ikhlas describes the identical mechanism from inside the heart instead of a laboratory. The martyr, the scholar, and the wealthy man in that hadith were all working for a reward, and they got exactly the one they were working for: people’s words. Nothing was left over for Allah to pay them with, because they had already been paid in full.
That is precisely what the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, named when he warned the companions about the deed done for someone else’s glance: “What I fear most for you is the lesser shirk. They asked: What is the lesser shirk, O Messenger of Allah? He said: showing off. Allah, mighty and majestic is He, will say to them on the Day of Resurrection, when people are recompensed for their deeds: Go to those for whom you used to show off in the world, and see whether you find any reward with them.” narrated by Mahmud ibn Labid, may Allah be pleased with him (Musnad Ahmad).
So be honest with yourself, not paranoid. You don’t need to interrogate every good thing you do. You do need to ask, every so often: does pleasing this audience matter to me more than pleasing Allah? Would I still do this if no one ever found out?
Two ordinary habits of mind make that question harder to answer honestly than it sounds. The first is what psychologists call social comparison: the habit of measuring your own life by glancing sideways at everyone else’s. You open an app, see someone’s face, grades, wedding, or house, and your own life suddenly feels smaller, though nothing in your room actually changed. Revelation named the same habit, and its cure, fourteen centuries before the term existed.
“And do not extend your eyes toward that by which We have given enjoyment to ˹some˺ categories of them, ˹its being but˺ the splendor of worldly life by which We test them. And the provision of your Lord is better and more enduring.” (Ta-Ha, aya 131)
The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, gave the same cure in his own words: “Look at those who are lower than you, and do not look at those who are above you, for that is more fitting that you should not belittle the favor of Allah upon you.” narrated by Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim).
The second is hedonic adaptation: the thing you thought would finally satisfy you quickly becomes normal, and then you need the next thing. One more achievement, one more compliment, one more upgrade. Ibn al-Qayyim, may Allah have mercy on him, gave this disease its exact diagnosis, and later in this chapter you’ll read it in his own words: the heart carries a poverty that nothing fills but Allah.
The Sunnah even teaches us to ask Allah for protection from the shirk we can see and the shirk we cannot see, because some of this is hidden under good intentions, good aesthetics, or good reputation, and you will not always find it by staring harder at yourself. Abu Musa al-Ash’ari, may Allah be pleased with him (a companion of the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, and a scholar of the Qur’an renowned for the beauty of his recitation) taught these words: “O Allah, I seek refuge in You from knowingly associating partners with You, and I seek Your forgiveness for what I do not know.”
“Indeed, Allah does not forgive that partners be associated with Him, but He forgives what is less than that for whom He wills.” (An-Nisa, aya 48)
That warning does not close the door on the living. As long as a person is still breathing, repentance is open, and Allah calls His servants who have wronged themselves never to despair of His mercy elsewhere in the Qur’an; you will meet that promise in full in the chapter on returning to Him. What matters here is simpler: the two students from the start of this section were never really doing the same deed. One was worshipping Allah. The other was worshipping an application. Nothing about the act told you which was which. Only the heart did.
## Worship is bigger than a prayer mat
After that warning about the shirk hiding inside modern spirituality and the subtler shirk of doing good only to be seen, worship can no longer mean a few religious minutes sprinkled over a mostly separate life. It means the whole life returning to its Owner. The prayer mat is not a corner where Islam begins and ends; it is a training ground. You stand there to remember who you belong to, then you carry that belonging into the classroom, the workplace, the group chat, and the private choices nobody sees.
That’s why Islam feels so complete when someone finally sees it properly. It doesn’t only tell you what to believe on a holy day. It teaches you how to speak when you are angry, how to earn when money is tempting, how to lower your gaze when desire is loud. It teaches you how to honour parents when patience is thin, and what it looks like to enjoy what Allah has given without letting any of it sit on the throne.
This also changes what purpose even means. Purpose isn’t only a grand mission you discover once and then write in a notebook. It is an intention you keep renewing. The same essay, the same job, the same walk to school, the same act of helping a friend, the same meal made for your family can become worship when it is done for Allah and kept within what He loves. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, said exactly this about the most ordinary act of love at home: “You will never spend anything seeking thereby the face of Allah, but you will be rewarded for it, even the morsel you place in your wife’s mouth.” narrated by Sa’d ibn Abi Waqqas, may Allah be pleased with him (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim).
Other cultures have sensed a version of this without revelation to guide them. The Japanese concept of ikigai, literally a reason for being, holds that a life needs an organizing reason or it drifts, and the idea has spread across the world precisely because that hunger is universal. But ikigai asks you to assemble your own reason out of your passions, skills, and what the world will pay for, and leaves you to discover whether the pieces ever really fit. Worship starts from the opposite direction: the reason was given, not built, and it doesn’t depend on finding the right combination of talent and market demand. Whatever you are good at and whoever you become, the reason stays the same.
The scholars used to define worship broadly because the Qur’an and Sunnah define life broadly. Worship includes what is obvious, like prayer, fasting, Qur’an and dua. But it also includes honesty, restraint, mercy, clean money, keeping promises, forgiving when you can, studying with a good intention, and leaving a sin even when nobody would have known. In that sense, ordinary life is not empty space beside worship. It’s often the place where worship becomes real.
None of this is meant to be lived alone, either. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, said: “The believers, in their mutual kindness, compassion, and sympathy, are just like one body. When any limb aches, the whole body reacts with sleeplessness and fever.” narrated by An-Nu’man ibn Bashir, may Allah be pleased with him (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim). Purpose, in other words, was never meant to end at your own front door. Checking on a friend, standing up for someone being wronged, giving your time to your local community, all of it is the same worship carried outward, one limb of the same body caring for another.
So Islam doesn’t ask you to choose between being serious about Allah and being present in your life. It asks you to bring Allah into the life you are already living. Aim high, study well, build things, care for people, enjoy what is halal, and take care of your body and your future. Just keep all of it in service of the purpose, not in place of it. A short, honest check can keep that clear without turning into constant self-interrogation:
- Would I still do this if no one ever knew I did it?
- Am I doing this to please Allah, or to please whoever is watching?
- Does this pull me closer to Him, or further away without me noticing?
- If I stripped away everyone’s opinion of me, would I still choose this?
You do not need a clean answer to all four every single day. You just need to keep asking.
## The pursuit of success
Keeping your intention clean is manageable enough when the act is small: an honest word, a patient silence, a smile given for Allah. It gets much harder when the ambition is large. What about wanting to be genuinely successful, wealthy, and powerful in this world, without ruining your Akhirah (the next life)? That is where a quiet doubt tends to sneak up on ambitious believers: is ambition itself a sin?
This is where the religion gets badly misunderstood. Islam doesn’t ask you to drop your ambitions or live in a cave. Wealth and power aren’t evils in themselves. The companions of the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, included men like Uthman and Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf, who were among the wealthiest merchants of their era. And before any of them came Khadijah bint Khuwaylid, may Allah be pleased with her, the Prophet’s first wife, a woman so established in trade across the Levant that she had employed him before either of them knew what was coming. She became the first Muslim and spent her entire wealth in the service of the deen until she died. What separated all of them from the people who ruined themselves wasn’t the size of their accounts, but where the money sat. It sat in their hands, not in their hearts. 
This is the Prophet’s own example, not a modern compromise with worldly ambition. He was never poor and never had to choose simplicity for lack of means: he owned farmland, part of it rented out and part endowed as waqf, and he had traded goods across the Levant since his youth. He simply preferred not to hoard what he had access to. Uthman and Abd al-Rahman ibn Awf were not exceptions the companions merely tolerated; most of the ten companions promised Paradise in their own lifetimes were traders of real wealth. The Prophet named this as a virtue, not a grudging exception: “How excellent is righteous wealth for a righteous man.” narrated by 'Amr ibn al-'As, may Allah be pleased with him (Musnad Ahmad).
There’s a reason the Qur’an presents Dawud and Sulayman as wealthy and powerful prophets rather than poor and marginal ones, peace be upon them both. Sulayman did not bring an entire kingdom to submission through poverty; Allah placed real wealth and real influence in his hands, and he used both in the service of truth. The same principle holds now: whoever controls resources tends to shape what the rest of us see, hear, and believe. Walking away from wealth on the assumption that it is beneath the faith is not automatically piety. Sometimes it is just handing the loudest microphone in the room to somebody else.
Wealth handled this way also buys something the Qur’an treats as precious in itself: not needing anyone besides Allah. When the Queen of Sheba’s envoys tried to win Sulayman over with a gift, peace be upon him, he sent it back without hesitation:
“When ˹the envoy˺ came to Solomon, he said, 'Are you trying to enrich me? What Allah has given me is better than what He has given you. In fact, it is you who rejoice in this gift of yours!'” (An-Naml, aya 36)
He could not be bought, because Allah had already made him independent of what anyone else had to offer. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, promised a companion his company in Paradise on one condition: “Do not ask anybody for anything.” (Sunan Abi Dawud, narrated by Thawban, may Allah be pleased with him). Provision sought honestly and held loosely does not compete with Allah for the heart’s loyalty. It simply frees a person from bowing to whoever signs the paycheck instead.
Umar ibn Abd al-Aziz inherited more power than almost anyone in his century, as the eighth Umayyad caliph, ruler over an empire stretching from Spain to the edge of India. He had grown up wealthy enough that people said you could trace his path through the streets of Madinah by the perfume left hanging in the air behind him. As caliph, he returned seized lands, closed the gap between the treasury and the people it was meant to serve, and lived so far below what his position allowed that historians still argue about how a man with that much power owned so little. Within roughly two years, his zakat collectors could not find anyone poor enough left to receive it. He held the largest fortune of his age in his hands and let almost none of it settle in his heart.
Wealth held with the right intention also outlasts a single lifetime. For centuries, hospitals, schools, and full-time scholars across the Muslim world were funded not by constant fundraising but by waqf: an asset endowed once, generating income forever, so the mosque or the school never had to ask again. Getting there rarely happens overnight. It tends to run through the same three steps: build a real skill instead of chasing a shortcut, since whatever is made quickly tends to disappear just as quickly; learn under someone who has already walked the road, the way the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, learned to trade under the mentorship of his uncle Abu Talib before he ever traded alone; and be willing to take a long-term, calculated risk instead of clinging to the safety of a guaranteed wage. That risk is meant to rest on tawakkul, not fear. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, said: “If you were to rely upon Allah with true reliance, He would provide for you as He provides for the birds: they leave in the morning with empty stomachs and return in the evening full.” narrated by Umar ibn al-Khattab, may Allah be pleased with him (Sunan al-Sunan at-Tirmidhi). The birds still leave the nest. Reliance on Allah was never a reason to stay in it.
Ambition becomes a sin when the dunya becomes the master rather than the tool. It becomes a sin when we compromise our prayer, our honesty, or our modesty to get a promotion. When our desire for success starts to blur the lines of halal and haram, the ladder is leaning on the wrong wall. But if we seek success to provide for our families, to strengthen the ummah, and to give generously, our ambition is an act of worship. 
The test is simple: if all our wealth and status were stripped away tomorrow, would we still know who we are? If the answer is yes, then go out and conquer the world. Just keep it in our hands.
## The lie that a bit more will finally fill you
Underneath all of it is one quiet promise: just a little more and you will finally feel complete. A bit more money, one more upgrade, the next phone, the next look, the next milestone. We live in a world built on trade-ins, where your things and even the people around you can always be swapped for a newer model. Total credit card debt in the country has climbed past \$1.2 trillion, and close to half of the people carrying that debt say it no longer even feels like a crisis, just the ordinary cost of keeping up. That’s what chasing the next thing actually costs when the chase never ends. You can win every prize the world offers, a Nobel medal, a chief executive’s chair, and still find the heart was never built to be filled by any of it.
Researchers have measured exactly this. In 1978 psychologists tracked down twenty-two major lottery winners and compared them to people who had not won and to a group of accident victims left paralyzed. The winners were not happier day to day than people who had never won anything, and they took noticeably less pleasure in small, ordinary things, a coffee, a joke, a conversation, than everyone else in the study. Decades later the Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman and the economist Angus Deaton studied hundreds of thousands of Americans and found the same ceiling from another angle: day-to-day emotional well-being rose with income only up to a point, and beyond it, more money bought a better answer to how are you doing overall without making people feel any better on an ordinary Tuesday. The mechanism has a name now, hedonic adaptation, but the Qur’an and the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, described the ceiling long before anyone measured it.
Ibn al-Qayyim gave the same diagnosis in an image the scholars have loved ever since. In Ighathat al-Lahfan he describes the dunya as sea water: the one who drinks from it only grows thirstier, and the thirst does not stop until the drinking destroys him. That is the nature of this world when it is treated as an end in itself. It can be used, enjoyed lawfully, and thanked for, but once the heart tries to drink from it as its final source, every sip increases the thirst.
The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, taught that true wealth is not an abundance of possessions but the contentment of the soul; this is in Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim, narrated by Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him. Centuries later Ibn al-Qayyim described the same truth from the inside. In Madarij as-Salikin he writes: in the heart is a disarray that nothing gathers but turning to Allah, a desolation that nothing removes but intimacy with Him, a sorrow that nothing lifts but the joy of knowing Him, and a poverty that nothing fills but Him. Make anything smaller than Him your everything, and it collapses under the weight.
Some companions of the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, knew this so fully that they walked away from everything the world admires. Take Mus’ab ibn Umayr, may Allah be pleased with him. He had been the most perfumed young man of Makkah, the best-dressed, the one mothers pointed to and said, look at the son of Umayr. When he chose Islam, his mother withheld food from him and his people withheld their wealth. He left it all. At the battle of Uhud he was martyred, and when they came to shroud him, his cloak was too short to cover both his head and his feet at once; if they covered his head, his feet showed, and if they covered his feet, his head showed. The scene is preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari, narrated by Khabbab ibn al-Aratt, may Allah be pleased with him, who stood among the companions that day. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, stood over him and remembered what he had left behind. A life that began in silk ended in a cloak that did not even reach his ankles, and he is among the most envied young men in our history. The world had offered him what it offers everyone; he had simply seen what it actually pays.
## Where are we going?
One of the two questions this chapter opened with already has an answer: we are here to worship Him, understood in the widest sense that word can carry. The second is the one most people spend a lifetime avoiding: where is all of this actually headed?
Steve Jobs spent his life building things the whole world wanted. At the very end of it, his sister described him looking first at his sister Patty, then for a long time at his children, then at his wife Laurene, and then past all of them at something no one else in the room could see, and saying only, three times, oh wow, oh wow, oh wow. Nobody knows what he saw. What his last moment shows plainly is that the destination question does not wait politely for people who never got around to answering it. It arrives whether or not you were ready, for the richest man in the room exactly as much as anyone else.
Every civilization has tried to answer that by looking at the wrong horizon. Build the pyramid, found the empire, publish the book, raise the family name, and something of you survives a little longer than you do. The Qur’an does not deny that legacies exist. It simply insists that a legacy is not the destination, only a longer road.
“Every being on earth is bound to perish. Only your Lord Himself, full of Majesty and Honour, will remain ˹forever˺.” (Ar-Rahman, aya 26 and aya 27)
It can sound like a bleak verse at first. Read closely, it is a compass. If everything on this earth is passing, then a life spent trying to build a permanent home out of it is aimed at the wrong target, however comfortable the target looks along the way. The Qur’an calls this world exactly what it is, a temporary provision, and sets it directly against what actually lasts.
The Qur’an reaches for two different words. This life is mata', provision for the journey: the food and water you pack for a trip, useful and even enjoyable, but never the destination itself. The next life is dar al-qarar, the home of settling down, the place the journey was actually for.
“O my people! This worldly life is only ˹a fleeting˺ enjoyment, whereas the Hereafter is truly the home of settlement.” (Ghafir, aya 39)
A traveller who forgets which is which will spend the whole trip decorating a rest stop and arrive at the real house with nothing prepared.
This is the direction of every single life without exception, believer and disbeliever, rich and poor, remembered and forgotten, not a private, individual drift.
“To Him is your return all together. Allah’s promise is ˹always˺ true.” (Yunus, aya 4)
Nobody opts out of the destination. The only thing anyone actually chooses is the direction they travel in and the condition they arrive in. The Qur’an uses a specific word for this journey, kadh: a restless, driven labor that doesn’t pause, that every human life is already doing whether they intend it or not.
“O humanity! Indeed, you are labouring restlessly towards your Lord, and will ˹eventually˺ meet the consequences.” (Al-Inshiqaq, aya 6)
The executive grinding toward a corner office and the worshipper spending the last third of the night in prayer are both walking toward the same appointment; they are simply walking in different postures, and they will not arrive in the same condition. That is what makes the first question of this chapter matter so much. What you are here for tells you what you are meant to be carrying when you arrive, and where you are going tells you that the road is not a circle and it does not simply fade out. It has a destination, and the destination has an Owner, and the Owner already told you His address.
This chapter will not walk through what that meeting actually contains. The grave, the resurrection, the scale, and the two homes waiting on the other side of it each get their own full chapter later in this book. What matters here comes first: the direction of travel. A person convinced that life is a closed loop ending in nothing organizes every decision around this world, because this world is supposedly all there is. A person who knows the road has a destination organizes decisions differently, the way a traveller who is actually going somewhere packs differently than one who believes they are only walking in circles. The ladder metaphor from the start of this chapter finishes here: the ladder itself was never the building, only ever the way up to a door, and which wall it leaned on was always the smaller question.
## What the world calls happiness
The world tends to describe happiness as a feeling: a high, a buzz, whatever lifts us in the moment. By that definition happiness is fragile, because feelings rise and crash and the chase never ends. Islam speaks of something steadier, what the Qur’an calls sakina and tuma’nina, a settledness of the heart that does not depend on the next good thing happening. It’s less like a firework and more like solid ground, and it tends to arrive not when you aim straight at it, but as a by-product of living for what you were made for. That is the same verse this chapter opened with: only in the remembrance of Allah do hearts find rest. It was true on the first page. Everything since has just been the proof.
Allah describes exactly what becomes of a heart that settles there:
“O reassured soul, return to your Lord, well-pleased and pleasing ˹to Him˺, and enter among My ˹righteous˺ servants, and enter My Paradise.” (Al-Fajr, ayas 27–30)
The scholars knew this long before the modern self-help shelf. Imam al-Ghazali devoted an entire work, Kimiya as-Sa’ada, the Alchemy of Happiness, to the idea that real happiness is the knowledge of Allah, that the heart was created to know Him and is restless until it does. Ibn Taymiyyah, pressured and even imprisoned for his faith, said something his student Ibn al-Qayyim preserved for us in al-Wabil as-Sayyib: my garden and my paradise are in my chest; they go wherever I go; my killing is martyrdom, my imprisonment is solitude with Allah, my exile is travel; what can my enemies truly do to me? A heart anchored in Allah carries its own light into any room, even a prison cell.
## The secret to a happy life
Contentment does not come from cramming life with more, but from balance. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, taught that balance directly when three companions came to his house and were told about his worship. One said he would pray every night and never sleep, one said he would fast forever and never break his fast, one said he would never marry. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, came out and answered them in one sentence: “By Allah, I am the most fearful of Allah among you, and the most mindful of Him; yet I fast and I break my fast, I pray and I sleep, and I marry women. So whoever turns away from my Sunnah is not of me.” narrated by Anas ibn Malik, may Allah be pleased with him (Sahih al-Bukhari).
So keep your prayer, make dua and pour your heart out to Allah, learn the seerah, and build taqwa, a living awareness of Allah in everything you do. Ibn al-Qayyim mapped the design of a heart aimed at Allah in his writing: love as the engine pointing you toward Him, fear as the guardrail that stops you drifting into carelessness, hope as the thing that stops you collapsing into despair. Lose either of the last two and the whole thing goes wrong. Everything this chapter has described, worship, sincerity, ambition kept in its place, contentment that doesn’t chase the next thing, is that balance held across an entire life.
Remember now the career questions we set aside at the beginning. They were not the wrong questions; they were the right questions asked in the wrong order. Once Allah is at the centre, the definition of worship you met earlier in this chapter, Ibn Taymiyyah’s, comes back to claim the rest of your life: the degree, the job, and the ladder are all very much in scope. And what decides whether your hours count as worship is the same thing the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, named in the first hadith every student of knowledge meets: “Actions are but by intentions, and every person will have only what he intended.” narrated by Umar ibn al-Khattab, may Allah be pleased with him (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim).
With intention, your studies become an obligation to your community fulfilled, your job becomes provision sought lawfully and spent generously, your gifts become a trust used to help people Allah loves, your ordinary days fill up with what they were always meant to carry. Keep them all, and aim as high as you like. Just do not let them sit on the throne; let them serve the purpose instead of replacing it. Our career was always meant to be part of the answer, not the answer itself; with Allah at the centre, the same ladder we opened this chapter with is now leaning on the right wall. That is the life the next words point to, and the reward waiting at the end of a road built on Him.
And the reward at the end of this road is beyond anything the world can offer: gardens, peace, and above all the pleasure of Allah Himself.
“Allah has promised the believing men and believing women gardens beneath which rivers flow, wherein they abide eternally, and pleasant dwellings in gardens of perpetual residence; but approval from Allah is greater.” (At-Tawbah, aya 72)
The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, described a scene in which Allah Himself speaks to the people of Paradise, asks them whether they are pleased, offers them something greater than every gift they have already been given, and grants them His pleasure, telling them He will never be angry with them after it, forever: “Allah will say to the people of Paradise: O people of Paradise. They will say: Here we are, our Lord, at Your service. He will say: Are you pleased? They will say: Why should we not be pleased, when You have given us what You have not given to anyone of Your creation? He will say: Shall I not give you something better than that? They will say: O our Lord, and what is better than that? He will say: I shall bestow My pleasure upon you, and I shall never be angry with you after it, forever.” narrated by Abu Sa’id al-Khudri, may Allah be pleased with him (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim).
You know what you’re here for. That’s not the hard part. The hard part is feeling the weight of it in your chest instead of only holding it in your head. The distance between the two has a simple cause: a debt you can’t see is a debt you never feel pulled to repay. The debt I want you to sit with is the largest you’ll ever carry, precisely because no one has ever handed you a bill for it.
Before the book asks anything more of you, it asks you to do something harder than being argued into belief, which is work already behind us. It asks you to notice. The favors of Allah are not mainly the dramatic rescues and answered prayers you happen to remember. They are the vast, constant gifts you’ve been standing inside your whole life without once turning to look at them. What matters here is where those gifts have been pointing all along, back to the Giver.
## The favor you never think to ask for
Arabic keeps two different words for what Allah gives, and the distance between them is the whole subject of what remains of this chapter. *Ni’ma* is the blessing you notice, the raise or the answered prayer, the thing you’d name if someone asked what you were grateful for today. *Ala* names something deeper and easier to miss: favors so woven into your existence that you would never think to ask for them, because you’ve never once imagined life without them. The sun at the exact distance that neither freezes you nor burns you. Water being wet, air being free. A mother’s milk arriving the same week her child is born hungry. *Ni’ma* is the favor you notice; *ala* is the favor you live inside so completely that noticing it takes effort. Almost the whole work of gratitude is spent learning to see the second kind.
And there’s a reason the second kind is hard to see. It’s built into the machinery you perceive the world with. Your nervous system is designed to stop reporting anything constant. You feel your shirt the instant you put it on, then not again all day. A smell that floods a room fades within minutes, though the molecules are still there. Neuroscientists call this habituation. It is a mercy in its own right, because a brain that never tuned out the steady background would drown in noise. But it carries a spiritual cost: the favors that never switch off, the ones most worth being grateful for, are exactly the ones your own biology is built to erase. You’ll notice a headache for an hour and fail to notice a painless body for a decade.
This is why gratitude, in Islam, is not a mood. It’s a discipline that pushes against your own wiring. The psychologist Robert Emmons spent years documenting that people who deliberately keep account of what they’ve been given sleep better, report fewer physical symptoms, and are more satisfied with their lives than those who don’t. Revelation had named the same truth fourteen centuries before the laboratory, and unlike the laboratory, it also named the One gratitude was reaching for.
## The favors written into an ordinary morning
The thickest, least-noticed favors are the body you woke up in this morning. You did nothing to earn it and can’t, for the most part, even feel it working. Hearing, sight, and the intellect to make sense of this very sentence were all installed before you knew anything at all, before you could possibly have asked, and the purpose of that gift was stated outright. Most of us have spent decades using all three and never once thanked Him for a single one of them: *“And Allah brought you out of the wombs of your mothers while you knew nothing, and gave you hearing, sight, and intellect so perhaps you would be thankful.”* (Surah An-Nahl, aya 78)
Ubaydullah ibn Mihsan al-Khatmi, a companion of the Prophet, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “Whoever among you wakes up physically healthy, feeling safe and secure within himself, with food for his day, it is as if he were given the entire world.” (Sunan al-Sunan at-Tirmidhi) Read that as an inventory of what you woke up already holding today and never once petitioned for: a body that works, a place safe enough to have slept in, something to eat before nightfall. The Prophet calls that the whole world. Most of us step over it on the way to complaining about something else.
Then widen the frame past your own body to the world laid out to feed it. Food could have kept you alive without tasting like anything. Grain didn’t need a scent, and dates didn’t need to arrive already sweet and folded inside their own sheaths. There is a whole category of favor here that runs past what bare survival required, into what only a Giver who wanted you to enjoy being alive would think to include. *“He laid out the earth for all beings. In it are fruits, palm trees with clustered ˹dates˺, and grain with husks, and fragrant plants.”* (Surah Ar-Rahman, ayas 10–12)
## The favor of being spoken to
There is one favor greater than all of these, and Surah Ar-Rahman, the only surah in the whole Qur’an named after one of Allah’s own names, names it first, before a single mountain, sea, or fruit. Before the sun and the moon, before the earth and its fruit, comes the Qur’an itself, then the faculty that lets you receive it. The first favor the Most Compassionate lists is not something you eat or hold. It’s that He spoke to you at all, and made you the kind of creature that could hear Him: *“The Most Compassionate taught the Quran, created humanity, ˹and˺ taught them speech.”* (Surah Ar-Rahman, ayas 1–4)
This exposes the assumption sitting underneath a lot of our ingratitude: the sense that we somehow had all of it coming. We didn’t. You did nothing to be born where a call to prayer was already in the air rather than never reaching you, nothing to be handed *la ilaha illallah* as a child while millions live and die without hearing it put clearly even once. You didn’t select your parents or the century you were born into. That is the answer to a thought that troubles sincere people: *why do I have what others don’t?* Guidance, safety, a sound body, enough, none of it was a wage you earned and they failed to. It was given. And a gift you didn’t earn is not something to feel superior about. It is something to feel responsible for. The right response to being handed more than you deserved is not guilt and not pride, but a question: what am I now meant to do with all of this?
## The favors you could never finish counting
At some point the mind stops trying to itemize and simply concedes the scale of it. The Qur’an ends that concession with a verdict, and the Arabic of that verdict matters. The word is *kaffar*, and it shares its root with *kufr*, disbelief. That overlap is not loose. In Arabic the root k-f-r means, at bottom, to cover something over, the way a farmer buries a seed under the soil until it vanishes from sight. To disbelieve is to bury a favor so completely you forget it was ever a gift. Ingratitude and disbelief, in this language, are the same motion of the heart, aimed at different objects: *“And He has granted you all that you asked Him for. If you tried to count Allah’s blessings, you would never be able to number them. Indeed humankind is truly unfair, ˹totally˺ ungrateful.”* (Surah Ibrahim, aya 34)
This is why Surah Ar-Rahman does something no other chapter of the Qur’an does. After nearly every favor it names, one line returns, thirty-one times across a single surah. It is not mere rhythm. After each favor is set down, Allah turns the question directly onto you: *this one too? And this one? Are you going to bury this one as well?* The favors are *ala*, the kind your own biology is built to erase, and the refrain is revelation refusing to let the erasing happen in silence: *“Then which of your Lord’s favours will you ˹humans and jinn˺ both deny?”* (Surah Ar-Rahman, aya 13)
## From the gift to the Giver
Every favor in this chapter has been doing the same thing: pointing past itself. A gift is never only an object. It is a message about the one who sent it. The deepest failure available to a human being is not to refuse the gift but to take it greedily and never once look up at the hand extending it, to love the sunlight and forget the sun.
There is a reason gratitude with no one to thank feels incomplete, almost unbearable. The poet Dante Gabriel Rossetti described the worst moment in an atheist’s life as the one in which they feel genuinely, overwhelmingly grateful and have absolutely no one to thank. G.K. Chesterton, who quoted him, added that all goods look better when they look like gifts. The instinct is right. Gratitude is built to travel toward a person. Aimed at “the universe,” it hangs in the air with nowhere to land. Islam simply gives the instinct its address. The favors were never the destination. They were the road, and the road ends with Him.
If goodness this total has been poured over you your whole life, what could a fitting response possibly be except goodness returned? That is why the surah of favors, deep in its length, asks the question that turns receiving into worship: *“Is there any reward for goodness except goodness?”* (Surah Ar-Rahman, aya 60)
This is where gratitude and purpose meet. You know what you’re here for, a life given back to Allah. This chapter tells you why you would want to give it: because He gave first, and gave without pause, and gave what you’d done nothing to earn. Gratitude that stays a warm feeling is not yet gratitude. The Arabic *shukr* is something you do with your limbs and your choices, not only something you feel. Aisha, wife of the Prophet, may Allah be pleased with her, reported that she found the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, standing in prayer until his feet had swollen. She asked him why, when Allah had already forgiven his past and future sins. He said: “Should I not be a grateful servant?” (Sahih al-Bukhari) To truly see the favors is to owe Him your life, and to owe it gladly.
And a Giver this attentive, who fitted your eyes before you could ask for sight, who wrapped flavour around food that only needed to be fuel, who spoke to you when He could have left you in silence, raises one last question, the one the next chapter has to answer. Would a Maker who gave all of this, down to the smell of rain, really hand you a whole life and then leave you completely alone to guess what it was for?
