*When the room gets colder the moment Islam enters it.*
The lie from the last chapter doesn’t only tell you to sin. Sometimes it tells you to be embarrassed that you’re Muslim at all.
I know that feeling more than I’d like to admit. In Berkane, Islam sat in the air. The adhan, Ramadan, older men outside the masjid after Maghrib, my mother saying bismillah before some ordinary task nobody even thought to call religious. Then I moved to Belgium and felt something split. The same deen that was background at home could suddenly feel like a thing you had to explain, defend, or keep folded up until you got back to your room. You start noticing when a conversation about religion changes temperature. You notice which words make people smirk. You notice how easy it is to become two people: the one who says alhamdulillah without thinking, and the one who edits sentences in public so nobody decides you’re one of those Muslims.
If you’ve felt that split, I’m not surprised. A 2025 EU report found 4 in 10 Muslims in Europe experienced discrimination in a single recent year. And sometimes the atmosphere turns violent faster than anyone expects. In Britain, a 2024 mass stabbing was falsely pinned online on a Muslim immigrant. The lie spread faster than the correction, and anti-Muslim riots followed. You don’t need to imagine what digital slander can become anymore. We have the case study.
But the pressure on your iman doesn’t only come from strangers. Some of it comes from family culture, from national identity, from the old sentence that says, this is just how we do things, even when Allah and His Messenger say otherwise. That is the chapter I care about here. Not Islam versus the West in the abstract. Islam versus every rival claim that tries to tell you who you are before revelation gets a word in.
## When Islam becomes the awkward thing in the room
Mockery doesn’t always arrive as a big speech. Sometimes it’s a joke you are expected to laugh at. Sometimes it’s the look on someone’s face when you say you don’t drink. Sometimes it’s a teacher talking about religion as if faith is what clever people eventually outgrow. Sometimes it is subtler than all that, the assumption that a serious Muslim must also be backward, angry, or scared of freedom.
Allah did not leave you to invent a reaction every time this happens. He told His Messenger, blessings and peace be upon him, something every believer still needs when the room turns on them: *“Surely We will be sufficient for you against the mockers,”* (Surah Al-Hijr, aya 95). That is not only comfort. It is perspective. Their laughter is not sovereign. Their approval is not the air supply of your life.
And when mockery turns into a gathering, a table, a group chat, a room where Allah’s verses are being treated like a toy, the Qur’an gets more practical: *“And when you come across those who ridicule Our revelations, do not sit with them unless they engage in a different topic. Should Satan make you forget, then once you remember, do not continue to sit with the wrongdoing people.”* (Surah Al-An’am, aya 68)
That aya matters because a lot of us think staying quiet is neutrality. It isn’t. Sometimes leaving is the cleanest form of self-respect you have.
I’m not saying every awkward moment demands a dramatic exit. Wisdom still matters. Context still matters. But the default cannot be that you sit there smiling while the deen that gives your life meaning gets treated like a costume from a less enlightened century. Some rooms do not deserve your performance of being unbothered.
## Inherited doesn’t mean fake
There’s another pressure that lands harder because it sounds thoughtful. You grew up Muslim, so how do you know any of this is yours? Maybe you’re only carrying your parents' faith, your country’s habits, your childhood vocabulary.
The question is fair. What isn’t fair is pretending other people built themselves from scratch. The secular student in Brussels inherited a world too. The nationalist inherited a flag. The liberal inherited a moral atmosphere. The person who says “I’m just following my truth” usually means the truth of the culture that raised them.
Inheritance is not the problem. Unexamined inheritance is.
The Qur’an is precise here. When some people said they had believed, Allah corrected them: “The bedouins say, ‘We have believed.’ Say, ‘You have not yet believed. But say, “We have submitted,” for faith has not yet entered your hearts.’” (Surah Al-Hujurat, aya 14)
That verse is not there to humiliate the inherited Muslim. It is there to wake them up. A label is not the same thing as iman. A Muslim name is not the same thing as surrender. You can inherit the door. You still have to walk through it.
That is why some people raised inside Islam wake up at nineteen or twenty and realize they’ve never chosen any of it consciously. And that is also why the rediscovery can be stronger than the first inheritance. French political scientist Olivier Roy argues that Muslims living as minorities in the West often end up with a more deliberate Islam because culture stops carrying the deen for them. They have to study, choose, and define it on purpose. I have felt some of that myself. In Berkane, plenty of things were simply there. In Belgium, I had to ask what was culture, what was revelation, and what I was actually willing to stand by once nobody handed it to me for free.
So no, being born Muslim does not make your Islam fake. It only means you were given a mercy at the start. The work afterward is to turn that mercy into conviction.
## When family custom borrows Allah’s name
Some of the hardest collisions do not happen in classrooms or online. They happen at home.
A practice can sit inside a family for so long that nobody remembers when it stopped being culture and started being treated like deen. A daughter’s worth gets tied to appearances. A son is excused for things a daughter would be shamed for. Marriage decisions become about prestige before piety. A custom survives mainly because elders handed it down, and that fact alone is treated like proof.
The Qur’an does not let ancestral habit carry that authority. *“When it is said to them, ‘Follow what Allah has revealed,’ they reply, ‘No! We only follow what we found our forefathers practicing.’ Would they still do so even if Satan is inviting them to the torment of the Blaze?”* (Surah Luqman, aya 21)
That aya lands harder than people think, because it doesn’t only speak to pagan ancestors. It speaks to every inherited pattern that tries to outrank revelation.
This is where honesty gets expensive. You may have to admit that some things you grew up calling our way were never Islam’s way. And when that recognition comes, justice doesn’t permit a double standard that spares your own side. Allah says: *“O believers! Stand firm for justice as witnesses for Allah even if it is against yourselves, your parents, or close relatives. Be they rich or poor, Allah is best to ensure their interests. So do not let your desires cause you to deviate from justice.”* (Surah An-Nisa, aya 135)
Against yourselves. Against your parents. Against close relatives. That is not a verse for abstract courtrooms only. It is a verse for the family table too.
Notice what the Qur’an does not ask from you here. It does not ask you to become smug, rude, or theatrically rebellious. It asks you to tell the truth about who gets the final word. There is a difference between honoring your parents and granting culture divine status. Islam commands the first. It breaks the second.
## The loyalties that compete for you
Once you see that culture can overreach, another question opens. If Islam is supposed to be the deepest layer of who I am, why do so many other identities feel louder?
Nation. Ethnicity. Class. Friend group. A city. A scene. A politics. Sometimes even the wounded pride of being the kind of Muslim who is always arguing online.
Arab nationalism is a clear case of this substitution being built on purpose. In the last century it was openly fashioned as an alternative identity, one that united people by language and ethnicity rather than by the ummah. Nasserism did it. Ba’athism did it. The point was not only to organize states. It was to teach people to feel their deepest loyalty somewhere other than revelation.
That same pressure still shows up in softer forms. A Moroccan can start acting as if Moroccanness itself settles the argument. A European Muslim can start performing a permanently defensive version of Islam shaped more by the host country than by the Qur’an. A diaspora child can end up with the worst of both worlds, cut off from the warmth of one culture and unconsciously absorbing the moral instincts of another.
You can see some of that in the numbers. A 2025 meta-analysis found second-generation immigrants in Europe have significantly higher rates of drug and cannabis use than first-generation immigrants, with higher alcohol use specifically among women. The researchers tie the pattern to acculturation into host-country norms. The children didn’t necessarily stop believing all at once. They started absorbing all at once.
That is why this chapter is not about hating culture. Culture can carry language, food, manners, humor, local memory, and beautiful forms of hospitality. Islam leaves room for all of that. The question is simpler. What happens when culture asks for a loyalty it does not deserve?
If you do not answer that question early, the room answers it for you.
## The script of the modern self
There is another script competing for you, and it arrives in so many different voices that it can be hard to recognise it as a single script. It says: find yourself. Try things. Follow your desires and see where they lead. Your identity is something you build from the inside out, and no one, not your parents, not your religion, not your culture, has the right to tell you who to be. You are the author of yourself.
It sounds like freedom. It is supposed to. But follow the logic to the end. If desire is the guide, then the loudest desire wins. And the loudest desire is rarely the one that leads somewhere good.
We are taught from a young age that sexuality and gender are the most critical aspects of our identity. The modern world insists that you are whatever you feel, and that true happiness only comes when you act on every internal pull. Islam sees the human being differently. When Allah drew limits around our desires, He was not restricting joy. He was protecting us from the specific harm that follows when there are no limits.
A Muslim does not hate people who struggle with desires or experience same-sex attraction. Islam emphasises loving people far more than shaming them. But love does not mean endorsing what harms the soul. If we genuinely care about people, we explain that Allah created us with a design, and provided marriage to fulfill the need for love and intimacy.
The same is true for the modern claim that gender is merely a social construct, available for each person to redefine. Humanity was divinely created male and female, physically, biologically, and emotionally distinct, each with their own noble duties.
To the person reading this who may be experiencing desires that conflict with the deen, this is not written against you. Allah knows what He placed in you before you were born. The test He gives different people looks different. What you feel is not chosen, but what you do with it is. Desires do not define a person, and conquering urges contrary to our faith is exactly how piety is established.
The real question underneath all of this is simple: who gets to tell you who you are? The social media feed? The algorithm measuring what makes you feel worst about yourself? Or the One who created you, before you had a name to answer to?
In Islam, your desires are not your defining feature. You are a servant of Allah. The script says: find yourself. Islam says: you were already found, before you were born, by the One who made you. The question is not who you are. The question is whether you will return to the One who always knew.
## Being Muslim in your own skin
A healthy answer is plainer than people think. You do not need to apologize for every command before you have even named it. You do not need to turn your deen into a debate performance for whoever happens to be in front of you. And you do not need to run every aya through modern therapy language or liberal politics before letting it stand as revelation.
You also do not need to pretend the pressure is imaginary. It isn’t. I’ve felt it in Belgium. I’m sure you’ve felt your version of it wherever you are. The point is not to deny the pressure. It is to stand inside it without handing over your center.
The Qur’an gives the center in one line: *“They say, ‘If we return to Medina, the honourable will definitely expel the inferior.’ But all honour and power belongs to Allah, His Messenger, and the believers, yet the hypocrites do not know.”* (Surah Al-Munafiqun, aya 8)
All honor belongs to Allah. Not to the room that clapped for you. Not to the country that tolerated you. Not to the family custom that kept you acceptable. Not to the feed that made you legible.
That changes a person.
You stop begging rival identities to certify you. You stop acting as if Islam needs a final stamp from modern culture before you can stand upright with it. You can admit when Muslims are wrong without concluding Islam is wrong. You can love what is good in a people without borrowing their worldview whole. Dignity is plainer than swagger. It prays. It learns. It refuses what Allah forbade even when nobody would have found out. It treats people well without dissolving into them.
I don’t think the goal is to feel no friction. Maybe some of that friction is part of the honor. If the world around you keeps trying to author you from the outside, maybe one of the most beautiful things Islam does is return you to the One who already knows what you are.
So if you have been feeling split, between Berkane and Belgium, between the masjid and the classroom, between family custom and Allah’s speech, between wanting to belong and wanting to stay clean, do not mistake that split for proof that the deen doesn’t fit you. It may be the beginning of you finally wearing it on purpose.
## When the whole ummah is in the room
I used to hear the sentence before I could answer it. Growing up in Berkane, then watching Europe from a student flat in Belgium, you don’t need a debate club to run into it. Someone points at a hospital wing, a clean train line, a research lab, a court that mostly works, and then they point at a city they associate with us, and they ask the question like it’s already settled.
If Islam were true, why would Muslims be the ones lagging?
You’ve heard a sharper version of it too. They point at the skylines and the patents, at who produces the medicine and who imports it, at who sets the standards and who chases them. If this deen were from Allah, wouldn’t the people who claim it be the ones leading the world?
I want to take that doubt seriously, without dodging it or turning it into a slogan. A lot of us have felt the weight of it in our own chest before we ever heard a non-Muslim say it out loud.
## The objection, said properly
The comparison people make is not abstract. They put Europe’s hospitals, infrastructure, rule of law, and scientific output next to much of the Muslim world as it stands today, and they draw a clean line from the gap to a verdict about the religion. If the truth makes a people, they say, then the truest people should look the strongest. And right now, on a lot of visible metrics, we don’t.
I had this wrong for years. I thought the only honest options were either to deny the lag, or to feel embarrassed by the deen itself. Both were lazy. The lag shows up in enough places that pretending otherwise insults people who live under it. The embarrassment is a category error, and the rest of this chapter is about why.
## Islam is not graded on our report card
A religion’s truth does not rise or fall on how well, or how badly, its followers live it out. If it did, every revealed message would have been cancelled the week after it arrived, because people have always been people.
Think of a hospital. The medicine does not stop working because some of the staff skip their own checkups, eat badly, and still wear the coat. You judge the medicine by what it does when someone takes it. You don’t throw the pharmacy out because the ward is messy.
Every prophet dealt with this gap. Musa, peace be upon him, led a people who turned to a calf the moment he was gone. Isa, peace be upon him, was followed by people who would later argue over him more than they obeyed him. And Muhammad, blessings and peace be upon him, spent years correcting companions who loved him and still fell short of what he brought. The gap between message and messenger’s people is an old human story, older than any of our modern shame about it.
So when someone points at a corrupt ministry, a broken street, a tyrant with a Muslim name, or a community that barely prays and calls that Islam, they are confusing the product with the packaging. Islam is the revelation. Muslims are the people carrying it, carefully in some places and carelessly in others.
By the way, I’m not saying our failures don’t matter. They matter a lot. They just don’t decide whether Allah spoke.
## “The Westerners are the real Muslims”
There’s a version of this doubt that hits closer than GDP charts, and I hear it from our own people more than from strangers. You’ve heard it at a dinner table. You’ve heard it after someone comes back from Europe or America. You’ve probably said a softer form of it yourself.
Westerners are the real Muslims. We just have the label.
They mean the queue that doesn’t dissolve into elbows and shouting. The public servant who won’t take a bribe for a simple stamp. The neighbor who returns a wallet with the cash still inside. The street that stays clean because people treat shared space like it belongs to someone. The hospital appointment that happens on the hour it was booked. The teacher who shows up. The bus that arrives. They look at that order, then they look at the chaos and petty corruption some of us grew up treating as normal, and they reach for a sentence that sounds almost pious: those people are living Islam better than we are.
I felt the pull of that sentence hard when I first landed in Belgium. In Berkane I knew the deen by sound and season, the adhan, Ramadan, the way people say inshallah even when they mean maybe. Then I watched a clerk process a document without making me feel like I needed a cousin in the office. I watched people wait their turn like the turn itself mattered. Part of me, before I had language for it, started grading the West as more “Muslim” than home, and grading home as more “Muslim” only on paper.
Let’s face it, the observation is not empty. Islam did not only teach us how to pray. It taught amanah, trustworthiness, until a believer’s word was supposed to be safer than a signed contract, and it taught that cheating a customer is a form of war against Allah and His Messenger. So when a non-Muslim society outruns a Muslim-majority one on honesty in daily life, that is not a small bruise to the ego. That is a public exposure of how far we have drifted from what we claim.
Having said that, the leap from “they practice some of our manners better” to “they are the real Muslims” is where the sentence goes wrong, and it goes wrong in a way that can quite frankly hollow out a person’s deen.
Muslim is not a nickname for good citizenship. A Muslim is someone who submits to Allah upon the testimony that none has the right to be worshipped except Him, and that Muhammad is His messenger, then tries to build a life on that submission. Character is part of that life. It is not a substitute for it. You can return lost property and still reject the One who commanded you to return it. You can have cleaner streets than a Muslim city and still organize a whole culture around desires Islam came to restrain.
The Qur’an already refused to let people confuse a claim, or a partial resemblance, with the full thing. If even people who said the shahada, the testimony of faith, with their tongues could still be corrected about the difference between outward submission and settled iman, then people who never accepted the shahada at all cannot be turned into “the real Muslims” because their post office works. Submission has a door. Good manners are not that door. They are fruit that should grow after you walk through it. Some of the nomadic Arabs came to the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, saying they had believed, and Allah corrected the wording itself, in the same verse that woke the inherited Muslim earlier in this chapter: “The bedouins say, ‘We have believed.’ Say, ‘You have not yet believed. But say, “We have submitted,” for faith has not yet entered your hearts.’” (Surah Al-Hujurat, aya 14)
There’s another problem with the slogan. It flattens both sides until neither is seen clearly. It turns Western societies into a morality play in which they only ever appear as honest, punctual, and fair, while Muslim societies only ever appear as corrupt and loud. Both pictures are incomplete. Plenty of Western life is built on things the deen names as ruin: riba as the normal bloodstream of the economy, alcohol as a social default, sexual ethics rewritten every decade, loneliness sold back to people as a subscription. Plenty of Muslim life still carries warmth, neighborliness, respect for parents, and a public memory of Allah that many Western streets have lost. If we are going to be severe with our failures, we should be accurate about theirs too, or we end up converting out of embarrassment rather than conviction.
For many of us, the sentence does a third job under the table. It becomes a way to leave without admitting we are leaving. If the West is “more Muslim,” then drifting toward the West feels like drifting toward the deen, even when the destination has no prayer, no Qur’an, no halal and haram, no Lord waiting at the end of it. The slogan soothes the guilt. It lets a person look at their own community’s hypocrisy, which is often real, and treat that hypocrisy as proof that the revelation itself is empty. That is the category error again, only dressed in a compliment to someone else’s manners.
So when someone says Westerners are the real Muslims, I want us to answer without defensiveness and without self-erasure. Yes, some of them outdo us in traits Islam made obligatory. That should shame us into repentance, not into abandoning the shahada. No, that does not make them Muslim. And no, the cure is not to keep narrating our inferiority until the deen feels like a family heirloom we are too embarrassed to carry. The cure is to become the kind of Muslims whose public life no longer forces people into that comparison in the first place.
The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, tied faith to the safety people feel around a believer. Abu Hurayrah, one of the Prophet’s most prolific companions, reported that the Messenger of Allah, peace and blessings be upon him, said: “The Muslim is the one from whose tongue and hand the people are safe, and the believer is the one from whom the people’s lives and wealth are safe.” (Sunan an-Nasa’i)
If that description fits a non-Muslim colleague more than it fits us, the answer is not to crown the colleague with our name. The answer is to recover the description.
## When Muslims actually lived it
If the deen produced only decline, the objection would land harder. History doesn’t cooperate with that story.
For a stretch of centuries, roughly from the eighth to the thirteenth, Muslim cities were among the world’s centers of learning. Ibn Sina’s *Canon of Medicine* was studied in European universities for centuries, Fatima al-Fihri’s endowment in Fez grew into al-Qarawiyyin, a center of learning that endured for centuries, and the bimaristans, the hospitals of that world, treated the sick as a public trust, not a private luxury, caring across faith lines in a way that still surprises people who only know the caricature. Cordoba, Baghdad, and Cairo were not waiting for Europe to invent seriousness. Parts of Europe were still building the university culture that would later take so much from these same libraries.
That flowering came from inside the tradition, not as an accident around it. The same deen that produced those scientists treated knowledge as worship. Anas ibn Malik, the companion who served the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, from a young age, reported that the Messenger of Allah, peace and blessings be upon him, said: “Seeking knowledge is a duty upon every Muslim.” (Sunan Ibn Majah) A civilization does not stumble into that kind of curiosity. It grows it when revelation keeps telling it that ignorance is a failure of duty.
So the proof of concept already exists. When Muslims took the deen seriously as a full way of life, including the mind, they did not look like a people waiting to be rescued by someone else’s modernity.
## What actually broke the run
If the Golden Age is the proof of concept, then the next question is what interrupted it. Not one villain. A stack of them, over a long time.
Colonization is not a mood. It was a machine. Empires stripped resources, redrew maps, and dismantled local institutions across huge stretches of the Muslim world. In 1916, the Sykes-Picot Agreement between Britain and France carved up Arab provinces of the collapsing Ottoman Empire with almost no regard for the people who would have to live inside those lines. Kurds were split across borders they did not draw. Sectarian and ethnic tensions were frozen into state shapes designed for someone else’s convenience. A century of outside intervention did not invent every local failure, but it did lock many of them into maps that still bleed.
Formal independence did not always mean freedom in practice. Scholars use the word neocolonialism for a reason: debt conditionality, security guarantees, trade dependency, and foreign protection arrangements that keep a state standing while limiting how free it is. The 2003 invasion of Iraq sits in that longer story as a brutal reminder that sovereignty on paper can still be rewritten by force; the weapons of mass destruction used to justify the war were not there, and that part is settled history. Outside hands have shaped the region long after textbooks said the colonial chapter closed.
Then there is governance that hollows a society from the inside. Oil wealth can fund a state without needing much taxation, and therefore without the ordinary pressure that makes rulers answerable to the people who pay. Resource money can buy security forces, silence, and temporary calm. It cannot buy a culture of accountability on its own. Political scientist Ahmet Kuru, in a comparative history of Muslim and Western development, argues the long reversal does not trace cleanly to the teachings of Islam; it traces to choices and institutions that formed later, including an alliance between certain religious establishments and military rulers that traded legitimacy for protection and slowly narrowed the room for independent thought. I am not going to turn this into a list of living names. The pattern is old enough, and classical enough, that Islam already has a vocabulary for it: scholars who bless power instead of restraining it.
Abu Sa’id al-Khudri, one of the Prophet’s companions, reported that the Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, said that the best jihad is a word of justice before an oppressive ruler. (Sunan Abi Dawud)
A community that forgets that sentence will eventually confuse loyalty with silence. And silence, over generations, is expensive.
There was also a slower drift. In some circles, a civilization that once produced its own scientists and jurists started importing both ideas and infrastructure as finished products. Curiosity became consumption. The ummah that once translated the world into Arabic so it could master it began waiting for other people to invent the next tool and sell it back with a user manual.
I should probably admit something here. When I first left Morocco for Belgium, part of me absorbed the scoreboard without noticing. Cleaner systems. Stronger institutions. Better research pipelines. It took me longer than it should have to separate “they built better states in this era” from “their metaphysics must be truer.” Those are not the same sentence. I mixed them because the first one was easier to see from a train window.
## When power rents a better face
Sometimes the shame cuts from the other direction: polished hypocrisy wearing religious language.
You’ve probably watched a version of it. A government that still sells itself as a guardian of religious seriousness flies in global entertainment brands, fills arenas, and rents the world’s applause while tightening its grip at home. Human rights organizations have a plain name for the broader pattern: image laundering, a spotlight hired to make harder questions look old-fashioned. I’m not writing a foreign-policy pamphlet here. I’m naming a spiritual injury. When public religion becomes branding, ordinary Muslims inherit the bill. The world looks at the show and thinks it has seen the deen. Young people look at the contradiction and feel something in them go cold.
That coldness shows up in the data too. Survey research across the Arab world has tracked rising numbers of young people describing themselves as not religious, and trust in religious political parties falling hard in the same years. Some of that is specifically about religion. Some of it is a wider collapse of trust in public institutions. Either way, when power uses the language of the deen without the ethics of the deen, people do not only blame the politicians. Too often they blame the whole house.
## What the deen actually promised
Nowhere in the Qur’an or the sunnah is asphalt the proof of iman. Nowhere is a G7 seat the prize of tawhid. Allah did not say: believe, and your GDP will rise on schedule. If material success proved a religion true, the crown would keep changing hands every century, and whoever is richest this decade would own the truth. Post-war Japan and South Korea industrialized at astonishing speed with no dependence on Christianity or Islam as the engine. A country’s paved roads and hospital beds test its economy, its history, its wars, and its politics. They do not, by themselves, test the truth of its scripture, and the yardstick has to be honest about what it is measuring.
What He promised was guidance, a standard of justice, and sakinah for hearts that remember Him: a good life for whoever does good as a believer, and a final success that does not fit in a quarterly report.
“Whoever does good, whether male or female, and is a believer, We will surely bless them with a good life, and We will certainly reward them according to the best of their deeds.” (Surah An-Nahl, aya 97)
“Know that this worldly life is no more than play, amusement, luxury, mutual boasting, and competition in wealth and children. This is like rain that causes plants to grow, to the delight of the planters. But later the plants dry up and you see them wither, then they are reduced to chaff. And in the Hereafter there will be either severe punishment or forgiveness and pleasure of Allah, whereas the life of this world is no more than the delusion of enjoyment.” (Surah Al-Hadid, aya 20)
That does not mean poverty is holy, or that weakness is a spiritual aesthetic. It means the deen never offered the scoreboard the objector is using. If you test Islam by a promise it never made, you will always find it “failing.”
What Allah did tell this ummah is heavier than a development index:
“You are the best community ever raised for humanity—you encourage good, forbid evil, and believe in Allah.” (Surah Aal Imran, aya 110)
“Best” here reads like a job description more than a medal. When we stop commanding what is right and forbidding what is wrong, we should not be shocked if the honor attached to that description feels thinner in our hands.
And He tied the condition of a people to what they do with themselves:
“Indeed, Allah would never change a people’s state ˹of favour˺ until they change their own state ˹of faith˺.” (Surah Ar-Ra’d, aya 11)
That aya does not let us blame every wound on outsiders, and it does not pretend outsiders never wounded us. It puts agency back where revelation always put it: in what a people accept, excuse, protect, and become.
## So what now, if the lag is real?
If Islam is true, and many Muslim societies are still behind on metrics that matter for human dignity, then abandoning the deen is the wrong move. The work is to stop confusing our drift with Allah’s speech.
We start by telling the truth about the gap without letting enemies of the deen own the diagnosis. Corruption is not “our culture,” and tyranny is not “just how Muslims are.” Intellectual laziness dressed up as piety still fails the deen. These are failures against Islam.
Then we remember that a civilization is built more than it is inherited forever. Wealth is influence. So is independence. So are institutions. The Prophet, peace and blessings be upon him, was never financially illiterate, and many of the companions who were promised Paradise were traders and builders rather than people who romanticized helplessness. For centuries, waqf endowments funded schools and hospitals so the ummah did not have to beg for every generation’s basics. Most of the great institutions of this religion ran on assets given once for the sake of Allah and left standing.
We’ve done civilizational work without waiting for permission before. Trading families from the Hadramawt valley in Yemen carried Islam across oceans into what is now Indonesia and Malaysia, home to vast Muslim populations today. No army sailed with them as the main engine. They traded fairly, kept their word when it cost them, married into communities, and lived the religion in a way people could trust. Entire regions chose it. They understood something we half forget: whoever owns the marketplace, the skills, the stories, and the institutions ends up deciding what feels normal to the next generation.
That is part of the work in front of us now. Muslims who master the hard skills of this century instead of only consuming them. Endowments that outlive fundraising campaigns. Schools that form minds. Media that does not rent our dignity out by the episode. Homes that raise children inside a living culture rather than inside an algorithm. The goal is bigger than surviving someone else’s civilization. It is building one rooted enough that our children grow up standing inside it.
These days, when a train carries me past a skyline that used to shame me, I still see the gap. What changed is the sentence it produces in me. It used to say, look what they have. It says now, look what we owe.
## The question underneath the question
Go back to the sentence I used to hear before I could answer it. If Islam is true, why are Muslims behind?
One answer is that too many of us are behind Islam. Another is that history was brutal, and brutal history leaves debris for a long time. And underneath both of those, the test was never whose skyline is taller. Those answers can sit together without cancelling each other.
The question underneath all of them is whether what Allah said is true. Hospitals, courts, and competence matter, and we should want them badly, for Allah’s sake and for people’s sake. A faith still stands or falls on revelation, not on this year’s ranking table.
If the medicine is true, the right response to a sick ward is to take the medicine again, properly, and rebuild the ward until it looks like it belongs to the people who claim the cure.
May Allah make us a people whose condition matches the deen we defend, give us minds that seek knowledge as worship, give us the courage to tell the truth even when power would rather we stay quiet and to keep what is His even when the room goes cold, make us Muslims who know the difference between culture and revelation, and let iman enter our hearts so deeply that we no longer need borrowed approval to stand upright. Ameen.
