*The message didn’t begin with him. From Adam to Isa to the man himself: his truthfulness, his sacrifices, and why his Sunnah is revelation.*
You’ve just seen the case that this Book is Allah’s own speech. Which raises a question standing right behind it: who did He trust to carry it? I know whose name is on this chapter, and we’ll get to him, I promise. But I want to invite you somewhere first. If I introduce you to Muhammad, blessings and peace be upon him, without everything that came before him, you’ll meet him the way his critics prefer to present him: a man who appeared out of nowhere in seventh-century Arabia carrying a brand-new religion. That version of the story is the one version Islam itself has never told. So come back with me. Not a hundred years, not a thousand. All the way back, to the first human being who ever opened his eyes.
## Where does his story actually start?
It starts with Adam, peace be upon him, who was the first man and the first prophet at the same time. Sit with that for a second, because it means there has never been a single generation of us that Allah left guessing. The Lord who introduced Himself to you in chapter 1, the One nearer to you than your own jugular vein, made a promise at the very beginning of our story, while Adam was still on his way down to this earth:
“We said, 'Descend all of you! Then when guidance comes to you from Me, whoever follows it, there will be no fear for them, nor will they grieve.'” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 38)
Guidance would keep coming. That was the arrangement from the first day of human history, and Allah kept it the way He keeps every promise He has ever made:
“We surely sent a messenger to every community, saying, 'Worship Allah and shun false gods.'” (Surah An-Nahl, aya 36)
Every community. Nuh, peace be upon him, stood in front of his people for nine hundred and fifty years while they slid into the first idol-worship our species ever invented, and he never changed the sentence he was sent with: worship Allah, you have no god other than Him. Ibrahim, peace be upon him, grew up in a house where his own father carved the idols, shattered them in broad daylight, was thrown into a fire built to end him, and walked out untouched. Years later he stood in an empty desert valley, lifting stone foundations with his son Ismail, peace be upon him. That valley would one day be called Makkah, and those foundations the Kaabah. Listen to what he asked for while the work was still in his hands:
“Our Lord! Raise from among them a messenger who will recite to them Your revelations, teach them the Book and wisdom, and purify them. Indeed, You ˹alone˺ are the Almighty, All-Wise.” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 129)
Keep that dua in your pocket. This whole chapter is the story of it being answered.
After Ibrahim the line kept running through his descendants. Yusuf in Egypt, peace be upon him. Musa, peace be upon him, sent to the most powerful tyrant on earth and handed the Torah, a revealed Book entrusted to an entire nation. Dawud, peace be upon him, given the Zabur. Isa, peace be upon him, given the Injil. Different centuries, different languages, one message. Abu Hurayrah, one of the Prophet’s most prolific hadith narrators, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “The prophets are paternal brothers; their mothers are different, but their religion is one.” (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim). Islam didn’t begin in Makkah. Makkah is where it was completed.
## What do we say about Isa?
I’ve had this conversation in Belgium more times than I can count. A classmate finds out I’m Muslim, and somewhere between two lectures the question arrives: so what do you people think of Jesus? They’re braced for distance, maybe for hostility. What they hear instead usually rearranges the map in their head.
We believe in him. Belief in Isa, peace be upon him, is a condition of being Muslim at all; a person who denies him has left Islam, full stop. We believe he was born of the virgin Maryam with no father, that he spoke in the cradle, and that by Allah’s permission he healed the blind, cured the leper, breathed life into birds of clay, and raised the dead. We believe he is the Messiah sent to the Children of Israel, that he was never killed on that cross but raised alive to his Lord, and that he will return near the end of this world. And we believe Allah gave him a true revelation:
“Then in the footsteps of the prophets, We sent Jesus, son of Mary, confirming the Torah revealed before him. And We gave him the Gospel containing guidance and light.” (Surah Al-Ma’idah, aya 46)
His mother, Maryam, has an entire surah of the Qur’an carrying her name, and she’s mentioned by name more times in the Qur’an than in the whole New Testament.
So where does the road split? One sentence carries the whole disagreement: we love him as a mighty prophet, and we refuse to worship him as a god. When someone says son of God, the answer was already given to you in chapter 1, in the four short lines of Surah Al-Ikhlas: He has never had offspring, nor was He born. And when the miracle of a fatherless birth is offered as proof of divinity, the Qur’an replies with a comparison:
“Indeed, the example of Jesus in the sight of Allah is like that of Adam. He created him from dust, then said to him, 'Be!' And he was!” (Surah Aal Imran, aya 59)
Adam had neither father nor mother, and nobody on earth worships Adam. A miracle points at the One who performed it. And when the Qur’an lets Isa introduce himself, speaking from the cradle, this is the opening line it records:
“Jesus declared, 'I am truly a servant of Allah. He has destined me to be given the Scripture and to be a prophet.'” (Surah Maryam, aya 30)
## Why send one more prophet?
Sit a practising Muslim and a practising Christian at the same table and start listing what they both hold, and the list runs longer than either of them expects: a Day of Judgment when every soul answers for its life, a paradise and a fire that are not metaphors, angels carrying out their Lord’s commands, prophets sent by the Creator to His creation. We even believe in their books. The originals, that is. The Torah on the day it reached Musa and the Injil on the day it reached Isa were the speech of Allah, guidance and light in the Qur’an’s own words. A Muslim doesn’t sneer at those scriptures; if anything, we grieve over them, because Allah left them in human custody, and, as you’ve already seen, their wording drifted across centuries of hand-copying while the final Book was preserved intact.
So when Allah sent Muhammad, blessings and peace be upon him, He was answering a problem the whole chain had been building toward: the same message needed to go out one final time, to everyone rather than to one nation, and this time no human custodian would be trusted with its preservation.
“We have sent you ˹O Prophet˺ only as a deliverer of good news and a warner to all of humanity, but most people do not know.” (Surah Saba, aya 28)
“Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but is the Messenger of Allah and the seal of the prophets. And Allah has ˹perfect˺ knowledge of all things.” (Surah Al-Ahzab, aya 40)
The seal of the prophets. The relay that began with Adam ends with him, so there’s no prophet coming after him and no book coming after his Book. How Allah has guarded that Book, down to the letter, you’ve already seen, and subhanAllah, it’s one of the most remarkable stories in the deen. But a final message raises a question about its carrier before it raises any question about its text. Out of every human being who has ever lived, who did Allah trust with the last words He would send us? Al-Irbad ibn Sariyah, one of the Prophet’s companions, may Allah be pleased with him, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “I am the dua of my father Ibrahim and the glad tidings of Isa.” (Musnad Ahmad, graded sahih by al-Albani). The dua made beside the Kaabah’s rising foundations was answered in that same valley, and the answer was him. Let me introduce you.
There’s something strange about Muhammad, blessings and peace be upon him. The people who hated him most, the ones who organised boycotts against his family, who paid poets to ridicule him, who sent armies to finish what the ridicule couldn’t, they didn’t start by calling him a liar. They’d watched him for forty years. They’d trusted him with their savings, asked him to arbitrate their disputes, and given him a title that stuck: Al-Amin. The trustworthy one. Then the message came. And the same community that had named him the truthful one suddenly claimed his tongue was inventing speech about God.
That’s where the case for him has to begin, because everything else depends on it.
## Didn’t his enemies already know he was honest?
The Qur’an does not present the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, as a stranger who appeared from nowhere. Allah commanded him to remind his people that he had lived an entire lifetime among them before revelation. That argument matters. A single speech, a viral moment, or a polished public image proves nothing about character. What proves it is years of ordinary contact: debts repaid, trust returned, anger restrained, promises kept when no one’s filming.
“Say, 'Had Allah willed, I would not have recited it to you, nor would He have made it known to you. I had lived my whole life among you before this ˹revelation˺. Do you not understand?'” (Surah Yunus, aya 16)
At-Tabari explains this verse as a direct appeal to what Quraysh already knew of him: they had never found him lying before the Qur’an, so how could they suddenly accuse him of inventing speech about Allah? Ibn Kathir makes the same point when he notes that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, was known among them for truthfulness and trustworthiness before his mission. Muslims loving him after they believed is not even the strongest evidence here. His enemies had to fight a reputation for truth they themselves had helped build.
He grew up among a people who weren’t easy critics. They knew his lineage, his poverty, his travels, his business dealings, his marriage, his grief, his silences, and his public conduct. In a small tribal society, you don’t get to curate a private self and a public brand the way people do now. Your character leaks into the street. If you cheat, people know. If you lie, people remember. If you betray a trust, the city keeps the story.
Khadijah, the Prophet’s first wife, may Allah be pleased with her, understood this before anyone else. When he returned from Hira trembling, she didn’t calm him with vague reassurance. Aisha, the Prophet’s wife, may Allah be pleased with her, reported that Khadijah told him: “Never! By Allah, Allah will never disgrace you. You keep the ties of kinship, you bear the burden of the weak, you give to the destitute, you honour the guest, and you help against the calamities of truth.” narrated by the hadith of the beginning of revelation (Sahih al-Bukhari). Her argument was moral theology: Allah doesn’t disgrace a man whose life has been spent carrying the burdens of others. The first believer in him wasn’t fooled by charisma; she’d lived close enough to know.
Her testimony came from love, and a skeptic is free to discount a wife’s witness. The Qur’an doesn’t leave the case resting on friendly witnesses; it calls the other side. In the bitterest years of Quraysh’s campaign against him, Allah revealed a verse that entered their private verdict into the record for all time: they didn’t actually believe the man was lying. Their war was never with his honesty. It was with what his honesty was now carrying, because admitting the messenger was truthful would have meant the message was true, and the message was going to cost them their idols, their trade routes, and their rank.
“We certainly know that what they say grieves you ˹O Prophet˺. It is not your honesty they question—it is Allah’s signs that the wrongdoers deny.” (Surah Al-An’am, aya 33)
Years later that private verdict was dragged into the open, in front of an emperor, and the scene is preserved in Sahih al-Bukhari, narrated by Abdullah ibn Abbas, the Prophet’s cousin, may Allah be pleased with them both. The Prophet’s letter inviting Heraclius to Islam reached the Roman court, and Heraclius ordered that any Arabs in the region be brought to him. A Quraysh trading caravan was found, and at its head was Abu Sufyan, in those years still the chief organiser of the war against Islam. The emperor sat him down, placed his caravan companions behind him with instructions to call out any lie, and interrogated him about the man claiming prophethood: his lineage, his followers, whether they were increasing, whether anyone abandoned the religion in disgust after entering it. And among the questions was the one this whole section turns on: did you ever accuse him of lying before he said what he said? Abu Sufyan, with his own men listening behind him, had to answer no. When the interrogation was finished, Heraclius explained his reasoning aloud, and it deserves a slow reading: I asked you whether you had ever accused him of lying before he said what he said, and you claimed not, so I knew that he would not leave off lying to people and then go and lie about Allah. A Roman emperor with no stake in Makkah’s quarrels, reasoning from testimony extracted in public from the enemy’s own commander, built this section’s entire argument by himself. It’s been sitting in Sahih al-Bukhari for twelve centuries, waiting for every generation that thinks the question is new.
## He started with what they already knew
When revelation became public, he climbed Safa and called his people by name. Ibn Abbas, may Allah be pleased with them both, reported that he asked them: “If I were to inform you that cavalry were in the valley intending to attack you, would you believe me?” (Sahih al-Bukhari). They said yes. They’d never known him to lie. Only then did the warning come.
Think about what that move does. It forced his listeners to separate two things they wanted to keep blended: their discomfort with the message, and the question of the messenger’s credibility. You can sense the frustration in Abu Lahab’s response, which the Qur’an preserved: is this what you gathered us for? The message made him uncomfortable. But the man delivering it was already known to be true, and he couldn’t touch that without admitting he’d been wrong about him for forty years.
## Why does the Qur’an correct him in public?
One of the most striking things about the Qur’an is that it does not flatter the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, in the way human propaganda flatters leaders. It consoles him, honours him, commands obedience to him, and raises his mention, but it also corrects him in public. Surah Abasa preserves one such moment: Allah named the instant he turned from the blind Companion Abd Allah ibn Umm Maktum, one of the early Muslims, may Allah be pleased with him, while addressing the leaders of Quraysh. The same pattern shows up again in Surah At-Tahrim, where Allah corrected him over forbidding for himself what Allah had made lawful. A false prophet manufacturing a book to protect his own image wouldn’t preserve divine correction against himself and then recite it in prayer for the community forever.
Scholars such as al-Qurtubi and Ibn Kathir mention the Abasa passage as part of the Prophet’s honour, not against it, because it shows the Book was revelation above him, not speech controlled by him. The Messenger was the noblest of creation, but he was still a servant under command. This protects Muslims from two opposite errors: reducing him to a mere historical reformer, or raising him beyond servanthood. Islam doesn’t need a flawless public-relations myth. It has something stronger: a true Messenger, corrected by his Lord, then obedient to that correction.
Public correction is painful even when it comes from another human being. Here the correction is revelation, recited by the Prophet himself, memorised by his companions, written into the Qur’an, and preserved until the end of time. It became impossible for later Muslims to turn him into a manufactured hero who never receives direction. The Qur’an guards his prophethood without pretending he was above being corrected.
## He refused revenge when he had every right to it
The easiest mercy to praise is soft mercy, the kind shown when nothing is at stake. The Prophet’s mercy was harder than that. It appeared when he had power to retaliate, when grief was fresh, when enemies were in reach, and when the ego of an ordinary man would have demanded revenge. At Taif, after being humiliated and stoned until, as the seerah books describe, blood ran into his sandals, he was offered a destruction no human tyrant would have refused. He refused it. Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her, reported that he said: “Rather, I hope that Allah will bring forth from their descendants people who will worship Allah alone, associating nothing with Him.” (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim).
“We have sent you ˹O Prophet˺ only as a mercy for the whole world.” (Surah Al-Anbiya, aya 107)
This is where mercy is often misunderstood. Many today imagine mercy as the removal of all discomfort, or the refusal to ever judge. Prophetic mercy runs deeper than comfort. A doctor who cuts to heal is showing mercy. So is a parent who forbids the fire, even while the child screams for it. The Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, didn’t flatter souls into destruction. He was tender with people, but that tenderness had a direction: toward Allah, repentance, justice, and the Hereafter.
And the doctor’s mercy is not an abstraction, because Sahih al-Bukhari preserves it operating in real time, narrated by Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him. A Bedouin walked into the Prophet’s mosque in Madinah and, in front of everyone, began to urinate in it. The companions rose at him, and the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, stopped them: leave him until he has finished. Then he called for a bucket of water to be poured over the spot, and he taught the man, in a sentence sized to the offence and not an inch larger, that these mosques are not for any of this filth, but for the remembrance of Allah, the prayer, and the recitation of the Qur’an. The man himself delivered the verdict on what that mercy produced: he was later heard praying, O Allah, have mercy on me and on Muhammad, and do not have mercy on anyone else along with us. Nothing was surrendered in that scene. The ground got washed and the teaching got delivered, so the sin was never validated. The man walked out loving the one who corrected him, so nothing in him got destroyed either. Anyone who has ever been corrected in front of a crowd knows how rare it is to be given both halves at once.
That is why his mercy reaches both victim and sinner. He comforted the broken, but he also called the wrongdoer back before the wrongdoer met Allah with blood, arrogance, or desire on the record. Today, when people are trained by screens to think only in terms of personal expression, this kind of mercy feels almost strange. A person can spend a lifetime chasing what he feels like becoming and still waste it, because the only question that ever mattered is what Allah created him to become.
## Did he spread Islam by the sword?
You’ve already seen the claim that jihad simply means holy war answered in full: the strict conditions, a defensive cause, a legitimate collective authority, and the wide protections built in from the start, that stand between a Muslim and any permission to fight at all. This section watches those conditions govern a real war, the wars the man who carried this message actually fought.
A common contemporary attack is to isolate the Prophet’s battles from the world in which they occurred, then judge them through slogans produced by societies whose own wars are often more destructive, more anonymous, and less restrained. Everyone in that era lived inside a world of conflict. What actually matters is the kind of moral law he brought into it. The answer is uncomfortable for his critics: he restrained war before restraint was fashionable. Ibn Umar, the son of Umar ibn al-Khattab, may Allah be pleased with them both, reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “Do not kill women or children.” (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim).
The jurists didn’t treat such reports as sentimental footnotes. They built law from them. In the books of fiqh and in the discussions of scholars such as an-Nawawi and Ibn Qudamah, non-combatants are protected, mutilation is forbidden, treachery is forbidden, and fighting is bound by rules. Abu Bakr, the first Caliph, may Allah be pleased with him, instructed armies, in the charge recorded in the Muwatta of Imam Malik, not to betray, not to mutilate, not to kill children, women, or the elderly, and not to destroy trees needlessly. This is the heritage of a Prophet whose command shaped the conscience of a civilization.
“Fight in the cause of Allah ˹only˺ against those who wage war against you, but do not exceed the limits. Allah does not like transgressors.” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 190)
The restraint didn’t begin with the jurists; they found it waiting in the Book. The limit travels inside the very verse that permits fighting at all, and Allah seals the command with His own love withdrawn from whoever crosses it: He does not love transgressors. But the question a reader carries today is usually not about battlefield rules at all. It sits closer to home: what is my deen’s default posture toward the non-Muslims I live beside, study with, and work for, the ones who have never fought anyone over religion in their lives? The Qur’an answers that question by name:
“Allah does not forbid you from dealing kindly and fairly with those who have neither fought nor driven you out of your homes. Surely Allah loves those who are fair.” (Surah Al-Mumtahanah, aya 8)
Birr is the word the verse chooses for how a Muslim may treat the peaceful non-Muslim, and it is the same word the religion uses for the kindness owed to parents, which is as warm as the vocabulary of the deen gets. Righteousness and justice toward those who do not fight you over your religion are not a grudging tolerance squeezed out of the text by modern embarrassment; they are stated by Allah, with His love for the just placed as the seal on the verse. A Muslim in Europe, the US, or anywhere else who treats his neighbours well and deals honestly with people who never fought him over his religion is living exactly what this aya commands.
## Prophecies that unfolded
Umar ibn al-Khattab, the second Caliph, may Allah be pleased with him, reported a famous event where the angel Jibril came to teach the companions their religion. When Jibril asked about the signs of the Hour, the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, replied: “That you will see the barefooted, naked, destitute herdsmen competing in constructing tall buildings.” (Sahih Muslim).
At the time, this made no sense. The bedouins of the Arabian peninsula were poor desert dwellers; Rome and Persia held the power. Anyone guessing blind in that century would have bet on Rome building higher. Today, oil has turned that barren desert into a competition for the world’s tallest towers, and those same Arab states literally race each other for the record. He wasn’t guessing.
## Does it matter what he said, if we already have the Qur’an?
Many Muslims today treat the Sunnah like optional extra content: useful if it feels inspiring, disposable if it interrupts the lifestyle they were already building. But the Qur’an does not allow that relationship. Allah tied loving Him to following the Messenger, tied obedience to the Messenger to obedience to Himself, and commanded believers to take what the Messenger gives and stop where he forbids. The Sunnah runs deeper than a personality cult around a beloved historical figure. It’s revelation itself, lived out, explained, and carried forward hand to hand.
“Nor does he speak of his own whims. It is only a revelation sent down ˹to him˺.” (Surah An-Najm, ayas 3 and 4)
Nine words in the Arabic, and the claim this chapter was built around is settled by Allah Himself. The verses below will show that obeying the Messenger is commanded; An-Najm says something prior and stronger, that what he brought is itself revelation. His Sunnah isn’t a gifted man’s commentary on the Qur’an, to be respected the way a brilliant scholar’s opinion is respected and set aside the same way. When he taught the ummah how to pray, that teaching reached him the same way the ayat reached him: as wahy, carried through his words and his limbs instead of through recited speech. Everything else in this section stands on that, so it had to be said first, and it is Allah who says it.
“Say, ˹O Prophet,˺ 'If you ˹sincerely˺ love Allah, then follow me; Allah will love you and forgive your sins. For Allah is All-Forgiving, Most Merciful.'” (Surah Aal Imran, aya 31)
“Whoever obeys the Messenger has truly obeyed Allah.” (Surah An-Nisa, aya 80)
Ibn Kathir calls Surah Aal Imran, aya 31, the verse of examination: whoever claims love of Allah while not following the Muhammadan way is false in that claim until they follow him. That is not harsh; Ibn Kathir is simply being precise about what the verse actually tests. A religiously aesthetic feed, a reminder posted often, or a nasheed that moves someone to tears says nothing about whether love is real. Love becomes real when it governs the body: how we pray, what we watch, how we speak in private messages, whether our money is clean, whether our desires have a law over them, and whether the Prophet’s way can overrule our mood.
Many people don’t reject the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, openly. They keep him as an emotional symbol and strip his authority away. Many people love his mercy right up until it asks something of them, and quote his kindness while ignoring his worship. His manners get praised on repeat, then the same tongue turns around and uses itself for sarcasm, exposure, flirtation, and argument. They say “Sunnah” with warmth, but when the Sunnah touches entertainment, desire, dress, money, gender relations, or private browsing, suddenly it becomes “just culture” or “not that deep.”
The Qur’an does not allow this split. Allah sent more than a book of abstract values for each generation to reinterpret on its own. He sent a Messenger who lived an entire life in front of witnesses: praying, trading, marrying, judging, grieving, and finally dying among the very people who had watched him do it. The Sunnah is revelation translated into human motion. Without it, Islam becomes a set of slogans that every age can repaint in its own colours.
For a reader formed by streaming platforms, short-form feeds, private chats, and constant online reaction, this is not a small point. These systems do more than entertain. They train what feels normal, how quickly the tongue reacts, and how easily a limit starts to feel like oppression. The Sunnah retrains the human being. It slows the gaze, disciplines appetite, and gives silence back its dignity. Modesty becomes beautiful instead of embarrassing, and worship moves back to the centre instead of sitting as the leftover of the day.
**How the Sunnah was guarded**
The modern suspicion usually sounds like this: hadith came too late, people made things up, and we can just keep the Qur’an. The first problem is that the Qur’an itself sends us to the Messenger’s explanation. The second is historical. Hadith preservation was not a casual memory game. It developed into a disciplined science of chains, narrator biographies, comparison of routes, testing of memory, and criticism of hidden defects. Whether a statement sounded beautiful was never enough for the scholars. They wanted to know who said it, who heard it, whether the two men had actually met, and whether the narrator’s memory and character could bear the weight of the claim. Abd Allah ibn al-Mubarak, one of the early scholars of hadith, said it plainly: “The isnad is from the religion; were it not for the isnad, anyone could say whatever they wanted.” (recorded in the introduction to Sahih Muslim).
The hadith tradition never asked anyone to believe every story attached to the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him. If anything, it asked the opposite. It’s asking you to respect a civilization that became almost obsessive about separating what he said from what people wished he had said. The scholars travelled for a single narration, compared wordings, exposed liars, weakened beloved reports when the chain failed, and sometimes rejected narrations that sounded spiritually attractive because the evidence did not carry them.
This is why the hadith sciences matter so much in the age of the screenshot. Today, a claim can travel to millions before anyone asks who made it. A fake quote can be attached to a scholar’s name, a stitched clip can ruin a reputation, and a confident influencer can rewrite religion between two adverts. Islam built the opposite instinct into its scholarship: slow down, ask for the chain, verify the carrier, compare the evidence, and don’t confuse reach with reliability. That instinct isn’t a relic. A Muslim scrolling through claims and screenshots today needs exactly that discipline.
One step of the objection is still standing, and it deserves to be answered in its strongest form: grant every chain, why does a Muslim need any of it when the Qur’an is already in his hands? The first answer is that this proposal fails inside the Qur’an itself, because Allah describes the Messenger’s explanation as part of what was sent down:
“And We have sent down to you ˹O Prophet˺ the Reminder, so that you may explain to people what has been revealed for them, and perhaps they will reflect.” (Surah An-Nahl, aya 44)
The Reminder came down to him so that he would make it clear to people. Remove his clarification, and something Allah says He sent has been removed with it. But the more unsettling answer is that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, described the maker of this objection in advance, down to his furniture. Indeed I have been given the Qur’an and something like it along with it, he said, and soon there will be a man, reclining full on his couch, who will say: keep to this Qur’an; what you find lawful in it, take as lawful, and what you find forbidden in it, take as forbidden. It is recorded by Abu Dawud and at-Tirmidhi, narrated by al-Miqdam ibn Ma’dikarib, one of the Prophet’s companions, may Allah be pleased with him, and graded sahih by al-Albani. Fourteen centuries before the first studio microphone, the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, described this very posture in advance: a comfortable man reclining on his couch, waving off the Sunnah while never once opening a book of hadith to see what the science behind it actually involves.
And there is a question that ends the conversation in one move: derive fajr from the Qur’an alone. The Book commands salah dozens of times, in Makkan ayas and Madinan ayas, and never once states the number of its units. Two at dawn, four at dhuhr, three at maghrib: those numbers reached you through one channel only, the Sunnah, carried by the same chains the man on the couch says he does not need. Every Muslim who has ever stood up to pray fajr is already living the answer to the Qur’an-only argument, twice before sunrise, before a word of the debate has been spoken.
## What if love adds things he never asked for?
The hadith sciences guarded what he said. A second discipline had to guard something just as fragile: what gets folded into his religion after him, usually by people who loved him the most and never meant any harm.
I grew up watching Mawlid celebrated in Berkane the way I imagine it’s been done for generations: the streets lit up, chants echoing from the mosque, neighbours gathering, sweets carried between houses. As a child I didn’t question whether it was Sunnah. It felt like love for the Prophet, and the love was sincere. I still believe the love was sincere. What I had to learn later, slowly and without much comfort, is that love for him is not the same as doing what he asked. And that Allah had already closed this door before my grandfather’s grandfather was born.
“This day I have perfected for you your religion and completed My favor upon you and have approved for you Islam as religion.” (Surah Al-Ma’idah, aya 3)
A religion Allah Himself declared complete on the day of Arafat can’t be waiting on any later generation to finish the job. Every addition made in His name after that day, however sincere the person making it, is, in effect, claiming that His description of the religion as complete was itself incomplete. The Prophet sealed the same door from the other side: “Whoever introduces into this affair of ours something that is not part of it, it will be rejected.” narrated by Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim). And he named the scale of the danger without hedging: “The most truthful speech is the Book of Allah, and the best guidance is the guidance of Muhammad, and the worst of affairs are the newly invented matters, and every invented matter is an innovation, and every innovation is misguidance.” narrated by Jabir ibn Abdullah, one of the Prophet’s companions, may Allah be pleased with him (Sahih Muslim).
The word “every” wasn’t left loose. A minority of later scholars proposed a category of “good innovation,” pointing to Umar ibn al-Khattab’s decision to regularize the congregational tarawih prayer, which he called “What an excellent innovation this is.” (Sahih al-Bukhari). But the Prophet had already prayed tarawih in congregation himself for a few nights before stopping, out of fear it would be made obligatory on the ummah (Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim). Umar was reviving and regularizing something already done; he wasn’t inventing an act of worship from nothing. Imam ash-Shatibi’s entire work al-I’tisam is devoted to this question, and he concluded that “every” means what it says.
Innovation and the erosion of Tawhid feed each other. The mechanism, how one opens the door for the other and how an invented practice can slide across generations until it replaces what was actually given, is a conversation this book returns to in a later chapter. For now, the door is named: something can feel spiritual, answer a real need, and still carry no actual weight with Allah at all. That is not a comfortable place to stop. It isn't meant to be.
None of this requires imagining anyone acting in bad faith. Most innovations aren’t smuggled in by people trying to corrupt the religion; they’re added by people trying to love it more than the Prophet himself asked them to. That’s precisely why they spread so easily and why they’re so hard to remove. Nobody wants to be the one who tells a room full of sincere worshippers that what they’ve been doing since childhood has no basis.
A narrow example makes the pattern concrete. It’s become common in many mosques for a worshipper to turn to whoever prayed beside him the moment the prayer ends and say “Taqabbalallah,” as though this were part of the prayer’s etiquette, alongside a ritual handshake done the same way every time. Ibn Baz and Ibn Uthaymeen were both asked directly about this, and both gave the same answer: there’s no basis for either practice from the Prophet or his companions when done as a fixed habit after every prayer. Saying it occasionally, or exchanging it on Eid the way the companions did, carries no such warning. The difference is not the words. It’s whether a phrase with no origin in the Sunnah has been promoted into an assumed part of it.
Everything that feels devotional isn’t devotion Allah asked for. Everything repeated since childhood wasn’t necessarily handed down from him. The Sunnah isn’t the enemy of feeling; it’s the only reliable judge of whether a feeling has been aimed correctly.
## When following him costs something
We live in a time when holding onto the Sunnah can invite genuine ridicule. You might find yourself the target of jokes from coworkers, friends, or even family because you leave a gathering to pray on time, or because you refuse to shake hands with the opposite gender, or because your clothing stands out as modest in a culture that prizes exposure. It hurts to be treated as backwards when you’re simply trying to follow the best human being who ever walked the earth.
He knew this would happen. He warned his companions that a time would come when holding onto the religion would be like holding onto burning coals. And he brought comfort to those who’d feel isolated by it: Islam began as something strange, and it will return to being something strange, so give glad tidings to the strangers (Sahih Muslim). Being mocked for following him isn’t a sign you’re doing something wrong. It’s a sign you’re walking the path he walked. He was called a madman, a poet, and a sorcerer by the very people he was trying to save.
When you face that mockery, remember whose approval you’re trading. You’re trading the laughter of passing people for the companionship of the Messenger of Allah at his Cistern on the Day when the records open. The ridicule of the world is temporary.
## What he left behind
He left this world with almost none of the things by which people measure success. No palace, no stored fortune, no dynasty built around his bloodline, no monument demanding worship. His armour, blessings and peace be upon him, was held in pawn with a Jewish man for thirty sa' of barley on the day he died, as Sahih al-Bukhari records from Aisha, may Allah be pleased with her. Yet he left a Book recited every hour on earth, a Sunnah studied in every generation, a law, a civilization, a pattern of mercy, and a way for ordinary people to turn eating, sleeping, marriage, trade, grief, victory, and death into worship. The man who carried the message did not merely deliver information. By Allah’s permission, he formed a people.
## The question he leaves you with
Admiration alone was never going to be enough to end this page on. The real ending is a question of allegiance. He is truthful. The Qur’an confirms him. His Sunnah is revelation in practice, guarded by scholars who took this much care over it. Whether he is impressive stops being the real question at that point. What actually matters now is which part of our lives still refuses to follow him. And one of the things he carried is the final message itself, the Book the whole relay from Adam was moving toward, and his life proves he’d never lie about it. So the real question is no longer whether the messenger is true or whether the Book is His. It’s what that message actually asks of you. But before you can carry it, a question deserves an honest answer: why this message specifically, and not another? That is exactly where this guide turns next.
