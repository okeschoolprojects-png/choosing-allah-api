*Before I ask you to believe anything, I want to introduce you to Someone. So let’s start there.*
You’ve known this name since before you can remember. You might say it when something beautiful catches you off guard, or when you’re scared and the car stops a second too late. Perhaps you’ve whispered it into your pillow when you wanted something you couldn’t say to anyone else. Perhaps you’ve heard it float over the rooftops five times a day in the adhan. It may have been the first word recited softly into your ear as a newborn, and if Allah is kind to you it’ll be the last word on your tongue when you leave.
So let me ask you something: do you know Him?
Most of us know *about* Him. We can say He’s One, that He made everything, that He’s above us somewhere. Knowing about someone only takes a few facts. Knowing someone means recognising how they treat you and what you can trust them with. Do you know Allah like that?
For a lot of us, the answer is no, and I put myself first in that line. His name came to us the way our mother tongue and our family’s recipes did. There’s a specific kind of ache in talking to someone every day without feeling as if you’ve truly met them.
That’s what this chapter is for. Before I ask you to defend Islam or change anything about your life, I want to introduce you to your Lord. There’s no guilt to carry about the years that passed before now. We begin from wherever you are.
## What does the word “Allah” mean?
We say the word so often it can become background sound. People translate it as “God” to make it easy in English, though the translation loses some of the weight carried by the Arabic.
“Allah” belongs to the One who alone deserves every act of worship, the One the heart turns to in love and awe. The English word can be pushed around into “gods” or “goddess.” The name Allah doesn’t go plural and can’t be shared among others. When you say it, you aren’t reaching for a vague higher power. You’re calling upon the Creator who made you by the name He chose for Himself.
## Let Him introduce Himself
I’m not the right person to tell you who Allah is. Neither is any scholar or teacher, because the only One who truly knows Allah is Allah. I can only take you to what He said about Himself and what His Messenger, blessings and peace be upon him, taught about Him.
The reports don’t agree on who asked to have Allah described to them, whether it was the Makkan polytheists demanding His lineage or the Jews of Madinah putting Him to the same test. They agree on what came back: Allah answered with a surah so short that many of us memorised it as children. We recite it often enough for the tongue to race ahead while the mind stays somewhere else.
This time around, I want you to pause before reading it. These four ayat answer the questions people have carried about God across centuries. He is One, so no rival stands beside Him and no partner shares His authority. He is As-Samad, the Sustainer needed by everyone, while He needs nothing. He was never born and never had children. Whatever picture the mind attempts to draw, there’s nothing comparable to Him.
“Say, ˹O Prophet,˺ ‘He is Allah, One ˹and Indivisible˺; Allah, the Sustainer ˹needed by all˺. He has never had offspring, nor was He born. And there is none comparable to Him.’” (Surah Al-Ikhlas, ayas 1–4)
## The Allah you may have grown up with
Does that sound like the Allah you grew up hearing about?
For many of us, the answer is no. We received the long list of halal and haram years before anyone thought to mention that He’s the Most Merciful. The Allah we pictured was mostly angry, disappointed in us, keeping a careful record and waiting for the next slip. Fear arrived first. Love, if it turned up at all, came limping in much later.
Other homes had no fear because Allah barely came up. His name hung around the edges of family life, solid enough to swear by and available when things fell apart. We shrank *inshallah* into a polite maybe, or a soft no, without stopping to weigh whose name was in our mouth. He became a phrase we reached for. We never truly felt we’d met Him.
If either experience sounds familiar, you aren’t strange and you aren’t beyond repair. Many people say Allah’s name every day while carrying distance from Him. Some of that distance was handed down by people who carried their own fear and confusion.
## He was your Lord before you were born
This still astonishes me: long before we had a name to answer to, we already knew Him.
Allah tells us that He gathered every human soul and asked one question. Every one of us testified that He was our Lord. We don’t remember standing there, yet something of that recognition remains within us. The scholars call it the fitrah, the original disposition Allah placed inside every human being.
“And ˹remember˺ when your Lord brought forth from the loins (lineage) of the children of Adam their descendants and had them testify regarding themselves. ˹Allah asked,˺ ‘Am I not your Lord?’ They replied, ‘Yes, You are! We testify.’ ˹He cautioned,˺ ‘Now you have no right to say on Judgment Day, “We were not aware of this.”’” (Surah Al-A’raf, aya 172)
I thought about this once while sitting with my niece as the sky outside was turning orange. She looked up and asked, with the seriousness children bring to sudden questions, “Who made the sky?” Before I’d finished answering, she’d moved to something heavier: “When people die, where do they go?” Adults can spend years circling questions like those, or keeping them buried because saying them aloud feels awkward. She just asked.
That doesn’t mean a child already understands everything about Allah. It means the questions are there before the arguments. A child who receives a gift looks for the person who gave it, then says thank you, and when they’re frightened they call for someone stronger than them. A person is built to depend and to give thanks, and worship gives that instinct its right direction: Allah is the One from whom every gift comes, the One we depend on even when we forget we’re depending on Him.
Life settles over that instinct, though. A child may be taught to associate Allah only with being told off, or watch people use His name while treating each other badly, and wounds like that can pile onto the fitrah until it’s hard to recognise. Yet a thing can be covered without being destroyed. The recognition can become buried without being erased.
Sometimes the fitrah surfaces years later. Someone may hear the Qur’an in a language they don’t understand and still feel strangely addressed. The feeling resembles recognition more than discovery, the ache of half-remembering Someone you’d forgotten you knew.
## Where is Allah, and is He far from me?
With that aside, I want to stay with the kind of questions a child asks before they learn to be embarrassed by them. I still remember asking some version of it myself, not in a classroom, just in the middle of an ordinary day, suddenly hit by the thought: where is Allah?
Mu’awiyah ibn al-Hakam as-Sulami, may Allah be pleased with him, was one of the Prophet’s companions. He had an enslaved girl who cared for his sheep. After he wronged her in anger, he came to the Prophet, regretting it and asking whether he could free her. The Prophet asked her, “Where is Allah?” She said, “In the heaven.” He asked, “Who am I?” She said, “You are the Messenger of Allah.” He said, “Free her, for she is a believer.” (Sahih Muslim)
Her name wasn’t recorded. But she’s the one who answered correctly, the one the Prophet called a believer, and she knew where Allah was from inside a life that hadn’t given her much.
Allah is above the seven heavens, above His Throne, in a manner fitting His majesty. He is separate from what He created and nothing in creation contains Him. We don’t imagine *how*. We stay with the words He gave us:
“The Most Compassionate, ˹Who is˺ established on the Throne.” (Surah Ta-Ha, aya 5)
Being above creation doesn’t make Him unaware of one person inside it. Allah is with His servants through His knowledge, hearing, sight and care. Nothing you do slips outside His knowledge, no voice becomes too small for Him to hear and no distance blocks His sight. His being with you doesn’t mean He’s physically inside the room or mixed with creation. He remains above His Throne while knowing you completely, wherever you happen to be.
“And He is with you wherever you are. For Allah is All-Seeing of what you do.” (Surah Al-Hadid, aya 4)
The people asleep down the hall have no idea what’s keeping you awake; Allah’s knowledge has never lost sight of you, and His hearing has never needed you to raise your voice.
Think about what happens when people are desperate. Hands rise without being coached. Eyes turn upward. Some people who’ve spent years believing there’s no God still find themselves calling out to Him when something terrible happens and there’s nowhere else to turn. That doesn’t prove anything by itself, but it looks like the fitrah cracking through the surface: dependence has always had an object, and the Lord above the Throne has never been too high to hear what comes from the lowest place in us.
## What is He doing right now?
Some people picture God as a watchmaker who built the universe, wound it up and stepped away. That leaves them with a distant owner who no longer touches the details of the life He began. Your Lord describes Himself differently.
“All those in the heavens and the earth are dependent on Him. Day in and day out He has something to bring about.” (Surah Ar-Rahman, aya 29)
As you read this, He’s bringing about matters throughout creation. He answers a prayer on the far side of the world and sends rain toward fields you and I will never see. Birds still find their way back to nests, and creatures move through the darkest parts of the sea, fed and held together. Somewhere a child takes a first breath. In that same second, somewhere else, a servant takes the last one. None of it wears Him down, and nobody waits for Him to wake before asking.
This makes the ordinary day look different. The morning didn’t simply arrive because mornings do, and your heart didn’t keep beating through the night because you supervised it. The kettle boils, your lungs keep filling on their own, your heart keeps its rhythm; the same acts repeat so often that the repetition itself is what hides them from view.
Everyone who loves you has to sleep eventually. A mother with a newborn may fight it for hours, walking the room and listening for every change in the child’s breathing, then her body gives in and her eyelids close. Her love remains. Exhaustion simply belongs to being human, and for a few minutes she may miss the first small sound the baby makes. Every form of created care reaches that limit.
In your whole life there’s never been a night when the One holding creation together nodded off or looked away. Sleep has never touched Him. He doesn’t become occupied with one servant and overlook another. You’ve never had to wait for His attention to return, because it never left.
“Neither drowsiness nor sleep overtakes Him.” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 255)
Musa, peace be upon him, introduced Allah to Pharaoh through this active care. Pharaoh asked, “Who then is the Lord of you two, O Moses?” Musa could have answered in a hundred ways. He chose one sentence that holds the whole world inside it: Allah gave every creature its form, then guided it.
Think of a bee finding its hive miles from where it began, or a newborn who has been alive for minutes and somehow turns toward its mother’s milk. The One who gave them their form didn’t abandon them afterward. He guided what He’d made.
“He replied, ‘Our Lord is the One Who has given everything its ˹distinctive˺ form, then guided ˹it˺.’” (Surah Ta-Ha, aya 50)
## Can I picture Him, and will I ever see Him?
Have you tried to picture Allah?
There’s no need to feel guilty if you have. The mind makes pictures from the physical world it knows, so it reaches for a vast light or an endless sky. Children say the question aloud. Adults often keep drawing somewhere in the back of the mind.
Whatever image appears, let it go. Allah created light and sky, so He can’t be a larger version of either. Anything your mind can fully picture belongs to the created world. Our minds were never built to contain Him. A cup was never meant to swallow the ocean.
That limit, in and of itself, is a mercy. If faith depended on forming an image of Allah, we’d all end up worshipping a picture assembled from created things. One mind would imagine light, another a face, another the largest body it could conceive. Allah freed us from that confusion with a single boundary: we know Him through what He revealed about His Names, attributes and actions, while the question of *how* remains with Him.
“There is nothing like Him.” (Surah Ash-Shura, aya 11)
Will we ever see Him? Our eyes can’t take Him in during this life. Musa, peace be upon him, asked for that sight and the mountain crumbled when Allah showed it a trace of His glory. The limit belongs to us, to eyes made for this world and bodies that tire after a single day.
Something greater waits in the next. Paradise contains every beauty a human being has tried to imagine, with none of the loss that follows beauty here. Yet its highest gift will rise above the gardens and rivers: looking upon the face of the One we worshipped without ever laying eyes on Him. A believer spends a whole life learning Names, making dua into empty air and lowering the forehead to the ground, then finally sees the One to whom every prayer was travelling.
“On that Day ˹some˺ faces will be bright, looking at their Lord.” (Surah Al-Qiyamah, ayas 22 and 23)
Suhayb ar-Rumi, may Allah be pleased with him, was one of the Prophet’s companions, a man who had endured slavery and torture for his faith before Allah gave him a home among the believers. It’s fitting that he’s the one who passed on this description of the greatest reward waiting on the other side of every sacrifice. He reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “When the people of Paradise enter Paradise, Allah will say, ‘Do you want anything more?’ They will say, ‘Have You not brightened our faces? Have You not admitted us to Paradise and saved us from the Fire?’ Then Allah will lift the veil, and they will be given nothing more beloved to them than looking at their Lord.” (Sahih Muslim)
## He gave you His Names so you could know Him
If you can’t picture Him and can’t see Him yet, how do you build closeness with Him?
I grew up watching one link of that chain on my uncle Ammi Abdelghani’s orange farm in Boughriba, outside Berkane. He did everything a farmer can do, and then he waited, because the part that turns a blossom into an orange was never in his hands. It took me years to work out who’d been feeding us the whole time.
Allah gave us His Names. Each one opens a way of knowing how He deals with creation. Stand outside a great house at night and you catch light through different windows. The windows don’t contain the whole house, though each one lets you see something true:
Ar-Rahman, the Most Merciful, is a mercy so wide that the breath of someone who denies Him still rises and falls by it, unasked for. Every meal you’ve eaten arrived through Ar-Razzaq, the Provider, a chain of soil, rain, hands and roads He allowed to hold together long enough to reach you.
He is Al-'Alim, the All-Knowing: He knows what worries you today, what you’ve misunderstood about it and where the matter ends. And His love is active and given, never stored away. This is Al-Wadud, the Most Loving. These Names aren’t labels added to a distant being. They tell you who’s been dealing with you all along.
You don’t have to memorise all ninety-nine before any of this counts. Start with one Name, learn what it means, then watch for how Allah deals with you through it, without shrinking Him down to the gifts you can see.
I remember *Ya Razzaq* landing on me properly for the first time in Belgium. It was one of those weeks where you open your banking app, do the math twice, and still don’t see how the month is supposed to close. Rent was coming, summer job applications came back negative, and my head kept running ahead to the next three months like they were already a locked door. I started saying the Name on the walk to the tram, under my breath. Nothing dramatic happened. No surprise transfer, no miraculous envelope.
Then, months later, Allah opened it in a way I couldn’t have scheduled. I got a summer internship in Brussels that paid well, alhamdulillah. Not because I unlocked a trick, but because provision has a timing and a route that belongs to Him, subhanahu wa ta’ala.
What changed was how I read the waiting. I stopped treating the gap as a sign that nothing was coming. I remembered that provision isn’t only cash in hand, it’s doors Allah opens, timing He controls, people He moves, and strength He puts in you to keep going until the door opens.
So be practical with it. When the future feels tight, say *Ya Razzaq*, and then do the next right thing in front of you, send the email, apply for the job, cut what you can, ask for help. When you need mercy, say *Ya Rahman*. When you don’t know what Allah is doing with your life, say *Ya ‘Alim*. When your heart feels unwanted, say *Ya Wadud*. There’s no magic in the sound. You’re calling your Lord by the Names He taught you, and that changes the way you walk through the same day.
“Allah has the Most Beautiful Names, so call upon Him by them.” (Surah Al-A’raf, aya 180)
## Does He see me?
The world meets versions of us. Family sees one part, friends another. We learn which expression belongs in which room, which part of the story can be told and which one would change how people look at us. Even the person who loves you most has to guess what sits behind your face. Allah sees the whole person at once, the worry you carried all day and the small hope you felt embarrassed to admit inside your own head.
There’s relief in being known without having to perform the explanation. You don’t need to organise the mess before bringing it to Him. He knew the sequence before you did, knew which sentence wounded you and why it stayed, knew the intention beneath the action that everybody else read wrongly. Nothing needs translating for the One who created the heart in which it happened.
“Allah ˹even˺ knows the sly glances of the eyes and whatever the hearts conceal.” (Surah Ghafir, aya 19)
For someone raised on fear, being watched can sound like a camera fixed to the ceiling, waiting for a mistake. His sight isn’t that. He knows you on the days when you can’t work out why you feel low, or why you returned to something you promised to leave. The One who created you knows the person He created.
“How could He not know His Own creation? For He ˹alone˺ is the Most Subtle, All-Aware.” (Surah Al-Mulk, aya 14)
The good you did that nobody saw didn’t dissolve into nothing. You let an insult pass when the perfect reply was ready, and biting your tongue cost more than the room could know. You gave money you could barely spare and made sure nobody knew where it came from. Perhaps you got out of bed for Fajr, the dawn prayer, while everyone slept, prayed badly by your own measure and returned to bed feeling nothing at all. There was no applause and no thank-you. He saw them, and no good deed disappears with Him.
The reverse is true too. He sees the part you hide. That should make us careful, though His sight isn’t there to drive a sincere servant into despair. The Lord who knows the sin also knows the fight before it, the regret after it and the small movement of the heart that wants to come home. Nothing is hidden, including the desire to become better.
Somewhere in a forest nobody will enter, a leaf loosens and falls to a patch of soil no human eye will see. Allah knew it before it moved. Billions of lives don’t turn you into a number inside it.
“Not even a leaf falls without His knowledge.” (Surah Al-An’am, aya 59)
## Does He hear me?
Lips barely moving in the dark still reach Him. The companions once asked whether their Lord was near enough to call softly or far enough that they should raise their voices. Throughout the Qur’an, questions often come with the instruction, “Say.” Here, when the question concerns Him and the servant’s dua, Allah answers directly: “I am near.”
“When My servants ask you ˹O Prophet˺ about Me: I am truly near. I respond to one’s prayer when they call upon Me.” (Surah Al-Baqarah, aya 186)
You don’t need flawless Arabic before you speak to Him, and the mosque isn’t the only place He hears you. The edge of your bed after midnight, or the walk home after saying something you regret, neither is too ordinary for dua. There are duas taught by the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, and learning them is a gift, though your Lord already understands the language in which your grief first formed. Some prayers arrive as complete sentences. Others arrive as a name repeated because the person has nothing else left. Sometimes all you carry is a need pressing against the chest and a few words that feel too small for it. Verily, Allah knows what you were trying to say.
## Is Allah mainly angry with me, or does He love me?
Most of us inherited the same warning before we inherited anything else about Him: behave, or Allah will punish you; pray, or you’ll answer for it on the Day of Judgment. I heard some version of that so often growing up that it became the shape my first picture of Allah took, long before anyone mentioned mercy. So here’s the question underneath all of it: is Allah mainly angry with you, keeping score and waiting for you to fail, or does He love you?
Abu Hurayrah, may Allah be pleased with him, spent more of his years close to the Prophet than almost anyone else who walked beside him, gathering up his words the way you’d gather something you knew you’d need later. It’s through him that this reached us: he reported that the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said: “Allah has one hundred parts of mercy, of which He has sent down one on the earth, and due to this His creation shows mercy to each other, and He has kept back ninety-nine parts for Himself, which He will use for His servants on the Day of Resurrection.” (Sahih al-Bukhari)
Every small kindness you’ve ever watched pass between two people, a mother lifting her child before it even cries, a stranger letting something go they had every right to hold onto, runs on that single part. Ninety-nine are still sitting with Him, waiting for the Day you’ll need them.
In another hadith reported by Abu Hurayrah, the Prophet, blessings and peace be upon him, said that Allah said: “My mercy precedes My wrath.”
So when you picture Allah keeping score, start from that ratio instead, ninety-nine parts of mercy against one, and let it change what you expect to hear when you finally turn back to Him:
“Say, ‘O My servants who have exceeded the limits against themselves, do not despair of Allah’s mercy, for Allah certainly forgives all sins. He is indeed the All-Forgiving, Most Merciful.’” (Surah Az-Zumar, aya 53)
That’s not a line reserved for someone who’s already clean. It’s addressed to the one who exceeded the limits, carrying the kind of sin that makes coming back feel impossible. Al-Wadud, the Most Loving, doesn’t wait for you to earn your way back. He put that mercy there before you ever needed it.
Whatever hand hurt you growing up, a parent’s temper, a teacher’s threats, a relative who used Allah’s name to control you, or a community that made obedience the price of love, that person isn’t who you’re talking to when you pray. Allah isn’t the person who hurt you.
## Where does this leave your prayer?
None of this is asking you to feel a certain way before you pray again. It’s not a mood you need to manufacture or a rush of love you need to fake until it becomes true. Show up to the next prayer exactly as you are: distracted, maybe frustrated at how long you stayed away. The first prayer back might feel stiff, like reintroducing yourself to someone you used to know well. Come anyway.
Allah didn’t wait for you to feel ready before He kept those ninety-nine parts of mercy in reserve. He isn’t waiting for you to feel ready now. Stand, say what you can, and let the standing be the return. The feeling tends to follow the showing up, not the other way around.
## Do you know Him now?
Go back to the question this chapter opened with: do you know Him?
Knowing Him isn’t only the facts you could recite by now. It’s the sense that Someone specific has been listening the whole time. I felt that difference once, standing in the Prophet’s mosque in Madinah after a prayer, when a Moroccan friend who’d lived in Spain his whole life turned to me and asked, in Arabic, if I’d finished my worship. He didn’t ask if I’d finished praying. He asked about my worship, and that one word rearranged years for me. I’d said that prayer thousands of times on autopilot, my body moving through the motions while my mind travelled through half the day, and standing there I understood what had been happening all along: I’d been worshipping Allah, speaking to Someone who’d been listening the whole time.
And when I think about who I’d want to hand that understanding to, I think of my niece, the one who looked up at a sky turning orange and asked me who made it. Her questions will grow up with her. One day she won’t ask who made the sky anymore. She’ll ask who Allah is, and she’ll ask it the way we all eventually do, carrying whatever the years have piled on top of the question.
Most of us were handed the good ol’ list, dos and don’ts standing in for an introduction, and some of us weren’t handed anything at all. You’ve read this far, so you’re holding something better. You can tell her about the One who doesn’t sleep, who hears her before she raises her voice, who wrote mercy over Himself before He created her, and who’s been closer to her than anyone in the room since the day she was born.
That’s the introduction most of us never got. And the children who’ll one day tug at your sleeve and ask their own version of who made the sky can get what we never did: an answer from someone who knows Him.
